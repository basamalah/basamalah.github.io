18. How will the experimental probability approach theoretical probability?​


Experimental probability is based on the actual outcomes of a particular experiment or event, while theoretical probability is calculated using mathematical formulas and probability theory.

As the number of trials in an experiment increases, the experimental probability tends to approach the theoretical probability. This is because the law of large numbers states that as the number of trials increases, the experimental probability will become more and more representative of the theoretical probability.

For example, if you flip a fair coin 10 times, the experimental probability of getting heads may be different from the theoretical probability of 0.5. However, if you flip the coin 1,000 times, the experimental probability is likely to be very close to 0.5, which is the theoretical probability.

Therefore, the more trials that are conducted in an experiment, the closer the experimental probability will approach the theoretical probability.
