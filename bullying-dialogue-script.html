9. ano ang dialogue script​


Answer:

The dialogue in a screenplay may serve as a way to express a character's point-of-view or to understand the interpersonal dynamics between two or more characters. Dialogue is also a way to convey the story's mood or tone. In a television comedy, much of the humor will come from your dialogue.

Answer:

Dialogue should convey information and move the story forward, while at the same time help us understand the characters. Spice it up with slang and colloquialisms appropriate to their background. The relative sophistication of a character’s vocabulary, along with the phrasing and cadences of their speech, can provide clues to their education, upbringing and attitudes.

One mark of a mediocre script is that the characters confide what's on their minds or in their hearts. In the vernacular of the trade, this is called being “on the nose.” The subtext, or what a character leaves unsaid, is often more significant than what he or she says. If your characters keep their true thoughts and feelings hidden, your story will have greater tension and emotional resonance.

14. suggest short film script about bullying ​


Answer:

Title: Alone

Opening shot of a school playground. The camera focuses on a group of kids playing basketball. One of the kids, JASON (12), is watching from a distance. He looks lonely and sad. Suddenly, a group of boys approach him.

BULLY 1: Hey, what are you doing here? You're not cool enough to play with us.

JASON: (nervously) I wasn't trying to play. I was just watching.

BULLY 2: (pushing Jason) Well, you shouldn't be here. You're a loser.

The bullies continue to taunt and push Jason until he runs away. The camera follows him as he runs to the bathroom and locks himself in a stall. He's crying.

Flashback to Jason at home. He's sitting on his bed, looking at a picture of himself and his old best friend. He remembers the good times they had together.

Back to the present. Jason is still crying in the bathroom stall. Suddenly, he hears a knock on the door. It's a boy named TYLER (12).

TYLER: Hey, are you okay in there?

JASON: (hesitantly) Y-yeah, I'm fine.

TYLER: (suspicious) Are you sure? You don't sound fine.

JASON: (sighs) No, I'm not okay. Those guys were bullying me.

TYLER: (sympathetically) I know how you feel. They used to bully me too.

Tyler talks to Jason and tells him about how he stood up to the bullies and made new friends. He convinces Jason to come out of the stall and face the bullies.

The camera cuts to Jason and Tyler walking out of the bathroom. The bullies are waiting for them.

BULLY 1: (sarcastically) Oh, look who's back. The crybaby and his new friend.

TYLER: (firmly) Leave him alone. He hasn't done anything to you.

The bullies try to start a fight, but Tyler stands up to them and scares them away. Jason is amazed at Tyler's bravery.

JASON: (grateful) Thank you for standing up for me.

TYLER: (smiling) No problem. We're friends now, right?

Jason smiles and nods. The camera follows Jason as he walks away, feeling happy and confident for the first time in a long time.

Closing shot of the school playground. The camera pans to the basketball court. Tyler and Jason are playing basketball together, and the other kids are cheering them on.
