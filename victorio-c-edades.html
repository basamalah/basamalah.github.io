23. VICTORIO EDADES Major artworks: Poinsettia Girl analysis ​


Victorio Edades was a prominent Filipino artist and considered as the "Father of Philippine Modern Art." One of his significant works is the "Poinsettia Girl."

The painting depicts a young girl holding a bouquet of poinsettias against a stark, geometric background. The girl's delicate features are rendered with a meticulous attention to detail, from the intricate patterns of her clothing to the subtle shading of her skin. Her expression is serene, almost otherworldly, lending the painting an ethereal quality.

One of the striking features of the painting is its use of color. The poinsettias, with their vibrant red hue, provide a stark contrast to the cool blue-green background, creating a visual tension that draws the viewer's eye. The contrast between warm and cool colors also creates a sense of depth and dimensionality, giving the painting a sense of space and atmosphere.

In terms of composition, the painting is characterized by its strong geometric shapes and lines. The background is dominated by a series of intersecting rectangles and triangles, which give the painting a sense of structure and order. The girl, meanwhile, is positioned off-center, creating a sense of movement and dynamism within the composition.

Overall, the "Poinsettia Girl" is a masterful example of Edades' skill as a painter and his ability to capture the essence of his subjects with great sensitivity and attention to detail. It is a testament to his influence on Philippine modern art and continues to be celebrated as a significant work in the canon of Filipino art history.

25. Defination of Victorio Edades a famous artwork​


“The Builders” (1928) is one of Victorio Edades' major work that he exhibited in 1928 during his one-man show. Edades' executed this upon his return to the Philippines from studies in the United States. In his campaign for modernism, this is one of his paintings that shows his enhanced knowledge and skill in modern art.

The nude builders and blocks against a dark background unearthed an interesting note towards progress, that in spite of having peers around, you are on your own in your work. Unlike Bayanihan scenes depicted by other painters, where everyone was familiar with each other and recognized each other’s importance in the tradition, The Builders exemplified the dark side of progress and industrialization that would be built on the backs of anonymous men who will never receive gratification, much less see or enter the palaces they build. This was a stark realization which Edades noticed and therefore painted. His brush strokes on the outlines of the elements are heavy and large, putting more pain and burden onto the canvas. The seemingly rough texture, when seen in detail, almost looks like wounds about to burst. It was a macabre dance of production. It’s terrible to look at, but one cannot just turn away and ignore the irony.

This artwork emphasized linear and structural composition above other pictorial elements, conveying the essence of men engaged in labor through the contortion of the bodies.
