3. A mixture of 0.20M acetic acid and 0.30M sodium acetate is given. Calculate the pH of the medium if the pKa of the acetic acid is 4.76.


Answer:

The mixture of acetic acid and sodium acetate is a buffer solution that resists changes to pH. To calculate the pH of the buffer solution, we can use the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:

pH = pKa + log([A^-]/[HA])

where pKa is the dissociation constant of acetic acid, [A^-] is the concentration of acetate ion, and [HA] is the concentration of acetic acid.

In this case, the pKa of acetic acid is known to be 4.76, the concentration of acetic acid is 0.20 M and the concentration of sodium acetate is 0.30 M.

Acetic acid dissociates according to the following reaction:

CH3COOH + H2O ⇌ CH3COO^- + H3O^+

The equilibrium constant for this reaction is given by:

Ka = [CH3COO^-][H3O^+]/[CH3COOH]

Taking the negative logarithms of both sides, we can obtain:

-pKa = log(Ka) = log([CH3COO^-][H3O^+]/[CH3COOH])

Rearranging and simplifying the equation, we get:

pH = pKa + log([CH3COO^-]/[CH3COOH])

Substituting the given concentrations of acetic acid and sodium acetate, we have:

[CH3COOH] = 0.20 M

[CH3COO^-] = 0.30 M

So,

pH = 4.76 + log(0.30/0.20)

pH = 4.76 + log(1.5)

pH = 4.76 + 0.176

pH = 4.94

Therefore, the pH of the buffer solution is 4.94.

21. APPLYDirection: Calculate the given problem.A mixture of 0.20 M acetic acid and 0.30 M sodium acetate is givenCalculate the pH of the medium if the PKA of the acetic acid is 4.76.DO​


Answer:

Solution

Since both the acid form and base form of HA are present, this is a class 3 problem. The easiest way to solve these problems is to treat them formally as a class 1 problem in which the initial concentration of A- is no longer "0".

HA H+ + A- Ka = 10-5 M

Initial conditions: 0.02 M ~0 0.01 M

Change Due to Dissociation -X +X +X

Net Concentration at Equilibrium 0.02-X X 0.01 + X

Ka = = = 10-5

It is usually possible to ignore X in both the denominator and in the [A-] term in the numerator. Thus,

= 10-5

X = 10-5(0.02/0.01) = 2 x10-5 = [H+]

pH = -log(2 x10-5) = 4.7

These problems reduce to a very simple form since the value of X depends on Ka and the initial ratio of A-/HA. Thus, unlike the other two classes of problems, the value of X does not depend on the actual concentrations of A- and HA (provided both A- and HA are large enough that the X can be ignored).

Since this class of problem is the one most frequently encountered and since pH rather than [H+] is usually being calculated, a simplified method for solving these problems has been devised.

Ka =

log(Ka) = log

= log[H+] + log

-log(Ka) = -log[H+] - log

pKa = pH - log

Rearranging:

pH = pKa + log

This is the Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation.

Sample Problem 1

a) A solution was prepared by dissolving 0.02 moles of acetic acid (HOAc; pKa = 4.8) in water to give 1 liter of solution. What is the pH?

b) To this solution was then added 0.008 moles of concentrated sodium hydroxide (NaOH). What is the new pH? (In this problem, you may ignore changes in volume due to the addition of NaOH).

c) An additional 0.012 moles of NaOH is then added. What is the pH?

Solution

a) This is a class 1 problem:

HOAc H+ + OAc- pKa = 4.8 Ka = 1.6 x10-5 M

Initial Conc 0.02 M ~0 0

Change -X +X +X

Net 0.02-X X

Ka = = = 1.6 x10-5

[HOAc]initial > 100 x Ka, so... X can be ignored in the denominator.

X2/0.02 = 1.6 x10-5 ; X2 = 3.2 x10-7

X = 5.6 x10-4 M = [H+]

pH = -log(5.6 x10-4) = 3.25

b) NaOH is a strong base. It will react nearly quantitatively with HOAc to produce OAc-.

HOAc + OH- OAc- + H2O

0.008 moles HOAc + 0.008 moles OH- 0.008 moles OAc-

Amount of HOAc remaining = 0.02 - 0.008 = 0.012 moles. Thus,

[HOAc] = 0.012 M and [OAc-] = 0.008 M

Since both [HOAc] and [OAc-] are present, this is a class 3 problem.

pH = pKa + log

= 4.8 + log = 4.8 + log(0.67)

= 4.8 - 0.17

pH = 4.63

c) After the addition of another 0.012 moles of NaOH, all of the HOAc has been converted to NaOAc. thus, [OAc-] = 0.02 M. Since only the "base form" of the HOAc is now present, this is a class 2 problem:

OAc- + H2O HOAc + OH- Keq =

Initial Conc 0.02 M [H2O] ~0 ~0

Change -X -X +X +X

Net 0.02-X [H2O] X X

[H+] = 10-14/(3.5 x10-16) = 2.9 x10-9

pH = 8.54

Note: Every problem involving titration and buffers is similar to the sample problem just given. The addition of strong base (or acid) affects the initial concentrations of HA and A-. The pH can then be determined using the appropriate class 1, 2, or 3 solutions. Given the pH, the ratio of A-/HA can be determined from the Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation. The concentrations of A- and HA can be determined if the total concentration (A- + HA) is known, or if the concentration of either species is known.

23. 2. If 0.06M acetic acid exists as ions to the extent of 1.7%, computethe ionization constant for acetic acid.​


The ionization constant for acetic acid = 1.734.10⁻⁵​

Further explanation

The degree of ionization is the amount of ionized substance.

The degree of ionization (symbol α) is the ratio of the amount of ionizing substance to the amount of substance dissolved

α = amount of ionizing substance / amount of substance dissolved

If the substance ionizes completely, the degree of ionization = 1

If no substance ionizes, the degree of ionization = 0

So that the ionization degree is 0 <α <1

Electrolytes can be distinguished from the degree of ionization

Strong electrolytes have a degree of ionization close to or equal to 1

Weak electrolytes have a degree of ionization approaching 0 (small)

According to Arrhenius, acids are substances which, when dissolved in water, release H ions.

An HₓY acid in water will ionize:

HₓY (aq) --------> xH⁺ (aq) + Yˣ- (aq)

Example:

HCl -------> H⁺ + Cl⁻

The amount of H ions produced by 1 acid molecule is called valence acid, whereas acidic residuals are formed after the release of Hions.

Usually, the name acid begins with the word acid followed by the name of the remaining acidic ion

The ion concentration of a weak acid is determined by the value of the acid ionization constant (Ka).

The greater the value of Ka, the greater the dissociated acid produces its H ion and the greater its acidity

Based on the number of protons (H ⁺) released, acids can be divided into:

• 1. Monoprotic acid is an acid which releases one H + ion such as HCl, HCN, HNO₃

• 2. Polyprotic acid is an acid that releases more than one H⁺ ion.

This acid can be divided into two:

• a. Diprotic acid: releases 2 H + ions, such as H₂SO₄, H₂CO₃

• b. Triprotic acid: releases 3 H + ions such as H₃PO₄

In general, the weak acid ionization reaction

HA (aq) ---> H⁺ (aq) + A⁻ (aq)

Ka's value

[tex]\large {\boxed {\bold {Ka \: = \: \dfrac {[H ^ +] [A ^ -]} {[HA]}}}}[/tex]

For example,Phosphate acid includes Triprotic acid and weak acid which will be ionized in 3 stages:

• H₃PO₄ (aq) ⇌H⁺ (aq) + H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) ⇒Ka₁

• H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) ⇌H⁺ (aq) + HPO₄⁻² (aq) ⇒Ka₂

• HPO₄⁻² (aq) ⇌H⁺ (aq) + PO₄⁻³ (aq) ⇒Ka₃

Ka = Ka₁ x Ka₂ x Ka₃

Relationship of Ka, Kb with the degree of ionization of acids and bases

Ka = Ca x α² Kb = Cb x α²

If 0.06M acetic acid exists as an ions to the extent of 1.7%,  it can be said that the degree of ionization of acetic acid is 1.7%

So Ka from acetic acid:

[tex]\rm Ka= 0.06\times (1.7\%)^2\\\\Ka=\boxed{\bold{1.734.10^{-5}}}[/tex]

30. Calculate the pH of a mixture of 1M acetic acid and 0.5M sodium acetate.​


Answer:

pH = 4.44

Explanation:

Use the principle of common ion effect.

NaC2H3O2 → Na^(+) + C2H3O2^(-)

[i]: 0.5 M 0.5 M 0.5 M

Use ICE method.

HC2H3O2 = H^(+) + C2H3O2^(-)

[i]: 1 0 0.5

[c]: - x + x + x

[e]: 1 - x x 0.5 + x

Solve for x.

Ka = [H^(+)][C2H3O2^(-)] / [HC2H3O2]

1.8 × 10^(-5) = (x)(0.5 + x) / (1 - x)

1.8 × 10^(-5) - [1.8 × 10^(-5)]x = 0.5x + x²

x² + 0.500018x - 1.8 × 10^(-5) = 0

Using quadratic formula

a = 1, b = 0.500018, c = -1.8 × 10^(-5)

x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / (2a)

x = [-0.500018 ± √(0.500018² - 4(1)(-1.8 × 10^(-5)))] / [2(1)]

x(+) = 3.6 × 10^(-5) M (accept)

x(-) = -0.50 M (reject)

Determine the hydrogen ion concentration.

[H^(+)] = x = 3.6 × 10^(-5) M

Solve for the pH.

pH = -log [H^(+)]

pH = -log (3.6 × 10^(-5))

pH = 4.44
