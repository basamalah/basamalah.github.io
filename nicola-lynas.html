Nicolas Appert was the French inventor of airtight food preservation. Appert, known as the "father of canning", was a confectioner. Appert described his invention as a way "of conserving all kinds of food substances in containers

THE LIFE OF NICOLAS APPERT

Nicolas Appert, full name Nicolas-François Appert, was a French chef, confectioner, and distiller who created the process of preserving food by sealing it in hermetically sealed containers. He was born about 1749 in Châlons-sur-Marne, France, and died on June 3, 1841 in Massy, near Paris. In 1795, Appert began a 14-year period of experimenting in response to the French Directory's offer of a reward for a method of preserving food for transit. He preserved food like soups, fruits, vegetables, juices, dairy items, marmalades, jellies, and syrups in corked-glass vessels strengthened with wire and sealing wax and maintained in boiling water for varied lengths of time. He used the money to build Massy's first commercial cannery, the House of Appert, which functioned from 1812 to 1933. Appert also invented the bouillon tablet, a nonacid gelatin extraction processing of food, and an autoclave.

Twelve years later, Appert upgraded his technology from glass jars to cylindrical tin-plated steel cans. This innovation improved the mobility of food for both the English and French militaries. Appert was responsible for several inventions, including the bouillon cube, in addition to creating the autoclave. He also invented a process for extracting gelatin from bones that does not require the use of acid.
