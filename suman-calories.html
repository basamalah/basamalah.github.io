8. If the producer has 1000 calories, how much calories will the secondary consumer get? A. 100 calories B. 10 calories C. 1 calorie D. 0.1 calorie


Answer:

B. 10

Explanation:

The percentage of energy transferred from one trophic level to the next varies between 5% and 20% and

is called the ecological efficiency (efficiency of energy transfer). An average figure of 10% is often used.

The ten percent law states that the total energy content of a trophic level in an ecosystem is only about

one-tenth (or 10%) that of the preceding level

EXAMPLE: if the grasses produce 1000 kcal of energy, how many kcal are theoretically available to the frogs?

When energy is passed in an ecosystem from one trophic level to the next, only ten percent (10%) of the energy will be passed on. We can consider a typical food chain in the environment. It can be written as follows. Sun -----> Green Plants -----> Insects -----> Frogs -----> Snakes. The sun provides the energy that is used by the green plants. The green plants are the primary producers for the food chain. The energy from the sun is converted by the plants into food. The insects are the primary consumers. They are the first animals to eat the plants. When they eat the plants and obtain some of the energy (10%) they stored when making food. The rest of the energy is lost and dissipated as heat. Insects are eaten by frogs. The frogs are the secondary consumers in this food chain. They obtain part of the energy stored by the insects. The remaining energy stored by the insects is lost

18. manual operation about suman​


Answer:

Suman is a traditional Filipino rice cake made from glutinous rice cooked in coconut milk and wrapped in banana or coconut leaves. Here is a basic manual on how to make suman:

1. Rinse and soak 1 cup of glutinous rice in water for at least 2 hours or overnight.

2. Drain the soaked rice and transfer it to a pot. Add 2 cups of coconut milk, 1 cup of water, and a pinch of salt. Bring to a boil over medium heat.

3. Reduce the heat to low and simmer for about 20-30 minutes or until the rice is fully cooked and the liquid has been absorbed.

4. Remove the pot from the heat and let the rice mixture cool.

5. Prepare the banana or coconut leaves by wiping them with a damp cloth to remove any dirt or debris. Cut the leaves into squares or rectangles large enough to wrap the rice mixture.

6. Place a small amount of the rice mixture onto the center of each leaf square. Fold the leaf over the rice mixture and secure it with a piece of string or a toothpick.

7. Steam the wrapped suman over boiling water for about 20-30 minutes or until the rice is fully cooked.

8. Serve the suman warm or chilled, as desired. It can be eaten plain or with a variety of dips or sauces. Enjoy!
