2. What is George Count's philosophy of education ​


Answer:

George Sylvester Counts key aspects of his philosophy of education -

Education should be centered around the needs and interests of the student - Counts believed that education should be tailored to the individual needs and interests of the student, rather than focusing on the transmission of a predetermined body of knowledge.

Education should promote social justice: Counts believed that education should be used as a tool for promoting social justice and equality. He argued that education should challenge traditional power structures and work to create a more equitable society.

Education should be experiential: Counts believed that education should be experiential and involve hands-on, real-world learning. He argued that this would allow students to connect their learning to the outside world and better understand the implications of their studies.

Education should be critical and reflective: Counts argued that education should encourage critical thinking and reflection, rather than simply accepting information passively. He believed that this would allow students to develop the skills and knowledge they need to be active and engaged citizens.

4. what are the contributions of the following scientist: andre-marie ampere, alessandro volta, georg simon ohm​


Answer:

a.) Andre-Marie Ampere

    He invented the astatic needle, a critical component of the modern astatic galvanometer, and was the first to demonstrate that a magnetic field is generated when two parallel wires are charged with electricity. He is generally credited as one of the first to discover electromagnetism.

b.) Alessandro Volta

    Alessandro Volta was an Italian physicist whose invention of the electric battery provided the first source of continuous current. His inventions include several versions of batteries and he also invented the electrophorus and discovered methane.

c.) George Simon Ohm

    Georg Simon Ohm had humble roots and struggled financially throughout most of his life, but the German physicist is well known today for his formulation of a law, termed Ohm's law, describing the mathematical relationship between electrical current, resistance, and voltage.
