Novation is the extinguishment of an existing obligation by the substitution or replacement of a new obligation, which is either a new debtor or a new creditor, or a new object of the obligation.

In the given scenario, X and Y entered into a contract, and Z executed a surety undertaking to Y as the debtor. Later, X and Y entered into an agreement changing the interest rate. The change in the interest rate does not necessarily extinguish the original obligation of Y to X. Thus, it does not constitute novation that would release Z from its surety obligation.

A surety's obligation is independent of the principal obligation, and it remains valid even if the principal obligation is modified or changed, unless the surety expressly agrees to the modification or change. Therefore, unless Z, the surety, expressly agreed to the modification of the interest rate, it remains bound by its surety undertaking, and its obligation to Y as the debtor remains in force.

In conclusion, the change in the interest rate between X and Y does not constitute novation, and it does not release Z from its surety obligation to Y as the debtor. Z remains liable to fulfill its surety undertaking to Y unless it is released by Y or X, or unless there is an express agreement between the parties to release Z from its obligation
