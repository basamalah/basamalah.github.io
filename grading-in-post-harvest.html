5. biological basis for post harvest practices?​


Answer:

The biological basis for post-harvest practices is rooted in the understanding of plant physiology and the changes that occur in fruits, vegetables and other crops after they are harvested.

After harvest, the respiration rate of crops increases as they continue to use energy and nutrients stored in the tissue. This can lead to a decline in quality, as well as an increase in the production of heat, carbon dioxide, and ethylene, a plant hormone that promotes ripening and aging.

To slow down these processes, post-harvest practices such as cooling, controlled or modified atmosphere storage, and use of ethylene inhibitors are employed. Cooling helps to slow down respiration, which can reduce the rate of decay and maintain the quality of the produce. Controlled or modified atmosphere storage involves regulating the levels of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the storage environment to slow down respiration and ethylene production. Ethylene inhibitors can be used to prevent or delay the ripening process and extend the shelf-life of the produce.

In addition, post-harvest treatments such as washing, sanitizing, and packaging are used to maintain the safety and hygiene of the produce, and to prevent the growth of harmful microorganisms that can cause spoilage.

The biological basis for post-harvest practices is aimed at reducing the rate of physiological and biochemical changes that occur in crops after harvest, and maintaining the quality, safety, and freshness of the produce for a longer period of time.


6. biological basis for post harvest practices?​


“Biological basis for post harvest practices”

The Biological Basis for Post Harvest Practices is an important consideration for growers in an increasingly competitive agricultural market. By understanding the physiological changes and biology behind the post-harvest process, growers can ensure their produce remains fresh, of high-quality and reaches the customer efficiently.

One of the primary challenges of post-harvest management is maintaining the freshness of the produce. As soon as the crop is harvested, there are various biological processes that occur, such as the respiration of the plant cells breaking down, leading to the production of heat, ethylene and other byproducts. The rate of respiration is how growers measure the rate of ripening and, in turn, their quality control. As the metabolism of the fruit/vegetables slows, the ripening process is prolonged. Therefore, it is recommended that these products are stored at a lower temperature, with proper air exchange, humidity and circulation when possible.

In addition to the changing physical properties of the produce, post harvest practices also in involve inhibitors to help slow the affects of spoilage organisms. Post harvest application of chemical inhibitors, such as calcium chloride, helps to slow the growth of bacteria, wild yeasts and putrefactive organisms that may cause the products to spoil.

In order to ensure proper starch conversion and maintain quality, proper packing is important for any produce that is to be stored or shipped for a long period of time. Proper packing will reduce respiration of the produce, and in some cases, regulate ethylene levels, which can cause accelerated ripening.

Finally, certain post harvesting processes are done in order to present the food aesthetically, such as removing stems and leaves from fruits, removing the skin from certain fruits, and cleaning the products with water or an antibacterial agent to reduce the number of microbial contaminants.

To conclude, by understanding the biology behind the post harvest process, growers are able to ensure their product remains of the highest quality and reaches their customers in the best possible condition. Chemical inhibitors, proper packaging and temperature control are just a few of the various post-harvesting practices that play a role in determining the quality of produce.

Preharvest and Postharvest Factors Affecting the Quality and Shelf Life of Harvested Tomatoes: A Mini Review

Isaac Kojo Arah,1 Harrison Amaglo,1 Ernest Kodzo Kumah,2 and Hayford Ofori1

Show more

Academic Editor: Allen Barker

Published15 Dec 2015

Abstract

Tomato production can serve as a source of income for most rural and periurban producers in most developing countries of the world. However, postharvest losses make its production unprofitable in these parts of the world. Postharvest losses in tomatoes can be as high as 42% globally. Postharvest losses in tomatoes can be either quantitative or qualitative. Even though emphasis in crop research nowadays is increasing shifting from quantity to quality of produce, there is still little improvement in the quality of commercially produced tomato varieties, hence resulting in high quality losses. From the study it was discovered that the postharvest quality status of tomatoes partly depended on some preharvest practices carried out during production. Some of these factors are fertiliser application, pruning, maturity stage, cultivar selection, and irrigation. Using best postharvest handling practices or factors such as temperature, relative humidity, gases in storage, postharvest calcium chloride application, and physical handling procedures to maintain the quality after harvest was also critical. It was concluded by this study that understanding and managing both preharvest and postharvest factors properly will reduce the postharvest quality losses in tomatoes.
