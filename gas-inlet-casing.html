5. A Venturi tube may be used as the inlet to anautomobile carburetor. If the inlet pipe of 2.0 cmdiameter narrows to 1.0 cm diameter, what is thepressure drop in the constricted section forairflow of 3.5 m/s in the 2-сm section? (Assumeair density is 1.25 kg/m².)​


Answer:

ΔP = 114.84 Pa

Explanation:

Given:

d₁ = 2 cm = 0.02 m

d₂ = 1 cm = 0.01 m

v₁ = 3.5 m/s

ρ = 1.25 kg/m³

First, let's solve for the velocity of the 1-cm tube

Using the equation of continuity:

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂

[(π/4)(0.02)²](3.5) = [(π/4)(0.01)²]v₂

v₂ = 14 m/s

To get the change in pressure, we can use Bernoulli's equation

P₁ + (1/2)ρv₁² = P₂ + (1/2)ρv₂²

P₁ - P₂ = (1/2)ρv₂² - (1/2)ρv₁²

ΔP = (1/2)ρ(v₂² - v₁²)

ΔP = (1/2)(1.25)[(14)² - (3.5)²]

ΔP = 114.84 Pa <== answer

24. The Hillsboro Inlet Lighthouse lights up how much more area than the Jupiter Inlet Lighthouse? Round your answer to the nearest tenth. Jupiter Inlet Lighthouse- 18 miles. Hillsboro Inlet Lighthouse- 28.


Answer:

The Hillsboro Inlet lighthouse lights up 1444.4 miles² more area than Jupiter Inlet lighthouse.

Step-by-step explanation:

Radius:

Hillsboro Inlet Lighthouse: 28 miles

Jupiter Inlet Lighthouse: 18 miles

The lighthouse lights up an area in circle.

Area of circle = πr²

Where:

π ≈ 3.14

r ⇒ radius

Area each lighthouse covers:

Hillsboro, with 28-mile radius:

Area = (3.14)(28 miles)²

= (3.14)(784 miles²)

≈ 2,461.76  or  2,461.8 miles²  

Jupiter Inlet Lighthouse with 18-mile radius:

Area = (3.14)(18 miles)²

= (3.14)(324 miles²)

≈ 1,017.36 or 1,017.4 miles²

Solve for the area difference to find how much more area the Hillsboro lights up than Jupiter lighthouse:

Area difference = 2,461.8 miles²  - 1,017.4 miles²

Area difference = 1,444.4 miles² (in nearest tenth)

The Hillsboro Inlet lighthouse lights up 1444.4 miles² more area than Jupiter Inlet lighthouse.

How long will it take the smaller inlet pipe to fill the swimming pool alone?Step 1:Step 2:Step 3:Step 4:Step 5:​


Answer:

The answer is 12 hours.

Step-by-step explanation:

Step 1: Assign values.

Let a = time it takes for smaller pipe to fill the pool.

Let b = a-6 = time it takes for bigger pipe to fill the pool.

Let t = time it takes for both pipes to fill the pool; t=4.

Step 2: Create equation.

1/a + 1/(a-6) = 1/4

Step 3: Get GCF and multiplying it both sides of the equation.GCF

GCF is 4[a(a-6)].

Multiplying GCF to equation.

4 * [ a * (a - 6)] * [ 1/a + 1/(a - 6) ] = ( 1/4 ) * 4 [ a * ( a - 6)]

Step 4. Solving For a.

4 * [ a * (a - 6)] * [ 1/a + 1/(a - 6) ] = ( 1/4 ) * 4 [ a * ( a - 6)]

Simplifying 1. Applying distributive property

4a + 4a - 24 = a^2 - 6a

Simplifying 2. Combining like terms

a^2 - 14a -24 = 0

Evaluating quadratic solution. (by factoring or quadratic formula or plug into calculator) factors are:

a^2 - 14a -24 = (a -2) * (a - 12) ;

a = 2 ; a = 12

Step 5. Evaluate the applicable values for a.since the are two possible values for a, we need to determine what value satisfies the equation. We need to choose a value that would not result to a negative value for b.

@ a = 2

b = 2 - 6 ;

b = -4.

@ a = 12

b = 12 - 6 ;

b = 6.

Therefore we should take a = 12 as the answer.

WHAT IS EPIGLOTTIS?

The epiglottis is a cartilaginous flap in the neck that covers the laryngeal inlet upon swallowing. In essence, it covers the laryngeal inlet upon swallowing in order to prevent food from entering the larynx.

The epiglottis is a leaf-shaped flap in the throat that prevents food from entering the windpipe and the lungs. It stands open during breathing, allowing air into the larynx. During swallowing, it closes to prevent aspiration of food into the lungs, forcing the swallowed liquids or food to go along the esophagus toward the stomach instead. It is thus the valve that diverts passage to either the trachea or the esophagus.

The epiglottis is made of elastic cartilage covered with a mucous membrane, attached to the entrance of the larynx. It projects upwards and backwards behind the tongue and the hyoid bone.

The epiglottis sits at the entrance of the larynx. It is shaped like a leaf of purslane and has a free upper part that rests behind the tongue, and a lower stalk (Latin: petiolus).[2] The stalk originates from the back surface of the thyroid cartilage, connected by a thyroepiglottic ligament. At the sides, the stalk is connected to the arytenoid cartilages at the walls of the larynx by folds.The epiglottis originates at the entrance of the larynx, and is attached to the hyoid bone. From there, it projects upwards and backwards behind the tongue.[3] The space between the epiglottis and the tongue is called the vallecula.

FUNCTIONDuring swallowing, the epiglottis bends backwards, folding over the entrance to the trachea, and preventing food from going into it. The folding backwards is a complex movement the causes of which are incompletely understood. It is likely that during swallowing the hyoid bone and the larynx move upwards and forwards, which increases passive pressure from the back of the tongue; because the aryepiglottic muscles contract; because of the passive weight of the food pushing down; and because of contraction of laryngeal and because of contraction of thyroarytenoid muscles. The consequence of this is that during swallowing the bent esophagus blocks off the trachea, preventing food from going into it; food instead travels down the esophagus, which is behind it.

The epiglottis arises from the fourth pharyngeal arch. It can be seen as a distinct structure later than the other cartilage of the pharynx, visible around the fifth month of development.

The position of the epiglottis also changes with ageing. In infants, it touches the soft palate, whereas in adults, its position is lower.

A high-rising epiglottis is a normal anatomical variation, visible during an oral examination, which does not cause any serious problem apart from a mild sensation of a foreign body in the throat. It is seen more often in children than adults and does not need any medical or surgical intervention. The front surface of the epiglottis is occasionally notched. #BrainlyFast
