5. She is the Mother of Philippine Folk Dame because of her invaluable imawork as the first researcher in Philippine folk dancing​


Explanation:

MANILA, Philippines – Filipino dance has always been in spotlight, thanks to various awards and recognitions given to various dance troupes. There are the likes of the Ramon Obusan Dance Troup and the Bayanihan Dance Troupe, both of whom have traveled around the world to promote the country’s various dances.

There are, of course, Filipino groups that have reaped awards from genres like jazz, hip-hop, and ballet – there's Ballet Philippines, Philippine All Stars, and Jabbawockeez.

But before these groups made it locally and worldwide came 5 women who developed and promoted Filipino folk and modern dance.

Here are the names you should remember:

1. Francisca Reyes-Aquino. Reyes-Aquino is considered the mother of Filipino folk dance. Born in Bocaue, Bulacan, Reyes-Aquino graduated with a BS Education degree from the University of the Philippines. She traveled to various parts of the country to record dances from different provinces. (READ: Google marks Francisca Reyes-Aquino's 120th birth anniversary with doodle)

21. Who is considered the mother of philippine folk dance?


Mother of Philippine Folk Dance

Francisca Reyes-Aquino, affectionately known as the "Mother of Philippine Dance," dedicated her life to preserving Filipino culture by learning and instructing ethnic dances. Reyes-Aquino spent months traveling to various rural areas, including the barrios in Central and Northern Luzon, and studying the dances, activities, and even songs that were common there with a small team of researchers. Her thesis, Philippine Folk Dances and Games, which she published after completing her research in 1926 and which greatly assisted teachers and the majority of Filipinos in identifying "unrecorded forms" of regional dances, celebrations, and rites, was a great contribution to the field. She was recruited to lead a research team at the UP to find additional folk dances and teach them as a result. She visited Boston University to learn more about the discipline and returned with a passion for learning indigenous dances from Mindanao, Leyte, and Mountain Province.
