16. why is bicol known as bicol volcanic arc​


The Bicol region, located in the southeastern part of Luzon island in the Philippines, is known as the Bicol Volcanic Arc because it is an active volcanic chain composed of numerous stratovolcanoes and volcanic edifices.

The Bicol Volcanic Arc is part of the Pacific Ring of Fire, an area in the Pacific Ocean where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur due to the convergence of several tectonic plates. The subduction of the Philippine Sea Plate under the Eurasian Plate has resulted in the formation of the volcanic arc, with magma rising from the Earth's mantle and causing volcanic activity.

The Bicol Volcanic Arc consists of several active and inactive volcanoes, including Mayon Volcano, one of the most active and iconic volcanoes in the Philippines. Other notable volcanoes in the area include Bulusan Volcano, Mount Iriga, Mount Masaraga, and Mount Malinao.

The volcanic activity in the Bicol region has contributed to the fertility of its soil and the abundance of natural resources, including geothermal energy. However, it also poses risks to the local communities, as volcanic eruptions can cause damage to infrastructure, crops, and homes, as well as put the lives of people in danger.

Overall, the Bicol Volcanic Arc is a significant natural feature of the Philippines and a source of both opportunities and challenges for the people and communities living in the area.

The Pili Nut

Pili (Canarium ovatum), a fruit-bearing tree endemic to the Philippines especially found in the Bicol Region of Luzon Island, yields a kernel precious to the country’s food sector: the Pili Nut.

The Pili Tree is a low-maintenance crop. It only needs pruning from time to time, requiring minimal fertilizers, or even none at all.

The Pili Fruit, green when young and turns into deep purple or black when ready for harvest, is blanched in hot water for three minutes to easily peel the exocarp and pulp, and expose the hard, bony shell.
