To understand the significance of nudity in art, we need to travel back in time when sculptures were the most prominent art form. Sculptures formed an integral part of society and adorned temples, court houses, palaces, gardens and other public places of importance. Sculptors therefore needed to have high levels of skill and imagination to make the sculptures seem more life-like. Sculptors experimented with the pose, the symmetry, placement and angle of body parts and various other details to bring out a perfect life statue. This meant the viewer needed to perceive emotions from the sculpture, leading artists to use more than just facial expressions to convey emotions. Sculptors began to use the whole expanse of the body for this purpose, which also meant they could not dress up their subjects, as clothing cannot convey the person’s emotions, but portray them nude. The nude figure is a tradition in Western art, and has been used to express ideals of male and female beauty and other human qualities. In the second stanza, the speaker goes further, expressing a desire to be "naked of skin." This line is a metaphor for the speaker's desire to strip away even deeper layers of social conditioning and self-consciousness. In the final stanza, the speaker takes their desire to be nude to its logical conclusion: they want to be "naked of bone." This is the most extreme expression of the speaker's desire to be free and authentic. By shedding even their physical skeleton, the speaker is expressing a desire to be completely unencumbered, to exist in a state of pure being.

Overall, "In my desire to be nude" is a powerful exploration of the human desire for authenticity and freedom. The poem's rich imagery and metaphors invite the reader to consider their own desires for connection and vulnerability, and to contemplate the barriers that stand in the way of achieving these goals.
