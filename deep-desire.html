2. Deep desire to master technology is what causes it to slip out of hand.


TECHNOLOGY

  Technology itself, in Heidegger's view, is neither good nor bad; rather, the issue is that technological thinking, or calculative thinking, has come to dominate all other forms of thought. Heidegger observed that modern technology's primary function is frame, or Ge-stell, which literally translates to mean that everything in nature is "standing-reserve" (Bestand). Reality is "exposed" in a particular way by entering into a certain relation with it. And this is where technology enters the picture, as it is the method of revelation that best represents our period. Technology represents a particular mode of world-revealing in which people exert control over reality.

There are two risks associated with contemporary technology:

A threat to human existence whose ontological relationship to reality is reduced to exploitationA threat to human thought because it loses the ability to consider an alternative ontological relationship to the universe.

When Heidegger claims that "the essence of technology is by no means anything technological," he means that neither the machines themselves nor even the various human activities connected to contemporary modes of production are where technology's driving force lies.

29. A 70 kg man finds himself floating in a deep space, what is the mass of the man in that deep space


The force exerted by a person who weighs 70 kilograms (706 Newtons) on Earth (or 160 lbs). The astronaut's weight, measured in Newtons, is 632 when they are in orbit in deep space (142 lbs) or 64.45 kg. It reduces by almost 10 % in the deep space.

In every scenario, the mass of the astronaut will remain the same. Accordingly, the astronaut has a mass of seventy kilos on Earth, seventy kilograms on the moon, and seventy kilograms on mars. In a similar vein, the mass of an astronaut is multiplied by the gravitational constant of each planet to determine its weight.

Deep space is defined as the region of space that lies much beyond the boundaries of the Earth's atmosphere, specifically the region that extends beyond the earth-moon system.

It is a common misconception that space is devoid of all matter; however, this is not the case. Space is actually a near-perfect vacuum that contains a low density of particles, most of which is a plasma composed of hydrogen and helium, in addition to electromagnetic radiation, magnetic fields, neutrinos, dust, and cosmic rays.
