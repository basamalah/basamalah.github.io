12. How do some athletes even health enthusiasts find it important in strictly following the rules and health standards in doing their daily routines?


“2020 is cancelled”. “The world has gone mad!”. “I can’t believe this is happening!”. Just some of the comments that have been made on social media and overheard in everyday conversations throughout the first six months of this year.

From a sporting point of view, it’s easy to take the stance that 2020 is a year to be forgotten (unless you’re a Liverpool fan). The Olympic and Paralympic Games – the peak of most athletes’ careers – has been postponed; the Premier League disrupted by a long mid-season break; the cricket season massively delayed; rugby unsure if the rest if the season will go ahead… I could go on. COVID-19 has affected sport from a financial standpoint and, closer to home, has affected athletes on a much more personal level too.

But 2020 may be a year where real progress is made. Of course, there won’t be any new Olympic or Paralympic records as many would have been hoping for, but it’s given us all the time to slow down, think, and reflect on what is really important; we’ve been forced into being creative and innovative. I think everyone has now become a pub quiz genius, bake off champion and master at entertaining themselves to relieve the boredom of lockdown.

From an athlete perspective, there have been a lot of challenges to navigate. Team sports have had to work out how to stay connected and maintain the camaraderie that many have missed during lockdown. The majority of athletes haven’t been able to access their usual training facilities or equipment. And of course, there is the ongoing battle of motivating yourself to train hard, often on your own, in the middle of a global pandemic where there are 101 other things to be worrying about.

But what if all these hurdles mean that athletes emerge as more well-rounded people on the other side? I wanted to look back over the past six months and address some of the events that have happened across the world. Then look at how athletes have responded so inspirationally, which has demonstrated that they are more than just athletes – they’re also amazing people.

January seems like a distant memory now, but the year began with the tragic bushfires across Australia. An area of 46 million acres (roughly twice the size of South Korea) was burned, with 3,000 homes being destroyed and 33 people left dead.

Athletes were some of the biggest supporters to help the country recover from the bushfire catastrophe. Tennis player Nick Kyrgios donated $200 dollars for every serve he aced that summer; cricketers were donating money for every six hit or wicket taken; the GB Women’s hockey squad auctioned off signed sticks; and Lewis Hamilton donated $500,000.

Athletes came together to support an incredible cause and helped the bushfire response in their own way, demonstrating kindness and generosity.

March was the beginning of the one of the most difficult periods of time for athletes aspiring to go to the 2020 Games. On March 23, lockdown measures were put in place across Britain, meaning athletes were unable to train with teammates or access their usual facilities. Then the Games themselves were postponed for a year on March 24.

I can only begin to imagine how mentally tough this must have been. But the overwhelming response I saw from athletes was an acknowledgment that this was the right thing to do. Sometimes, as an athlete, it can seem like sport is the most important thing in the world, but here it was clear that everyone’s health was far more important, demonstrating selflessness and maturity.

Despite the bad news March brought, April was the month of developing new ways of training. The whole of the UK seemed to catch the running bug for a while, with NHS 5k Strava times congesting social media.

But for athletes (many of whom were already familiar with running), it was an opportunity to adapt and get creative with training facilities. I’ve seen garages turned into gyms, TRXs hung from trees, garden sheds converted into rowing machine rooms, and country lanes used as running tracks. Some of my favourite new ways of training have involved pets – everything from yoga to HIIT workouts – as they seem to love getting involved! This novelty of training at home demonstrated how committed athletes are to their profession and their creativity in finding new ways to train.

As the days ticked on through May, and the novelty of training at home seemed to wear off, many athletes turned their heads to other activities. Along with the rest of the UK, making sourdough and banana bread became a popular activity, but lots of athletes looked past their sporting careers and started thinking about what they were going to do after sport. (At this point, I imagine the EIS Performance Lifestyle team must have been rejoicing that their message was finally getting through!)

Training is extremely important and should form an integral part of all elite athlete’s daily routines. Training allows the body to gradually build up strength and endurance, improve skill levels and build motivation, ambition and confidence. In mathematics, solving linear equations are commonly used in geometry and algebra. It was applicable and being used in higher mathematics including calculus, physics and other branch of math. It is also applied in functions of set.

What is linear equation? Linear equation is a first degree polynomials, having one or more variables, and constant. It is an equation of a line.

How about function? Function is a binary relation between two sets that associates first set (input) that will produce another element of the second set (output).

Given Question

Describe the set of inputs, the function rule and the set of outputs for each function.

Problem 1

The Amount of battery charge of cellular phone is a function of the number of hour of use.

Answer and Explanation

Input: Battery Charge (x)

Output: Number of hours use (y)

Function Rule

y = kx, where k is constant.

Problem 2

An athlete's shoe size is a function of the athletes length of foot.

Answer and Explanation

Input: shoe size (x)

Output: length of foot (y)

Function Rule

y = kx, where k is constant.

The input is the shoe size, it is directly proportional to the function of the length of athlete's foot.

Problem 3

The amount of water on graduated cylinder displaced by same size marbles is a function of the number of marbles.

Answer and Explanation

Input: number of marbles (x)

Output: amount of water (y)

Function Rule

y = kx, where k is constant.

The input is the number of marbles, it is directly proportional to the function of the amount of water displaced in graduated cylinder.

What is k? k is the constant value in each relation of function. For example, y = 3x, and y = 2x.
