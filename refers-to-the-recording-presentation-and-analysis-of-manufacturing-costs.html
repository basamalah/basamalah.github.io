4. Under variable costing, fixed manufacturing overhead is treated as a product cost.


Fixed Manufacturing Overhead

By excluding fixed industrial overhead from the product-cost of production, variable costing is a managerial and cost accounting concept. Unlike absorption costing, which allocates the fixed production overhead to the items produced, this method does not do this.

FALSE

Under variable costing, fixed overhead is not regarded as a product cost. Fixed manufacturing overhead is still recorded as an item on the income statement, but it is now done so as a cost that is deducted from each period's revenue.

Supporting details:

Only the variable production costs are accounted for when using variable costing. The costs that are inventoried do not include selling and administrative charges because they are not considered to be product costs.

Fixed manufacturing overhead costs are handled as period costs under variable costing, thus they are always expensed during the period in which they are incurred. Regardless of the costing methodology employed, all other costs are regarded equally, hence profit is the same when the quantity of units produced and sold is equal.

Variable costing treats fixed manufacturing overhead as a period cost, whereas absorption costing treats it as a product cost (included in inventories on the balance sheet until sold) (expensed on the income statement as incurred).

The total cost of Job 100 is P6,505.

For defined the calculation, we have to find the Manufacturing overhead allocation rate for each department.

- Machining Department

Overhead allocation rate = Overhead / Machine hours

                                         = P250,000/ 100,000

                                         = P2.50

- Assembly Department

Overhead allocation rate = Overhead / direct labor-hours

                                         = P360,000/ 90,000

                                         = P4.00

To allocate overhead expenses, you must first determine the overhead allocation rate. This is accomplished by dividing total overhead by direct work hours.

Total manufacturing cost P7.05 P9.30 P13.80 P7.80 Estimated cost per unit Management finds the variation in unit costs to be confusing and difficult to work with. It has been suggested that the problem lies with manufacturing erhead, since it is the largest element of cost. Accordingly, you have been asked to find a more suitable way of assigning manufacturing overhead cost to units of product. After some analysis, you have determined changes in the level of production. Required: 1. The company uses a job-order costing system. How would you recommend that manufacturing overhead cost be assigned to production? Be specific, and show computations. 2. Recompute the company's unit costs in accordance with your recommendations in (1) above.


Answer:

1. In a job-order costing system, manufacturing overhead costs are typically assigned to each job or batch of products based on the amount of direct labor hours or machine hours used to complete the job. This is known as using a cost driver to allocate the manufacturing overhead costs.

To assign the manufacturing overhead costs to production using this method, you would first need to determine the total direct labor hours or machine hours used in each quarter. Then, you would calculate the overhead cost per direct labor hour or machine hour by dividing the total manufacturing overhead cost for the quarter by the total direct labor hours or machine hours.

For example, in the first quarter, the total manufacturing overhead cost is P240,000 and the total direct labor hours is 6,000. The overhead cost per direct labor hour would be P240,000 / 6,000 direct labor hours = P40 per direct labor hour.

Then, to assign the manufacturing overhead cost to each unit of production, you would multiply the overhead cost per direct labor hour by the number of direct labor hours used to produce each unit.

For example, if it takes 0.5 direct labor hours to produce each unit in the first quarter, the manufacturing overhead cost assigned to each unit would be P40 per direct labor hour x 0.5 direct labor hours = P20 per unit.

2. To recompute the unit costs using the method described above, you would need to determine the direct labor hours or machine hours used to produce each unit in each quarter. Then, you would multiply the overhead cost per direct labor hour or machine hour by the number of direct labor hours or machine hours used to produce each unit to determine the manufacturing overhead cost assigned to each unit.

For example, in the first quarter, if it takes 0.5 direct labor hours to produce each unit and the overhead cost per direct labor hour is P40, the manufacturing overhead cost assigned to each unit would be P40 per direct labor hour x 0.5 direct labor hours = P20 per unit.

To determine the total unit cost, you would then add the manufacturing overhead cost per unit to the other unit costs (materials, labor, and any other costs) for each quarter.

For example, if the materials cost for a unit in the first quarter is P10 and the labor cost is P5, the total unit cost would be P20 (manufacturing overhead) + P10 (materials) + P5 (labor) = P35 per unit.
