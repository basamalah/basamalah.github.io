6. What is objectives of Bioplastic?​


Answer:

The objectives of bioplastics are to provide a more sustainable alternative to traditional petroleum-based plastics. They are made from renewable plant materials such as corn starch, sugarcane, or cellulose, rather than fossil fuels. This can reduce the amount of greenhouse gases produced during their production and disposal, as well as decrease the dependence on non-renewable resources.

Some of the main objectives of bioplastics include:

Reduce greenhouse gas emissions: Bioplastics can be made from carbon neutral or even carbon negative materials, which means they can reduce the carbon footprint of plastic production.

Reduce waste: Bioplastics are biodegradable and compostable, which means they can break down naturally in the environment and do not contribute to plastic pollution.

Increase the use of renewable resources: Bioplastics are made from plant materials such as corn starch, sugarcane, or cellulose, which are renewable resources that can be replenished.

Cost-effectiveness: Bioplastics are becoming increasingly cost-effective and competitive with traditional plastics, making them a viable alternative for a wide range of applications.

Creating new market opportunities: Bioplastics are opening new market opportunities for farmers and manufacturers, and creating jobs in rural areas

10. 5. How do you compare the flexibility of the bioplastic at room temperature and refrigerator temperature?


Answer:
To compare the flexibility of a bioplastic at room temperature and refrigerator temperature, you would need to conduct experiments to measure the flexibility of the material at each temperature.

One way to measure the flexibility of a material is through a bend test, where a sample of the bioplastic is bent to a specific angle and the amount of force required to reach that angle is measured. The sample can be tested at room temperature and then again after being cooled to refrigerator temperature. The difference in the force required to bend the sample at each temperature can provide an indication of how much more or less flexible the bioplastic is at refrigerator temperature compared to room temperature.

Alternatively, you could use a tensile test, in which the material is pulled until it breaks. You measure the force required to break the material at room temperature and then again after being cooled to refrigerator temperature. The difference in the force required to break the material at each temperature can indicate how much more or less flexible the bioplastic is at refrigerator temperature compared to room temperature

It's worth noting that the flexibility of bioplastics can vary depending on the specific type of bioplastic and the specific conditions under which it is tested. It is also important to note that testing and comparing the flexibility of bioplastics at room temperature and refrigerator temperature can give us an idea of how the material would behave in different environments but it is not the only factor that affects the performance of a bioplastic.

ABSTRACT

Bioplastic is created to overcome plastic contamination while maintaining the characteristics of plastics which are flexible, cost effective and durable. In this study, bioplastic was formulated by using agriculture waste from cassava peel. Three different concentration of glycerol were set up at 20%, 30% and 40% with constant amount of CaCO3 (filler) were used for the optimization. The characterization of the bioplastic was carried out using FTIR analysis. Each sample shows similarities in spectra, indicating similar chemical composition of functional group present - which are hydroxyl group in alcohol and phenol (O-H), aliphatic saturated hydrocarbon chain (C-H) and ester, ether, carboxylic acid as well as anhydride group (C-O). In mechanical tests, the bioplastics are divided into tensile test, water absorption test and soil burial degradation test. Through tensile test, the bioplastic with 20% of glycerol shows the highest strength up to 5.82 ± 1.00 MPa, with Young's modulus at 99.79±42.03 MPa and tensile strain at 10.34±2.43%. Through water absorption test, the highest level of water absorbance was found to be at 40% glycerol addition at 28.64±0.16% meanwhile the lowest level of absorption was at 20% which is 24.49±0.21%. Even though the 40% glycerol shows the highest degradation rate, it was found that the 20% glycerol yielded higher mechanical properties and could maintain the characteristics of plastics better than the 40% glycerol.

Answer:

Abstract. Bioplastics are made wholly or in part from renewable biomass sources such as sugarcane and corn, or from microbe such as yeast. 

Bioplastics are sturdy materials that can be used to replace conventional petroleum-based plastics for sustainable food packaging. Materials derived from renewable resources and biodegradable polymers are considered as bioplastic, for example, PLA, PHA, PBAT, and starch. Biodegradation specification and methods of assessments for plastic materials have been established by several international and national organizations, including the American Society for Testing and Materials, International Standards Organization, International Committee for Standardization. Types of bioplastic materials used for the commercial market and research into food packaging, including renewable resources and petroleum synthesis were reviewed. Processing of bioplastic packaging via conventional conversion includes extrusion, thermoforming, blow molding, and injection molding. These produce different forms of flexible and rigid packaging.
