The art of beautiful handwriting

Calligraphy

the art of beautiful handwriting phrase is derived for "beauty" and "to write" from the Greek word "kallos" (graphein). It implies knowing the correct form of letters – i.e. the conventional signs by which language can be conveyed – and the capacity to make these signs work in such a way that their experienced, knowledgeable eye recognizes such an arrangement of the different parts and proportions. In the ordinary sense of the word, calligraphy, as art, must not be readable.

Calligraphy is considered in the Middle East and in eastern Asia to be a significant art equivalent to sculpture or painting by long and challenging history. The basic Greek and Latin alphabets and the abundance of literature tended in Western society to create an art that anyone would learn. However, a strong disparity between handwriting and more complex types of text and letters emerged with the introduction of printing in Europe at the mid-15th century. Indeed, by the end of the 16th century new words for "calligraphy" were introduced in most European languages and the words calligraphy were written only in 1613 in English. From the 16th century to the present, books from which to write tended to discriminate between regular and more artistic handwriting.  The method of printing was also believed to end the traditions of manuscripts. That doesn't exactly matter, for example, at the time of the printing most of the surviving hour books (lavish private manuscript books). Moreover, in the 19th century some types of books including musical scores, scientific notation and other works of specialty or limited publishing continued to be published manually. Therefore, even though handwritten books cannot be replicated in quantities or with absolute accuracy, the introduction of printed work has endured. Printing and handwriting continued to connect, with the continuing introduction of calligraphy into the new publicity method. In the past, for example, many calligraphers produced printing styles.
