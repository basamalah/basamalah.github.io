3. What do you think is the distinct Filipino accent?


Answer:

The accent a Filipino makes when speaking English could depend on a number of factors:

1.What first language (L1) or mother tongue the Filipino learned at home and how deep his understanding and use of that language is. There are as many as 80 mutually unintelligible Philippine languages and each of them has its own sound combinations, cadence, rhythm, flow, or beat so that it could sound very harsh or clipped, to choppy or flowing, rhythmic or cuddly.

2.What age he learned English, and how long and how often he has been using it.

3.What English dialect or accent he has learned (American, Canadian, UK, Australian, New Zealand, Philippine, etc.). Most Filipinos are used to American spelling and accent but there are some words pronounced in the British manner such as either, agile, and missile.

4.What other languages he learned while he was growing up with his parents' primary language/s, Tagalog, English, and some other language in school like Chinese, Spanish, Arabic, or any other language of a country he stays in.

Basically, educated Filipinos who've had years speaking English all the way to college learn to Americanize their accent and choice of words without getting too much of the lengthy diphthong vowel combinations like the Texans or Southerners do with their short schwa sounds between drawls and twangs that seem to go on forever.

Tagalog has the five basic Spanish vowels (a, e, i, o, u) together with identical timing for the staccato-like syllables of conjugated verbs, nouns, and adjectives. This choppy combination when smoothened with American-style "long-plus-short" blended syllables ("gonna," "sorta," "wudda") produces an educated Filipino accent that has a syllabication rhythm somewhere between the standard American accent with its "downward staircase" intonation and rapid, lilting Mexican Spanish with rising intonation towards the end of sentences.
