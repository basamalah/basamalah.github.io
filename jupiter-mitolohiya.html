Newton's Third Law states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Therefore, when Jupiter collides with a comet, the force that the comet exerts on Jupiter is equal and opposite to the force that Jupiter exerts on the comet.

The force from the comet exerted on Jupiter will affect Jupiter by pushing it away from the direction of the comet's motion. This is because the force on Jupiter is equal and opposite to the force on the comet, so the comet's force pushes Jupiter in one direction while Jupiter's force pushes the comet in the opposite direction. As a result, Jupiter's path will be altered, and it may experience a slight change in its orbit around the sun.

On the other hand, the force that Jupiter exerts on the comet will affect the comet by slowing it down and altering its trajectory. This is because the force on the comet is equal and opposite to the force on Jupiter, so Jupiter's force slows down the comet while the comet's force also affects Jupiter. Depending on the mass and speed of the comet, it may be deflected off course, or it may collide with Jupiter.

In summary, Newton's Third Law predicts that when Jupiter collides with a comet, the forces exerted on both objects will be equal and opposite. The force from the comet will affect Jupiter by altering its path, while the force from Jupiter will affect the comet by slowing it down and altering its trajectory. The specific outcome of the collision will depend on the mass and speed of the objects involved.

23. describe what happens to the positions of the stars, jupiter's moons as compared to the position of jupiter


Explanation:

The positions of the stars and Jupiter's moons appear to change relative to Jupiter as the planet rotates on its axis. This is due to the fact that Jupiter, like all planets, rotates on its axis as it orbits the Sun. As it rotates, different parts of Jupiter's surface come into view, causing the apparent positions of the stars and moons to shift in relation to the planet.

From our vantage point on Earth, we can observe Jupiter's four largest moons - Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto - as they orbit the planet. These moons appear to move in a predictable pattern relative to Jupiter, with each moon completing one orbit in a different amount of time. As Jupiter rotates, the moons appear to move across the planet's surface, sometimes passing in front of the planet or behind it. The motions of these moons were some of the first evidence that Galileo used to support the heliocentric model of the solar system, which suggested that the planets orbit the Sun, rather than the Earth.

Similarly, the positions of the stars appear to change as Jupiter rotates, due to the changing perspective of our view from Earth. As Jupiter rotates, the stars appear to move across the sky in relation to the planet, with different constellations coming into view over time. This apparent motion is due to the rotation of the Earth on its axis, which causes our view of the sky to change over time.
