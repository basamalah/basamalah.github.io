2. In a reaction, chlorine accepts an electron to form Cl-. Which statement is true about the atomic size of the chlorine ion? A. The atomic size of the chlorine ion is larger than the size of the chlorine atom. B. The atomic size of the chlorine ion is smaller than the size of the chlorine atom. C. The atomic size of the chlorine ion reduces to zero. D. The atomic size of the chlorine ion is equal to the size of the chlorine atom.


The answer is A. When chlorine atom accepts an electron, it becomes an anion. When this happens, the atomic size of the anion becomes larger than the parent atom because there are now more electrons than protons. The number of protons stays the same and the electrons "feel" a weaker attraction from the nucleus. Also, there is now a strong electrostatic repulsion between the electrons and they tend to push each other. Thus, these contributing factors lead to the expansion of the electron cloud of the Anion  making it "bigger" than its neutral atomic state.

The Atomic Number (Z) indicates the number of protons in an atom of an element. If the atom is neutral then the number of protons will be equal to the number of electrons. So the atomic number can also indicate the number of electrons.  

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number, but different mass numbers  

Atomic mass is the average atomic mass of all its isotopes  

the average atomic mass = m₁ x %m₁ + m₂ x %m₂...mₙ x %mₙ

m₁=m₂=mₙ = isotope mass

%m₁=%m₂=%mₙ=%abundance

24.47% chlorine-37(36.97)

Problem:

the ratio of hydrogen to chlorine 1:35. what mass of chlorine is needed when there are 25g of chlorine available to produce hydrochloric acid?​

Note:

The data suggest that in every 1 gram hydrogen atom, there are 35 grams of Chlorine atom needed in order to react to create hydrochloric acid.

We can use the data as a conversion factor in order to determine the amount of hydrogen in grams needed to react with 25 grams of chlorine.

Solution:

25 grams Cl × 1 gram H / 35 grams Cl = 0.71 grams of hydrogen
