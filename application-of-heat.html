Use a ruler and a pen to measure and mark a three inch squre on the aluminum foil. 2. Cut the square out. 3. Stick your thumb in the middle of the square and use your other hand to lift the sides of the foil around your thumb, creating a miniature boat shape. 4. Measure 1/4 teaspoon of sugar. 5. Put the sugar in the foil boat. 6. Study the sugar with your magnifying glass. Can you see each individual sugar piece? 7. What do you think will happen when you heat the sugar? Write your guess, or hypothesis, in your notebook. 8. Have a grown-up use matches to light the candle. 9. Put the metal dish next to the candle. 10. Using the tongs, hold the foil boat just above the flame. 11. Slowly count to ten as you observe what happens to the sugar. Don't just 12. use your your eyes -- use your nose to note how the sugar smells. 13. Remove the foil boat and blow out the candle. 14. Set the heated sugar on the metal dish. 15. Use your magnifying glass to study the heated sugar. 16. Did the sugar do what you expected?Results: The sugar should have melted down to a liquid form and turned a light brown color. Once the sugar had been heating for awhile, it should have turned black and started producing wisps of smoke. Once you transferred the sugar from the flame to the metal dish, the sugar would be blackish brown, solid and surrounded by black soot.     Why?Sugar is made of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen atoms. When heated over a candle, these elements react with the fire to turn into a liquid. The heat causes the sugar's atoms to combine with the oxygen in the air, forming new groups of atoms. Energy is released in this chemical reaction in the form of smoke and black soot. We can't usually tell the chemical composition, or what exact elements make up a substance, just by using our eyes. Did you know, for example, that sugar contained carbon, hydrogen and oxygen? Scientists can guess what will happen when your heat a substance if they know its chemical composition. What other substances can you test? Have a grown-up help you conduct this same experiment, but with salt. Do you think salt will react the same way as sugar does? Physical Changes

Physical changes are changes affecting to the form of chemical substance, but its not chemical composition.

Examples of physical properties include melting, transition to a gas, change of strength, change of durability, changes to crystal form, textural change, shape, size, color, volume and density.

Chemical Changes

Chemical changes occur when the substance combines with other to form a new substance, called chemical synthesis or, alternatively, chemical decomposition into two or more different substances.

These processes are called chemical reactions and, in general, are not reversible except by further chemical reactions

10. what are the applications of heat transfer​


Answer:

APPLICATIONS OF HEAT TRANSFER:

1. You can warm your back muscles with a heating pad. As the pad heats up, it transfers heat to the part of your body that it is in contact with.

2. The heat from a hot liquid makes the cup itself hot. If you grab a cup that has hot coffee, tea or broth in it, the cup itself will be hot and your hands will feel it.

3. If you are cold and someone holds you to warm you, the heat is being conducted from their body to yours.

4. If you leave a metal spoon propped up in a pot, it will become hot from the boiling water inside the pot.

5. Chocolate candy in your hand will eventually melt as heat is conducted from your hand to the chocolate.

6. When ironing a piece of clothing, the iron is hot and the heat is transferred to the clothing.

7. If you stoke a fire in the fireplace with a poker and leave the poker in the fire, the poker will become very hot.

8. A heat exchanger uses a hot fluid to conduct heat to a cooler fluid without the two actually coming into direct contact.

9. If you touch a hot stove, heat will be conducted to your finger and your skin will burn.

10. Sand can conduct heat. This is why walking on the beach on a hot summer day will warm your feet.

11. It's important to protect your dog's paws when walking on hot asphalt or concrete.

12. They conduct heat to your pet's feet just as they would to yours.

Light bulbs give off heat. If you touch a light bulb when it is on, your hand will get burned.

13. The wires in your home conduct electricity and allow lights to come on when you flip the switch.

14. When you turn on your computer or any other electric or battery-operated devices or appliances, electrical conduction is what makes them function.

15. With a landline phone, sound waves are converted into electricity that is conducted over wires to the other person's phone. There, it is converted back to sound waves.

16. Lightning rods are installed on buildings to attract lightning to hit them and conduct their energy down into the ground via attached wires rather than damaging the structure.

17. Hearts have their own naturally occurring electrical conduction system. When a heart beats, its electrical signals cause the heart to beat in an appropriate rhythm.

18. If the electrical system in someone's heart doesn't work properly, the person may need to have a pacemaker installed to simulate conduction.

19. The ions in water cause it to conduct electricity. This is why it is important to avoid swimming during a lightning storm; if lightning hits the water, anyone in it risks electrocution.

20. This also holds true with electrical appliances and water. If a plugged-in blow dryer (or any other electric item) falls into a tub or sink filled with water, do not touch it. The water would conduct the electricity such that touching the device could result in electrocution.

21. When table salt is dissolved in water, it can conduct electricity. That is because the water separates the sodium ions from the chlorine ions apart.
