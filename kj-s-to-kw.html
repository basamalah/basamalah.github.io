Answer:

We can use Hess's law to calculate ∆H for the reaction PCl5 (g) → PCl3 (g) + Cl2 (g) using the given data. Hess's law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the pathway taken, as long as the initial and final conditions are the same.

First, we need to reverse the second equation and multiply it by 6 to get the same number of moles of Cl2 as in the first equation:

6P4 (s) + 30Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) ∆H = 6(-3438 kJ) = -20628 kJ

Next, we need to cancel out the P4 (s) in the first equation with the P4 (s) in the second equation. We can do this by multiplying the first equation by 4:

4P4 (s) + 24Cl2 (g) → 16PCl3 (g) ∆H = 4(-2439 kJ) = -9756 kJ

Finally, we can add the two equations together, canceling out the 16PCl3 (g) on both sides:

6P4 (s) + 30Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) ∆H = -20628 kJ

4P4 (s) + 24Cl2 (g) → 16PCl3 (g) ∆H = -9756 kJ

6P4 (s) + 54Cl2 (g) → 24PCl5 (g) + 16Cl2 (g) ∆H = -30384 kJ

The overall reaction is the same as the reaction we want to find ∆H for, except that it includes an extra 16 moles of Cl2 (g) on the product side. To get the ∆H for the desired reaction, we can subtract the ∆H for the extra 16 moles of Cl2 (g):

∆H = -30384 kJ - (16 mol x 0 kJ/mol) = -30384 kJ

Therefore, the ∆H for the reaction PCl5 (g) → PCl3 (g) + Cl2 (g) is -30384 kJ.

The efficiency of a heat engine is given by the equation:

efficiency = work output / heat input

Multiplying both sides by heat input, we get:

work output = efficiency x heat input

Substituting the given values, we have:

efficiency = 31.4% = 0.314 (in decimal form)
heat input = 8.72 kJ

work output = 0.314 x 8.72 kJ
work output = 2.74 kJ

Therefore, the work performed by the heat engine in each cycle is 2.74 kJ.

Explanation:

Let's consider the following reaction for the synthesis of nitric acid.

NaNO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(l) → NaHSO₄(s) + HNO₃(g)

We can calculate the standard enthalpy of the reaction (ΔH°rxn) using the following expression.

ΔH°rxn = ∑ np × ΔH°f(p) - ∑ nr × ΔH°f(r)

where,

n: moles

ΔH°f: standard enthalpy of formation

p: products

r: reactants

ΔH°rxn = 1 mol × ΔH°f(NaHSO₄) + 1 mol × ΔH°f(HNO₃) - 1 mol × ΔH°f(NaNO₃) - 1 mol × ΔH°f(H₂SO₄)

ΔH°rxn = 1 mol × (-1125.5 kJ/mol) + 1 mol × (-135.1 kJ/mol) - 1 mol × (-467.8 kJ/mol) - 1 mol × (-814.0 kJ/mol)

The enthalpy change for the reaction :ΔH°rxn = -847.6 kJ/mol

Further explanation  

Determination of the enthalpy of formation of a reaction can be through a calorimetric experiment, based on the principle of Hess's Law, enthalpy of formation table, or from bond energy data  

The principle of Hess's Law is to calculate the enthalpy of a chemical reaction based on its initial and final states

A chemical reaction to produce a product can have a different step-by-step reaction, but the enthalpy change that occurs will remain the same

This reaction stage can be described in the form of an energy level diagram

ΔH₁ = ΔH₂ + ΔH₃

Given

The two chemical equations

Required

the enthalpy change

Solution

Reaction 1 :

2Al (s) + 3/2O₂ (g) ⇒ Al₂O₃ (s) ; ΔH°rxn = - 1669.8 kJ/mol

the second reaction is reversed (ΔH°rxn=+) :

Fe₂O₃(s)⇒2Fe(s) + 3/2O₂(g) ; ΔH°rxn = + 822.2 kJ/mol

Add the two reactions, and remove the same compound on different sides (3/2O₂) :

2 Al (s) + Fe₂O₃(s) ⇒ 2 Fe (s) +  Al₂O₃ (s) ; ΔH°rxn = -847.6 kJ/mol

23. A steam turbine receives 5,000 kg/hr of steam at 5 mpa and 4000c and velocity of 25 m/sec. it leaves the turbine at 0.006 mpa and 85% quality and velocity of 20 m/sec. radiation is 10,000 kj/hr. find the kw developed.


To solve the problem, we need to use the First Law of Thermodynamics which states that the change in internal energy of a system is equal to the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system. Assuming steady state conditions and neglecting kinetic and potential energy changes, the energy balance for the steam turbine can be written as:

m_dot * (h_in - h_out) = W - Q

where m_dot is the mass flow rate of steam, h_in and h_out are the specific enthalpies at the inlet and outlet of the turbine, W is the work done by the turbine, and Q is the heat lost by radiation.

Using steam tables, we can find the specific enthalpies:

h_in = 3587 kJ/kg

h_out = 2440 kJ/kg

Substituting the given values, we get:

5000 kg/hr * (3587 - 2440) kJ/kg = W - 10000 kJ/hr

Simplifying and converting units, we get:

W = 7387 kW

Therefore, the power developed by the steam turbine is 7387 kW.

Step 1: Determine the signs for heat and work.

Since heat is absorbed, then

q = +50 kJ

Since the work is done by the system, then

w = -65 kJ

Step 2: Calculate the change in internal energy.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta E & = q + w \\ & = \text{+50 kJ} + (-\text{65 kJ}) \\ & = \boxed{-\text{15 kJ}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the answer is B. -15 kJ.
