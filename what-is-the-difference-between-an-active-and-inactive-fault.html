Difference Between Active and Inactive Fault

A fault is a zone of brittle deformation generated by rock strata, whether or not they have undergone the process of soil consolidation. The fact that a defect exists does not guarantee that it will occur again. Faults can be classified as active and inactive, similar to how volcanoes can be separated into three categories: active, dormant, and dead.

Active faultStructures along which we expect displacement are known as active faults. All shallow earthquakes take place on active faults since a shallow earthquake is defined as a mechanism that induces displacement over a fault.Within the last ten thousand years, earthquakes are known to have recently been created by active faults.A geologic risk that is connected to earthquakes as a source is active faulting. The effects of movement on an active fault include strong ground motion, surface faulting, tectonic deformation, landslides and rockfalls, liquefaction, tsunamis, and earthquakes.

Inactive faultStructures that can be located but do not have earthquakes are known as inactive faultsA fault that is inactive may nevertheless cause an earthquake in the future even though it shows no symptoms of doing so in the past ten thousand years.

Additional information:

Rocks move and change shape constantly, albeit very slowly. Rocks can move and bend under the extreme pressure and temperature conditions that are typical deep under the Earth. Rocks are more brittle and cold in the drier regions of Earth, and they fracture when subjected to high forces. Brittle rock failure occurs as a result of earthquakes.

A fault is a gap where the rocks have moved apart. They can be as small as a few micrometers or as large as thousands of kilometers and tens of kilometers deep, but they are typically much thinner than they are long or deep. Different faults can support different types of rock deformation, such as compression and extension, in addition to diversity in size and direction.

9. what are the differences of active fault and inactive fault?


An active fault is a fault that has displayed recent seismic activity, while an
inactive fault has not displayed recent seismic activity. Do not be fooled by
the word "recent," however, as we are talking about "recent" from a geological
perspective, which is much different from a non-geologic perspective. Because of
the fickle nature of plate tectonics, an active fault could have earthquakes as often as once every few years or once every one thousand years.

The difference between active and inactive faults are:

Identifiable structures that do not produce earthquakes are called inactive faults.

2. Active Faults are inactive and sleeping. Inactive Faults produce earthquakes on a constant basis.

3. Active faults have characteristics along which we anticipate displacement. Hence they are referred to as active faults. Inactive faults are structures that can be located but do not cause earthquakes.

THE FAULTS

Dip-slip faults are faults that slide in the direction of the dip plane and, depending on their motion, are either normal or reverse (thrust). Strike-slip faults are faults that move horizontally and are classed as either right-lateral or left-lateral.

In geology, a fault is a planar crack or discontinuity in a volume of rock across which rock-mass movements have caused significant displacement.  The activity of plate tectonic forces produces massive faults inside the Earth's crust, with the greatest from the borders between the plates, such as the megathrust faults of subduction zones or transform faults. The majority of earthquakes are caused by the release of energy during rapid movement on active faults. Slow displacement of faults may also occur by aseismic glide.

A fault plane is the plane representing a fault's fracture surface. A fault trace or fault line is a surface location where the fault can be observed or mapped. A fault trace is also the line frequently used to show a fault on geology maps.

A fault zone consists of a collection of parallel faults. Nevertheless, the phrase is sometimes used to describe the zone of crushed rock along a single fault.

Active faults are structure along which we expect displacement to occur. By definition, since a shallow earthquake is a process that produces displacement across a fault, all shallow earthquakes occur on active faults.

As you can imagine, because of the complexity of earthquake activity, judging a fault to be inactive can be tricky, but often we can measure the last time substantial offset occurred across a fault. If a fault has been inactive for millions of years, it's certainly safe to call it inactive.
