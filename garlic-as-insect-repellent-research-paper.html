6. What are the typical target insects of most repellants? What harm can these repellants bring to humans?​


Answer:

What are the typical target insects of most repellants?

Insect repellents are used to repel mosquitoes, ticks, flies, and other biting insects. Mosquitoes can transmit diseases such as Zika, Malaria, and West Nile virus. Ticks can transmit Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, and other diseases. Repellents are not meant to kill insects, but to keep them away to prevent bites and the spread of disease.

What harm can these repellants bring to humans?​

An increasing number of evidence suggests that insect repellents may trigger undesirable hazardous interactions with biological systems with a potential to generate harmful effects including intermediate metabolites.

Biodegradation is the naturally-occurring breakdown of materials by microorganisms such as bacteria and fungi or other biological activity. Composting is a human-driven process in which biodegradation occurs under a specific set of circumstances.

Biotransformation is the process by which a substance changes from one chemical to another (transformed) by a chemical reaction within the body.Biotransformation is the process by which a substance changes from one chemical to another (transformed) by a chemical reaction within the body. Biotransformation plays a key role in modulating the toxicities of VOCs. Many VOCs are poorly soluble in water and the metabolism converts them to relatively water-soluble derivatives, which may be more readily eliminated in the largely aqueous urine and/or bile.

Biodegradation is nature's way of recycling wastes, or breaking down organic matter into nutrients that can be used by other organisms. In this case the purpose is to break down organic matter so that it will not cause pollution problems when the water is released into the environment.

28. environmental impact of insect repellents?


Answer:

Toxicity to non-target organisms: Insect repellents contain active ingredients that can be toxic to non-target organisms such as bees, butterflies, and other beneficial insects. When these insects come into contact with the insect repellent, they may die or suffer other negative impacts.

Contamination of soil and water: The active ingredients in insect repellents can contaminate soil and water when they are sprayed or applied. This contamination can affect the health of plants, animals, and humans who come into contact with these resources.

Air pollution: Aerosol insect repellents can release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) into the air, which can contribute to air pollution and negatively impact air quality.

Plastic waste: Many insect repellents come in plastic packaging that can contribute to plastic waste, which can harm wildlife and ecosystems.

Negative impact on biodiversity: The use of insect repellents can disrupt the natural balance of ecosystems by killing insects that play important roles in pollination, pest control, and other ecological functions.
