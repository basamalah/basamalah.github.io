4. What is the frequency of greenlight that has a wavelength of 5.5 x 10-7 m? What are the given, formula and procedure


The greenlight frequency with 5.5 × 10^-7 meters of wavelength is 5.45 × 10^14 Hz. To solve this question, we will use wavelength equation.

The electromagnetic spectrum is most frequently connected with wavelength, which is defined as the separation between two frequency wave peaks.

The data you are given will determine how to calculate wavelength. The fundamental wavelength formula can be used if you are aware of the wave's speed and frequency.

The energy equation would be used to calculate the wavelength of light given the specific energy of a photon. If you know the right equation, calculating wavelength is simple.

Wavelength equation:

λ=c/f

where, λ is wavelength, c is speed of light, and f is frequency

In this case, the given data (variables) are wavelength (λ) and speed of light (c),

λ = 5.5 x 10^-7 m

c = 2.998 x 10^8 m/s

Note:

Always maintain consistency in units throughout the equation. The majority of calculations are performed solely in metric units. If the frequency is expressed in kilohertz (kHz) or the wave speed is expressed in km/s, you must multiply these values by 1000 to convert them to Hertz and m/s (10 kHz = 10,000 Hz). Since the variable units are the same in meters (m), we don't have to convert the variable unit.

Then, we input the given variables to the equation:

λ = c/f

f = c/λ

f = (2.998 x 10^8 m/s) / (5.5 x 10^-7 m)

f = 5.45 x 10^14 Hz

As the result, the greenlight frequency is 5.45 x 10^14 Hz

12. It is a metrical tale or a tale that follows the structure of a poem. The size of each stanza has 12 syllables.


Metre refers to the rhythmic structure of lines of verse. The majority of English verse since Chaucer is inaccentual-syllabic metre, which consists of alternating stressed and unstressed syllables within a fixed total number of syllables in each line. The metrical rhythm is thus the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in each line. Groups of syllables are known as metrical feet; each line of verse is made up of a set number of feet. Thus:

Monometer: one foot per line

Dimeter: two feet per line

Trimeter: three feet per line

Tetrameter: four feet per line

Pentameter: five feet per line

Hexameter: six feet per line

Heptameter: seven feet per line

Octameter: eight feet per line

28. Discuss the reason's for the use of the magical, the supernatural, the miraculous and the exotic in such tales as the man of law's tale, the squire's tale, the prioress tale, the second nun's tale, and part others​


The use of magical, supernatural, wondrous, and exotic elements in tales such as the Lawman's Tale, the Squire's Tale, the Prioress' Tale, and the Second Nun's Tale serves multiple purposes.

First, it allows the stories to explore themes and ideas that are difficult to express through realistic depictions of everyday life. For example, stories about miraculous or supernatural events can convey moral or religious messages, such as the power of faith or the consequences of sin, in an engaging and thought-provoking way.

Second, it adds an exciting and entertaining element to the stories that helps capture the audience's attention and engage them. The use of magical or exotic settings, characters, and events can create a sense of wonder and escapism, transporting the audience to another time and place.

Finally, the use of such elements in these stories reflects the cultural and historical context in which they were written. Many of these stories were written at a time when belief in magic and the supernatural was widespread, and the inclusion of such elements reflects the beliefs and values of the society in which they were created.
