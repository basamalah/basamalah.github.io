19. 1. How to get the value of Pearson r product moment correlation?​


The figure below illustrates the idea of measuring the "similarity" of two vectors A and B. In the figure, the vectors are separated by an angle theta. A pretty good measure of how "similar" they are is the cosine of theta. Think about what cosine is doing. If both A and B point in roughly the same direction, the cosine of theta will be about 1. If they point in opposite directions, it will be -1. And if they are perpendicular or orthogonal, it will be 0. In this way, the cosine of theta fits our intuition pretty well about what it means for two vectors to be "correlated" with each other. And cosine of theta is the correlation coefficient. (Actually, Pearson correlation is also called centered cosine similarity)

If the pattern of residuals changes along the regression line then consider using rank methods or linear regression after an appropriate transformation of your data.

Pearson's product moment correlation coefficient (r) is given as a measure of linear association between the two variables:

r² is the proportion of the total variance (s²) of Y that can be explained by the linear regression of Y on x. 1-r² is the proportion that is not explained by the regression. Thus 1-r² = s²xY / s²Y.

Confidence limits are constructed for r using Fisher's z transformation. The null hypothesis that r = 0 (i.e. no association) is evaluated using a modified t test (Armitage and Berry, 1994; Altman, 1991).
