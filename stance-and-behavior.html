According to Moats (2010), oral language is made up of at least five essential elements: phonological abilities, pragmatics, syntax, morphological abilities, and vocabulary (also referred to as semantics). In order to communicate and learn through spoken engagement and conversation, oral language needs to have all of these elements.

Language that is directly spoken by speakers to language listeners is referred to as spoken language. Intonation determines how well one can understand spoken language. The sound system used in spoken language has set rules. Compared to writing, speaking language is used in more dynamic situations. Speaking carries out spoken language communication in a general and efficient manner. All languages in the world typically own spoken language.

The primary source of language is verbal communication. Spoken language usage can occur in official, semi-formal, or informal communication settings. Speaking becomes a second language for those with language impairments, especially those who are deaf, whereas sign language is taught first.

The use of language components including signs, syntax, language structure, and passive building is one of spoken language's many qualities. The syntax of spoken language is more repetitive and less organized since signs are of the paralinguistic type. In addition, topic-adjective patterns and passive formulations are rarely used in spoken language. While conversing, spoken language can also be improved.
