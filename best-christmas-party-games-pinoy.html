6. A welcome remarks for Christmas parties in school


A welcome remarks for Christmas parties in school

"Good day everyone. We are here gathered today to celebrate a special occasion together. I welcome you all to our classroom and I am very grateful that you come holding the same purpose. After several months of facing the books and chalkboards, it is time to unwind and soothe ourselves with delicious delicacies and sweet smiles. As we celebrate Christmas, let us put aside our school works and enjoy the whole day with Christ in our hearts. I could see the gifts you are holding and expectedly you are going to exchange it later to someone who will be your manito or manita. I feel excited too.  Feel free to use our facilities around and do not hesitate to ask help to anyone around here. Today, just like other days, we are family here in school.  Enjoy the food because everyone brings it for everybody. Parents, this is a good time to show our children the value and the ways of sharing so even if we are not around, they will practice sharing even when it is not Christmas. Children, serve your parents and accommodate them well because you know the classroom better than they do since this is your second home and they are your visitors this time.  Once again, welcome and enjoy the rest of your day with one another. Merry Christmas!  "

WHAT IS A WELCOME REMARKS?

Welcome remarks is a short speech usually done by the host or someone he/she assigned  to welcome the visitors and participants of a certain activity.  

THINGS TO REMEMBER IN DELIVERING WELCOME REMARKS Know your audience .Greet your audience (if there are special guests, mention their names) .Acknowledge your guests and welcome them warmly. Give a short background about the event including what they can expect .Say something that will really make them feel welcomed .End your speech with a line that assures their being welcomed.  Make your speech short, simple and direct to the point.

PURPOSE OF WELCOME REMARKS

to specifically acknowledge and welcome the guests and participants of a certain event it gives a short background or the purpose of the event to make the visitors at ease and so that the audience who do not know where they are will understand

7. Hacking a Pinoy Christmas abroad​


Answer:

Making do is probably hardest dur­ing Christmas. The Philippines has the world’s longest Christmas season, filled with celebration and reunions, traditions like Simbang Gabi and Noche Buena, and beloved dishes like puto bumbong and lechon. It’s difficult to make do and replicate a Filipino Christmas when your family is thousands of kilometers away and there isn’t a lechonero to be found anywhere near you. But Filipinos—with their resiliency and their resourceful­ness—find a way.

Food writer Margaux Salcedo talks of the Christmas she spent in Ohio and how she substituted half and half for the evaporated milk that is used to make her Nana Meng’s famous tsokolate. Another time, she made a frantic long-distance call because her cousin wanted to roast a lechon: “Nana Meng, ano ulit binubuhos sa lechon? Sprite? (What do you pour over lechon?)”

My classmates, Roni in Brit­ain and Jonef in Ireland have also attempted lechon—fashioning makeshift spits and roasting by trial and error. It took hours, Jonef tells me, and it started to rain before the lechon was done. A whole, cleaned pig isn’t easy to find so many people use whole, rolled pork bellies instead.

In Vienna, my friend Anne has man­aged to find substitutes from other cui­sines. “The Serbians sell lechon every day of the week,” she says. “I just make the liver sauce.” Anne, who trained as a chef, substitutes prosciutto for caul fat to wrap her embutido. And when she is too lazy to make ensaimadas, she buys bri­oche, spreads it with butter, and sprinkles it with sugar and grated Edam cheese.

Other friends settle for making just one or two Filipino dishes that can be cooked with readily available ingredients and cook something non-Filipino for the rest of their meal. In Texas, my friend Cil makes pork embutido because it’s a fairly easy recipe. Other than that, she mostly ends up with non-Filipino food. My col­lege friend Maricar works in New York and makes time-consuming molo soup using a recipe from her husband’s aunt. She usually opts to make a roast beef or roast lamb for their main course.

Friends who live in areas where there is a large Filipino community say that it is easier for them. They are able to cook using ingredients from Filipino grocery stores or substitutes from Asian groceries. There are also restaurants and caterers that serve Filipino food. In Australia, my friend Gigi is able to order anything from lechon and dinuguan to rellenong bangus. Even kakanin like sapin-sapin and bibingka are available. Asian stores and markets sell popular Filipino brands like Mang Tomas and even vegetables like malunggay and pandan leaves.

The same is true for my cousins in Toronto and Vancouver. Mona, who left the Philippines with her family years ago, says that specialty Filipino grocery stores like Seafood City sell most of the products they need, and they have been able to order lechon and crispy pata for Noche Buena. Despite that, her mom, my Tita Lottie, will still make specialties like suman from scratch. I would guess that anything homemade tastes better and is cheaper than something bought at a store. “Malaki, malamig, matigas, (big, cold, and hard), and one dollar each!” Margaux says of the suman she found in a Filipino grocery store in New York.

In places like California and London, Filipino fast food joints like Jollibee are available. Helen lives just a few miles away from a large Filipino community that is home to Jollibee, Red Ribbon, Chow King, and Goldilocks, as well as Filipino markets and turo-turo restau­rants. She never has a problem when she craves Filipino food, and she doesn’t even have to cook!

Other Filipinos learn to cook from necessity. Roni, in London, couldn’t find the Vigan longganisa that she craves whenever she misses home. So she learned to make it for herself, and dis­covered that she could sell it to friends. She also cooks and hosts supper clubs that showcase her contemporary take on Filipino food, and eventually branched out into bottled adobo and barbecue sauces that she sells on Amazon under the label Roni B’s Kitchen. For Filipinos who can’t cook at all, using these bottled sauces is an easy way to hack Pinoy flavors for Christmas.

I suddenly remember another de­tail about my family’s Christmases in Singapore. My parents always invited people who had nowhere else to spend Christmas Day to our home for lunch. It didn’t matter if they were good friends or business associates or random acquain­tances my dad had just met—there was always a seat for them at our Christmas table.

Eventu­ally, many of them became friends for life. So perhaps, that is the best way to hack a Pinoy Christmas—to surround yourself with love and share your Christmas—whether it is with family or with friends who have be­come family.

18. How to be a good emcee at a christmas party?


If you want to be a good emcee at a Christmas party, you should be prepared with your script and have a cheerful attitude throughout the program.  Always start with welcoming everybody to the party and making sure that everyone is enjoying themselves. Don't be nervous if you forget a few words from your script; just know the gist of what you're going to say so you can just change the words and speak spontaneously. Do not make people wait too long and make sure to acknowledge the performances. Say something nice for each presentation and introduce people positively. Look at the audience but do not stare at just one person. Just look at the audience in general and try to move around to interact with them. Smile and never forget that it is a party and it doesn't hurt if you have fun as well. 

19. Christmas party with classmates essay


The Christmas party with my classmates was one of the most memorable experiences of my academic life. It was a fun-filled event that brought together students from different grades, backgrounds, and interests. We had been looking forward to the party for weeks and the day finally arrived.

The party was held in the school hall, which was beautifully decorated with colorful balloons, streamers, and Christmas lights. The festive ambiance immediately put us in the holiday spirit. The tables were adorned with red and green tablecloths, and there were platters of delicious food and drinks on every table.

As soon as everyone arrived, we started playing games like Secret Santa, White Elephant, and Christmas charades. It was heartwarming to see everyone laughing and having a good time, forgetting about the stress of schoolwork and exams for a while. The highlight of the party was the talent show, where students showcased their singing, dancing, and acting skills.

The event was also an opportunity for us to give back to the community. We had organized a donation drive where students brought gifts and food items that were donated to the local shelter. It was a small gesture, but it meant a lot to those in need.

As the night went on, we continued to dance, eat, and play games. The DJ played a mix of Christmas songs and popular hits, and everyone was on their feet, dancing and singing along. It was a night of joy, happiness, and togetherness.

The Christmas party with my classmates was more than just a fun event. It brought us together, strengthened our bonds, and reminded us of the importance of giving back to the community. It was a night I will always cherish and a memory that will stay with me forever.

30. examples of opening remarks in christmas party


Opening Remarks

There are lots of ways to render an opening remarks to a Christmas party. One way is to give everyone a warm welcome and allow them to feel that they are part of the event and that the party is for everyone. The speaker might also cite an experience relating to Christmas and what he/she had gained from that experience. He/She might also give a message that includes the birth of Jesus Christ and how important it is to recognize him as the Savior.
