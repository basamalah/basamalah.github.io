Answer:

Stellar nucleosynthesis is the creation (nucleosynthesis) of chemical elements by nuclear fusion reactions within stars. Stellar nucleosynthesis has occurred since the original creation of hydrogen, helium and lithium during the Big Bang. As a predictive theory, it yields accurate estimates of the observed abundances of the elements. It explains why the observed abundances of elements change over time and why some elements and their isotopes are much more abundant than others. The theory was initially proposed by Fred Hoyle in 1946,[1] who later refined it in 1954.[2] Further advances were made, especially to nucleosynthesis by neutron capture of the elements heavier than iron, by Margaret and Geoffrey Burbidge, William Alfred Fowler and Hoyle in their famous 1957 B2FH paper,[3] which became one of the most heavily cited papers in astrophysics history.

20. what is stellar revolution​


Stellar revolution refers to the process of changes that occur within a star during its lifetime. Stars are born from clouds of gas and dust, and they undergo a series of changes as they age, eventually leading to their death.

If referring to chemistry, stellar nucleosynthesis refers to the process of creating new atomic nuclei within stars through nuclear fusion reactions. During nuclear fusion, atomic nuclei combine to form heavier elements, such as helium from hydrogen, carbon from helium, and so on. This process occurs at different stages of a star's life, depending on its mass and other factors, and is responsible for the creation of all naturally occurring elements up to iron.

In addition to stellar nucleosynthesis, stars also play an important role in the evolution and distribution of elements throughout the universe. When massive stars end their lives in supernova explosions, they release heavy elements into the interstellar medium, which can later be incorporated into new stars and planets. The study of stellar nucleosynthesis is therefore essential for understanding the origins and abundances of the elements in the universe.
