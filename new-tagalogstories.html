Answer:

JEWISH PASSOVER

Answer:

The Jewish festival of Passover is a highly important event that commemorates the Jewish people's liberation from Egypt's king over 3,000 years ago. This is known as the Exodus narrative. Passover is one of the most significant holidays for Jews in Israel and across the world. They commemorate the seven-day celebration by declaring the first and last days of the festival to be official holidays, and many people take the week off to travel across the nation. During Passover, Jews abstain from eating leavened foods (those containing yeast), such as bread, and stores refrain from selling bread and bread products for the whole week.

It is the oldest continuously observed Jewish holiday and lasts seven or eight days, depending on where you live. It starts on the evening of March 27 this year. Passover begins with a large religious dinner called the Seder (pronounced "say-der"), which means "order" in Hebrew. Family and friends gather to enjoy traditional foods. A unique Seder plate is put on the table, with symbolic dishes representing various aspects of the Passover tale, including as hard-boiled eggs, greens, a lamb bone, bitter herbs, salt water, and haroset (a bitter herb).

Matzah is a crisp cracker-like bread prepared from wheat and water that is unleavened, which means it hasn't been given time to rise. It is a highly significant meal on the Seder table during Passover. According to legend, the Israelites were compelled to flee Egypt so fast that they didn't have time to rise their bread. They packed the dough in sacks and drove across the desert, where the hot sun baked the dough into thin, hard matzah.

At the start of the Seder dinner, one piece of matzah is broken and one piece is concealed. After the meal, the youngsters look for this piece, known as the afikoman (pronounced "a-fee-ko-mahn"), and the child who finds it receives money or a prize.
