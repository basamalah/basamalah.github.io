A spectrometer (/spɛkˈtrɒmɪtər/) is a scientific instrument used to separate and measure spectral components of a physical phenomenon. Spectrometer is a broad term often used to describe instruments that measure a continuous variable of a phenomenon where the spectral components are somehow mixed. In visible light a spectrometer can separate white light and measure individual narrow bands of color, called a spectrum. A mass spectrometer measures the spectrum of the masses of the atoms or molecules present in a gas. The first spectrometers were used to split light into an array of separate colors. Spectrometers were developed in early studies of physics, astronomy, and chemistry. The capability of spectroscopy to determine chemical composition drove its advancement and continues to be one of its primary uses. Spectrometers are used in astronomy to analyze the chemical composition of stars and planets, and spectrometers gather data on the origin of the universe.

19. Since the average atomic mass of hydrogen is 1.0079 and hydrogen is composed of one proton and one electron then why isn’t the average atomic mass of helium exactly 4.0316 (4 x 1.0079) helium has an average atomic mass of 4.0026.


Atomic mass is, simply put, the mass of an atom of an element. Though an atom is composed of protons, electrons, and neutrons, the particles that heavily contribute to the mass of the atom are the protons and neutrons. The mass of electron is negligible small and the mass of a proton is relatively close to the mass of a neutron. The mass number of an atom is the sum of the number of protons and the number of neutrons in the atom. (See this link for mass number: https://brainly.ph/question/1191482 )

Hydrogen has 1 proton, 1 electron, and 0 neutron (mass number = 1)Helium has 2 protons, 2 electrons, and 2 neutrons (mass number = 4)

So given that the atomic mass of hydrogen is 1.0079, the atomic mass of helium should be 1.0079x4 = 4.0316 since the mass number of helium is 4 times as much as the mass number of hydrogen but this is not the case.

There are different reasons as to why the atomic mass of helium is not equal to 4 times the atomic mass of hydrogen.

- An element has an isotope. (See this link: https://brainly.ph/question/1369895 )

An isotope is a variation of the same element (therefore having the same number of protons) but having different number of neutrons. Hydrogen has 3 isotopes.  

Hydrogen-1 (most abundant) = 1 proton, 0 neutron, 1 electron (mass number = 1)Hydrogen-2 = 1 proton, 1 neutron, 1 electron (mass number = 2)Hydrogen-3 = 1 proton, 2 neutrons, 1 electron (mass number = 3)

Scientists determine an element's atomic mass by the average atomic masses of each of the individual atomic masses of the isotopes accounting their relative abundance. Helium as well has 2 isotopes:

Helium-3 = 2 protons, 1 neutron, 2 electrons (mass number = 3)Helium-4 (most abundant) = 2 protons, 2 neutrons, 2 electrons (mass number = 4)

The average atomic mass of helium must therefore be slightly different from the actual atomic mass of the Helium-4 isotope.

However, this reason is only one minor factor.


- Binding Energy

The most contributing factor to the large difference (4.0316 from 4.0026) is because of Einstein's infamous equation:

[tex]E = mc^{2}[/tex]

(See this link: https://brainly.ph/question/1881403)

This is the binding energy that holds the nucleus together. Since it is taught that like repels like, how can protons stick together considering they have the same charge. This is the because of the binding energy. It is implied in the equation that mass and energy are actually interchangeable. Helium is composed of 2 protons, therefore there must be nuclear binding energy holding those 2 protons together. Now where did the atom derive its nuclear binding energy? It is therefore from the nucleus itself, or more particulary, from its mass. Some of the nucleus' is actually being used up as an energy in order to prevent an inevitable split of the nucleus. This is why the actual helium atomic mass is smaller than what is predicted.

What is the function of a spectrophotometer?

Explanation:

Spectrophotometry is a scientific technique used to measure the intensity of light either transmitted through or reflected from gas, liquid or solid samples. In transmission, which is directly related to absorbance per Beer's law, spectrophotometry is the technique that measures how much a substance absorbs a beam of light passing through it. By measuring the intensity at each wavelength, a spectrum is created and this can tell us much information about the sample through which the light passed.In reflectance, spectrophotometry measures the amount of light that is reflected from an opaque specimen. Again a spectrum is created by measuring the intensity at each wavelength of light. All this can be done with a spectrophotometer.

30. what is the ratio of velocity of sound in helium gas to that in air at the same pressure and temperature?molar mass of helium is 0.0004 kg,y=1.66.​ pls i really need this now ​


In order to understand how helium has this effect on a voice, it is helpful to first consider how sound waves form and travel, as well as some basic properties of gases.

Sound waves are formed by the vibration of something (a drum-skin or your vocal chords, for instance) in a medium such as air. In the case of a drum, as one strikes its skin, it vibrates up and down. As it moves up, it pushes against the gas molecules of the air, forcing them upward against other molecules. The gas molecules are compressed together and this ripple of compressed molecules moves up away from the drum. Meanwhile, the drum skin moves down and back up again, resulting in another compression. This moving series of compressions is a sound wave, and the distance between them is known as the wavelength.

All gas samples have the same number of molecules per unit volume at a given pressure and temperature, whether the gas is helium or nitrogen (the primary constituent of air). But not all gas molecules have the same mass. Nitrogen (and thus air) has a mass roughly seven times greater than that of helium. Nitrogen is thus denser than helium and sound waves travel through it more slowly than they do in helium. At 20 degrees Celsius, for example, sound travels at 927 meters a second through helium, but only at 344 meters a second through air.

Like the vibration of a drum or a violin string, the vibration frequency of the vocal cords is independent of the type of gas that surrounds them. Whereas the velocity of the sound waves is faster in helium (and the wavelength greater), the frequency remains unchanged because it is determined by the vibrating vocal cords. Rather the timbre, or quality, of the sound changes in helium: listen closely next time and you will notice that a voice doesn¿t become squeaky but instead sounds more like Donald Duck. It is the lesser density of the helium--which serves as the medium for the sound waves--flowing through the larynx that produces this differing quality in the voice.
