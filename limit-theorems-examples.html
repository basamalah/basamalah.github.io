Answer:

The Limit of the Rational Function Theorem and Limit of a Radical of a Function are both concepts in calculus that deal with determining the behavior of a function as its input approaches a particular value.

The Limit of a Rational Function Theorem states that if a function can be expressed as a ratio of two polynomials, then the limit of the function as the input approaches a particular value can be found by evaluating the limit of the ratio of the highest degree terms of the numerator and denominator. Specifically, if the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, then the limit of the function as the input approaches infinity or negative infinity is zero. If the degree of the numerator is equal to the degree of the denominator, then the limit is equal to the ratio of the coefficients of the highest degree terms. If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator, then the limit does not exist.

The Limit of a Radical of a Function refers to the behavior of a function that contains a radical (square root, cube root, etc.) as its input approaches a particular value. To evaluate the limit of such a function, you can use algebraic manipulation to simplify the function and eliminate the radical, and then apply the Limit of a Rational Function Theorem to find the limit. Alternatively, you can use special techniques such as conjugate multiplication or L'Hopital's Rule to evaluate the limit directly without simplifying the function.

Theorems on Limits

Step-by-step explanation:

Lecture Outline

Two Special Limits

Limit of a Constant Function

Let c be a constant. Then Limit(c,x = a) = c .

Example: Suppose that we consider Limit(5,x = 1) . To evaluate this limit, we must determine what value the constant function f(x) = 5 approaches as x approaches (but is not equal to) 1. Since f(x) is constantly equal to 5, its value does not change as x nears 1 and the limit is equal to 5.

Limit of the Identity Function

Limit(x,x = a) = a .

If we write out what the symbolism Limit(x,x = a) = a means, we have the evident assertion that as x approaches (but is not equal to) a , x approaches a .

Basic Limit Laws

Let c be a constant and assume that Limit(f(x),x = a) and Limit(g(x),x = a) both exist.

Sum Law

The limit of a sum is the sum of the limits:

Limit([f(x)+g(x)],x = a) = Limit(f(x),x = a)+Limit(...

Example: We evaluate the limit Limit([x+2],x = 5) . By the Sum Law, we have Limit([x+2],x = 5) = Limit(x,x = 5)+Limit(2,x = 5) , and we know how to evaluate the two limits on the right hand side of the last equation using the two special limits we discussed above: Limit(x,x = 5) = 5 and Limit(2,x = 5) = 2 . Hence Limit([x+2],x = 5) = 7 .

Constant Multiple Law

The limit of a constant times a function is the constant times the limit of the function:

Limit([c*f(x)],x = a) = c*Limit(f(x),x = a)

Example: Evaluate Limit([3*x+1],x = 5) .

Difference Law

The limit of a difference is the difference of the limits:

Limit([f(x)-g(x)],x = a) = Limit(f(x),x = a)-Limit(...

Note that the Difference Law follows from the Sum and Constant Multiple Laws.

Product Law

The limit of a product is the product of the limits:

Limit([f(x)*g(x)],x = a) = Limit(f(x),x = a)*Limit(...

Quotient Law

The limit of a quotient is the quotient of the limits (provided that the limit of the denominator is not 0):

Limit([f(x)/g(x)],x = a) = Limit(f(x),x = a)/Limit(...

Example: Evaluate Limit((x^2-4)/(x^2+x-6),x = 2) .

Power Law

The limit of a positive integer power of a function is the power of the limit of the function:

Limit([f(x)]^n,x = a) = [Limit(f(x),x = a)]^n

Example: Evaluate Limit((x^4-3*x)/(2*x+4),x = 2) .

Root Law

The limit of a positive integer root of a function is the root of the limit of the function:

Limit([f(x)]^(1/n),x = a) = [Limit(f(x),x = a)]^(1/...

It is assumed that 0 < Limit(f(x),x = a) if n is even.

Limits of Polynomials and Rational Functions

We note that if f is a polynomial or a rational function and a is in the domain of f , then Limit(f(x),x = a) = f(a) . This fact follows from application of the limit laws which have been stated up to this point.

Limits of Piecewise Defined Functions via One-Sided Limits

Theorem

Limit(f(x),x = a) = L if and only if both Limit(f(x),x = a,left) = L and Limit(f(x),x = a,right) = L .

Example: The Heaviside Function

Define the Heaviside function H(x) as follows:

> H:=piecewise(x<0,0,x>=0,1);

H := PIECEWISE([0, x < 0],[1, 0 <= x])

We investigate the left and right-hand limits of the function H(x)+1 at 0 visually.

> plot(H(x)+1,x=-2..2,y=-1..3,discont=true);

[Maple Plot]

Notice that Limit(H(x),x = 0,right) = 2 while Limit(H(x),x = 0,left) = 1 . Further, Limit(H(x),x = 0) does not exist because H(x) does not settle down to one specific finite value as x approaches 0.

The Squeeze Theorem

If f(x) <= g(x) and g(x) <= h(x) when x is near a (except possibly at a ) and both Limit(f(x),x = a) = L and Limit(h(x),x = a) = L , then Limit(g(x),x = a) = L .

Example: We evaluate Limit([x^2*sin(1/x)],x = 0) . Note that the product rule does not apply here because Limit(sin(1/x),x = 0) does not exist. Hence we must investigate the limit using other techniques. It is helpful to look at a graph of the function.

> g:=x->(x^2)*sin(1/x);

g := proc (x) options operator, arrow; x^2*sin(1/x)...

> plot(g(x),x=-1..1);

[Maple Plot]

It appears that g(x) approaches 0 as x approaches (but is not equal to) 0. But it also appears that the graph is wiggling a bit near 0. Perhaps we should take a closer look at the graph near the origin.

> plot(g(x),x=-1/2..1/2);

[Maple Plot]

It still seems that 0 is a good guess for the value of the limit. How can we prove that Limit([x^2*sin(1/x)],x = 0) really is equal to 0? The idea of the Squeeze Theorem is that if we can trap a function between two other functions (one above and one below) and these two other functions can be shown to approach the same limit, then the function caught between them must also approach that limit. In our example, there are two elementary functions that can be used to squeeze g(x) = x^2*sin(1/x) , name f(x) = -x^2 and h(x) = x^2 . Let's consider the situation visually.

> plot([-x^2,g(x),x^2],x=-1/2..1/2,color=[green,red,blue]);

[Maple Plot]

The red graph of g(x) = x^2*sin(1/x) is trapped between the blue and green graphs of f(x) = -x^2 and h(x) = x^2 respectively.

Moreover, Limit(-x^2,x = 0) = 0 and Limit(x^2,x = 0) = 0 . We conclude from the Squeeze Theorem that limit([x^2*sin(1/x)],x = 0) = 0 also.
