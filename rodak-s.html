5. How did Kodak's failure to adapt to digital photography impact its relationship with consumers and the wider industry? ​


Kodak's failure to adapt to digital photography had a significant impact on its relationship with consumers and the wider industry. As Kodak struggled to keep up with the shift to digital photography, its reputation as a leader in the industry began to erode.

In particular, consumers began to lose confidence in Kodak's ability to stay relevant in the industry, which affected their willingness to invest in Kodak's products. This resulted in declining sales and revenue for the company.

At the same time, Kodak's competitors were able to gain market share by embracing digital photography and adapting to changing consumer demands. As a result, Kodak lost its dominant position in the industry and was no longer considered a leader in photography.

Kodak's failure to adapt to digital photography also had wider implications for the industry as a whole. As one of the largest and most influential companies in photography, Kodak's inability to adapt sent a clear message to other industry players. It demonstrated that even the most established and successful companies can struggle to keep up with changes in technology and consumer behavior, and that companies must be willing to adapt or risk becoming obsolete.

In conclusion, Kodak's failure to adapt to digital photography had a significant impact on its relationship with consumers and the wider industry. The decline of Kodak's brand and reputation, as well as its loss of market share, served as a cautionary tale for other companies in the industry and demonstrated the importance of adapting to change in order to stay relevant and competitive.

24. How did Kodak's business model and competitive advantages change over time, and what impact did these changes have on the company's performance? ​


Kodak was once a leader in the photography industry, but the company's business model and competitive advantages changed significantly over time. At its peak, Kodak's business model was focused on film and cameras, with a strong emphasis on vertical integration, meaning that it owned the entire supply chain, from manufacturing film to developing and printing photographs.

However, with the advent of digital photography, Kodak's competitive advantages were eroded. Unlike film, digital photography did not require a physical product, and Kodak was unable to keep up with the technological changes. As a result, Kodak's business model shifted to focus on digital photography and printers, but this was not enough to keep the company competitive.

Kodak's business model and competitive advantages also suffered due to the company's inability to adapt to changes in consumer behavior. Instead of embracing the shift towards digital photography, Kodak clung to its film-based business model for too long, and was slow to develop digital products that could compete with its rivals.

The impact of these changes on Kodak's performance was significant. The company's revenue declined sharply, and it struggled to remain profitable. Kodak ultimately filed for bankruptcy in 2012, after years of declining sales and profits.

In conclusion, Kodak's inability to adapt to changes in technology and consumer behavior led to a decline in its business model and competitive advantages. The company's failure to keep up with the times ultimately had a negative impact on its performance and contributed to its bankruptcy.
