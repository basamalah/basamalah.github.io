1. 12 reasons that make a Nation third world?​


There is no specific definition of what makes a nation a "third world" country, and the term is often used pejoratively to describe nations that are considered to be underdeveloped or economically disadvantaged. However, some possible factors that might contribute to a nation being considered "third world" include:

Lack of access to education and other basic services.Poor infrastructure, including inadequate transportation and communication systems.Low levels of industrialization and technological development.Dependence on exports of raw materials and agricultural products.High levels of poverty and inequality.Weak political institutions and governance.High levels of corruption.Limited access to global markets and investment.Lack of access to credit and other financial services.High levels of unemployment and underemployment.Environmental degradation and limited natural resources.Vulnerability to external shocks, such as natural disasters or economic downturns.

The Philippines has been labeled as a third-world nation for many reasons. Some of these reasons are that the country has a high level of poverty, low Human Development Index (HDI) ranking, and is susceptible to natural disasters.

One of the main reasons why the Philippines is considered a third-world nation is because of its high level of poverty. According to the Philippine Statistics Authority, as of 2015, more than 26% of the population lives below the poverty line. This means that they cannot afford to meet their basic needs such as food, clothing, and shelter.

Another reason why the Philippines is considered a third-world nation is because of its low HDI ranking. The HDI is a measure of a country’s level of development and is based on three dimensions: life expectancy, education, and income. The Philippines’ HDI ranking is currently at 0.686, which puts it in the category of “low human development”.

Lastly, the Philippines is susceptible to natural disasters. This is because the country is located in a region that is prone to earthquakes, typhoons, and floods. For example, in 2013, Typhoon Haiyan devastated the Philippines and left more than 6,000 people dead.

There are several solutions that can be implemented in order to address the issues of poverty, low HDI ranking, and susceptibility to natural disasters. One solution is to increase investment in education. This can be done by increasing the budget for education, and by making education more accessible to all. Another solution is to increase investment in infrastructure. This can be done by building more roads, bridges, and schools. Lastly, the Philippines needs to strengthen its disaster preparedness and response capabilities. This can be done by increasing the budget for disaster preparedness and by training more people in disaster response.

SARS-CoV-2—the causative agent of COVID-19—is a member of the highly diverse coronavirus family, named for the crown-like spikes present on their surface.1-3 Like other members of this virus family, SARS-CoV-2 is an enveloped, positive-sense, single-stranded RNA virus.1

There are several key stages in the SARS-CoV-2 life cycle, including binding, entry, proteolysis, RNA replication, and exit.1,3,4 Understanding the full viral life cycle may shed light on current therapies and potential therapeutic targets at each stage.2,3 Monoclonal antibodies can be used to interfere with SARS-CoV-2 entry into the host cell, and RNA replication may be decreased through use of nucleoside analogs. Halting viral replication by inhibiting proteolysis by a protease inhibitor is another area of interest.2-4

Binding and Entry
