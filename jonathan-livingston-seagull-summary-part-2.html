Answer:

John Livingston Seagull is often referred to as a "fable." A fable is a short story that conveys a moral and uses some key features. The most important feature of a fable is the moral of the story. In John Livingston Seagull, the moral is to go against the crowd and to follow your own desires despite what others think.

Typically, characters are animals in fables, whose characteristics and behaviors are then used by the author to tell us something about human nature. For example, Jonathan's desire to fly rather than eat represents the human desire to be individualistic. Fables often also have a rural, outdoor setting. This is true of Jonathan Livingston Seagull, which is set on a beautiful coast: "It was morning, and the new sun sparkled gold across the ripples of a gentle sea."

Si David Livingstone.

Marahil siya ang isa sa mga pinakabantog na mga pambansang bayani ng hulihan ng ika-19 na daantaon sa Britanyang Victoriano. Si Livingstone ay nagkaroon ng isang mitikong katayuan, na naganap sa ilang mga magkakaugnay na mga antas: bilang isang martir na misyonerong Protestante, bilang isang tao na dumanas ng kuwentong nakapagbibigay ng inspirasyon dahil sa pagiging mahirap niya na naging mayaman, bilang isang imbestigador na makaagham at eksplorador, bilang isang repormador na imperyal, bilang isang krusador na laban sa pang-aalipin, at bilang tagapagtaguyod ng imperyong pangkomersiyo.

17. TASK: Look for the summary of the story "Gulliver's Travels" by Jonathan Swift. Create a plot diagram that will highlight the important events that happened in the story.​


Answer:

"Gulliver's Travels" by Jonathan Swift is a classic novel first published in 1726. The story follows the journey of Lemuel Gulliver, a ship surgeon, who sets sail on four different voyages that take him to strange and wondrous lands.

Explanation:

Plot Diagram:

Exposition:

Lemuel Gulliver sets sail as a ship surgeon on the Antelope. His ship is wrecked, and he washes ashore in Lilliput.

Rising Action:

Gulliver finds himself in a land inhabited by tiny people, the Lilliputians, who are at war with their neighboring island. Gulliver helps the Lilliputians win the war and becomes a hero. Later, he travels to Brobdingnag, a land of giants, where he is exhibited as a curiosity.

Climax:

Gulliver lands in Laputa, an island inhabited by scholars and intellectuals, who are so absorbed in their research that they are unable to function properly in the real world.

Falling Action:

Gulliver travels to the land of the Houyhnhnms, a race of intelligent horses who are ruled by reason and virtue. The Yahoos, a race of savage and brutish human-like creatures, live alongside the Houyhnhnms.

Resolution:

Gulliver returns to England and is disgusted by the behavior of humans, realizing that he has more in common with the rational and virtuous Houyhnhnms than with his fellow humans.

Overall, "Gulliver's Travels" is a satirical work that critiques human behavior and society. Through the lens of Gulliver's travels, Swift offers a commentary on politics, science, and human nature.
