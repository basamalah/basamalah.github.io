5. what weaving technique is used in making a banig


The weaving technique used in making banig is the more subtle tikog.

Explanation:

The tikog stems are first cut to the desired length and then dried in the sun. Some of these sticks are dyed to the desired color and dried again. Then these are flattened before being woven into mats. Weaving is the process of making woven items (fabric) from crossing two sets of threads by inserting the weft threads transversely into the warp threads (warp threads). Prior to weaving, a loom is performed, namely the installation of warp threads parallel to each other on the loom according to the desired width of the fabric.

The loom is used to hold the warp threads while the weft threads are inserted transversely between the warp threads. The criss-cross pattern between the warp and weft threads is called plait. Weaving is a process of weaving warp and weft yarns by taking into account the elements of the fiber (yarn), properties, thread color, weaving method, woven structure and desired expression.

Weaving is made to be used as personal or family property and as a medium of encounter with the Highest. Weaving and or weaving is also an ideal to encourage the realization of social change that starts with oneself (weaver) as well as the social community (tribe). Gedogan is the name of the tool for weaving. Apart from gedogan, there is a set of other tools needed, which include: pajal, thread knurler, undar jantra, harvester, and binoculars.

8. Write the procedure in making the "banig" in chronological order.​


Answer:

Step 1: Harvest Coconut Leaves

Step 2: Start Weaving

Step 3: Folding Extra Length

Step 4: Repeat Folding on Other Side

Explanation:

Step 1:

You can collect the coconut leaves known as fronds from a large branch from the tree. we need about 10 to 12 pieces of these fronds

•Pull away the fronds from the branch

•Trim the frond edges which are used to attach with the branch

•Using a craft knife, remove the stick running at middle of the fronds

•Now you have 2 leaflets from one frond

Step 2:

•Place required number of leaflets side by side on the floor (9, 10 or 11 to the required size)

•Take another leaflet and weave through leaflets on the floor in an above and below pattern

•Continue adding leaflets till the numbers on both sides are equal.

•You can also add more leaflets on both sides to make it larger

Step 3:

•If you look at the partly woven mat at this stage, you will find the extra length of leaves in an above and below position

•First take the leaflets at one side which are below and fold them over the end leaf

•Insert the folded portion through other leaflets in perpendicular in the same pattern as above and below

•Trim end of leaflets protruding through. Do this for all four sides

Step 4:

•Now turn over the mat and repeat same we did on the other side, like folding extra length and inserting ends through the leaflets in perpendicular

•Trim all extra length of leaflets and our mat is finished

This mat is 10" X 10" in size and can be used as a table mat. It lasts for a long time even after drying

23. what weaving technique is used in making a banig​


A baníg is a handwoven mat usually used in East Asia and the Philippines for sleeping and sitting. This type of mat is traditionally made in the Philippines.

Technically, it is not a textile. Depending on the region of the Philippines, the mat is made of buri (palm), pandanus or sea grass leaves. The leaves are dried, usually dyed, then cut into strips and woven into mats, which may be plain or intricate.

The Samal of Sulu usually make their mats out of buri leaves. Mats from Basey, Samar use tikog leaves which are dyed in strong colours to make beautiful, unique designs. Banig mats from Bukidnon are made from sodsod grass, a ribless reed endemic to the area.
