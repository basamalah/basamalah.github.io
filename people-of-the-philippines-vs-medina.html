13. Asian literature vs Philippines literature


One cannot make a direct comparison between Asian and Philippine literature as the latter is actually an example of Asian literature, specifically Southeast Asian literature. We can, however, discuss common themes where famous Philippine literary works were rooted from. 
During the pre-colonial period, stories were passed on by word of mouth, which eventually became written works. Nature and beauty were the focal points of these stories, and these connect its audience to the world of gods and goddesses. Think of the famous Philippine epics and legends, for example, where superhuman beings overpower the mortals.
Then came the Spaniards who introduced Christianity to Filipinos. In the beginning, most of the published works were religious and secular articles used to propagate Catholicism and filial piety. Books about morality and etiquette emerged. Years later, narratives and essays about politics and social structure surfaced. Nationalism then became the central theme in Philippine literature.
Post-colonial literature surfaced after the war. These mostly talked about human experiences while the country was under siege--being marginalized, tortured, and how the war completely changed peoples' lives. These works continued to circulate years after the Martial Law has ended. These literary works carried so much angst just so the writer can strike back at the so-called center of power.

G.R. No. L-14752 is a Philippine Supreme Court case decided on April 30, 1963, involving a land dispute between the heirs of Juan Carino and the Philippine government.

The case originated from the fact that the government filed an action in the Court of First Instance of Baguio to recover a parcel of land from the Carino family. The land, which was located in Benguet, had been classified as public land under the Philippine Bill of 1902, and the government claimed that it was never privately owned by the Carino family.

The Carino family, on the other hand, claimed that they had been in possession of the land for over 30 years and had inherited it from their ancestor, Juan Carino, who had acquired it through occupation and cultivation. They argued that their possession of the land had ripened into ownership, and that the government's claim to the land was barred by the statute of limitations.

The Court of First Instance ruled in favor of the government, stating that the land was indeed public land and that the Carino family had not acquired ownership over it. The Carino family appealed the decision to the Supreme Court.

In its decision, the Supreme Court affirmed the ruling of the lower court and held that the land in question was public land. The Court stated that the Philippine Bill of 1902 had declared all lands of the public domain to be public lands, and that the burden of proving that the land in question had been privately owned by the Carino family was on them. The Court further held that the Carino family's possession of the land was not sufficient to establish ownership, as the land had never been formally titled in their name.

The Court also rejected the Carino family's argument that their possession of the land had ripened into ownership, as they had not fulfilled the requirements for acquiring ownership through prescription under the Spanish Mortgage Law. The Court stated that possession must be open, continuous, exclusive, and notorious for a period of 30 years in order for it to ripen into ownership.

Therefore, the Supreme Court affirmed the lower court's decision in favor of the government and ordered the Carino family to vacate the land.

27. carino vs people of the Philippines summary​


Answer:

Carino vs People of the Philippines, also known as the "non-delegation doctrine" case, was a landmark decision by the Supreme Court of the Philippines in 1922. The case involved the issue of whether the Philippine Legislature had the power to delegate its legislative authority to administrative bodies.

The Court ruled that the delegation of legislative power to administrative bodies was unconstitutional because it violated the principle of separation of powers enshrined in the Philippine Constitution. The Court stated that the power to make laws is a function of the legislature and cannot be delegated to other bodies.

The case established the non-delegation doctrine in Philippine jurisprudence, which remains a fundamental principle of constitutional law in the country. It serves as a safeguard against excessive delegation of legislative power and ensures that the separation of powers is maintained.
