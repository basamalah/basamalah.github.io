Scientists of the 18th and early 19th centuries studied compounds obtained from plants and animals and labeled them organic because they were isolated from “organized” (living) systems. Compounds isolated from nonliving systems, such as rocks and ores, the atmosphere, and the oceans, were labeled inorganic. For many years, scientists thought organic compounds could be made by only living organisms because they possessed a vital force found only in living systems. The vital force theory began to decline in 1828, when the German chemist Friedrich Wöhler synthesized urea from inorganic starting materials. He reacted silver cyanate (AgOCN) and ammonium chloride (NH4Cl), expecting to get ammonium cyanate (NH4OCN). What he expected is described by the following equation.

AgOCN + NH4Cl → AgCl + NH4OCN

Instead, he found the product to be urea (NH2CONH2), a well-known organic material readily isolated from urine. This result led to a series of experiments in which a wide variety of organic compounds were made from inorganic starting materials. The vital force theory gradually went away as chemists learned that they could make many organic compounds in the laboratory.

Today organic chemistry is the study of the chemistry of the carbon compounds, and inorganic chemistry is the study of the chemistry of all other elements. It may seem strange that we divide chemistry into two branches—one that considers compounds of only one element and one that covers the 100-plus remaining elements. However, this division seems more reasonable when we consider that of tens of millions of compounds that have been characterized, the overwhelming majority are carbon compounds.

Genetic Disorders

Genetic disorders are diseases caused by mutations, or changes in DNA.

These mutations can occur due to a person's inherited genes, errors during DNA replication, or environmental factors.

Three main categories of genetic disorders are single-gene, chromosomal,

and multifactorial disorders.

Huntington's disease is an example of a single-gene disorder, a defect in one particular gene.

Down syndrome, or trisomy 21, is an example of a chromosomal disorder, a defect in chromosome number or structure.

Lung cancer is an example of a multifactorial disorder, a defect in multiple genes combined with environmental factors like pollution or radiation.

30. Acetone, also known as propanone, is a common solvent used in chemical industries. It is the smallest ketone, which is colorless, flammable liquid. The molecular formula of acetone is C3H6O. Solve for the percentage of C, H, and O. ​


Hint:

The percent composition of a compound is calculated with the molecular formula:

[tex]\sf mass\;\%=\dfrac{mass}{total\;mass}\times 100\%[/tex]

Solution:

First we need to identify the molar mass of each element.

[tex]\sf C=3 \times \dfrac{12.01\; g}{1\; mol}=36.03\;g/mol[/tex]

[tex]\sf H=6 \times \dfrac{1.008\; g}{1\; mol}=6.048\;g/mol[/tex]

[tex]\sf O=1 \times \dfrac{16\; g}{1\; mol}=16\;g/mol[/tex]

The molar mass of the compound is

[tex]\sf (36.03+6.048+16)\;g/mol=58.078\;g/mol[/tex]

The following percentage compositions are:

[tex]\sf C\;mass\;\%=\dfrac{36.03\;g/mol}{58.078\;g/mol}\times 100\%=\boxed{\sf 62.04\%}[/tex]

[tex]\sf H\;mass\;\%=\dfrac{6.048\;g/mol}{58.078\;g/mol}\times 100\%=\boxed{\sf 10.41\%}[/tex]

[tex]\sf O\;mass\;\%=\dfrac{16\;g/mol}{58.078\;g/mol}\times 100\%=\boxed{\sf 27.55\%}[/tex]

Therefore, the percentage composition of acetone can be described as 62.04% carbon, 10.41% hydrogen and 27.55% oxygen.
