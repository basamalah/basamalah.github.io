16. Who are the 3 gods of Hinduism?Why the 3 gods of Hinduism is special?How the 3 gods of Hinduism to be a same?​


In Hinduism, there are many gods and goddesses, but the three most prominent and widely worshipped deities are Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva, collectively known as the Trimurti.

Brahma - The Creator: Brahma is the god of creation in Hinduism. He is usually depicted with four heads, each facing a different direction, and four arms. Brahma is believed to have created the universe and all living beings.

Vishnu - The Preserver: Vishnu is the god of preservation in Hinduism. He is often depicted with blue skin and four arms, holding various objects such as a conch shell, a discus, and a mace. Vishnu is believed to have appeared on earth in various forms or avatars, including as Rama and Krishna, to restore balance and order to the world.

Shiva - The Destroyer: Shiva is the god of destruction and transformation in Hinduism. He is usually depicted with a third eye on his forehead and a serpent around his neck. Shiva is also known as the god of meditation and is associated with asceticism and self-discipline.

The Trimurti is considered special in Hinduism because they represent the three fundamental aspects of existence: creation, preservation, and destruction. They are believed to work together in harmony to maintain the balance of the universe.

Although the Trimurti are distinct deities with their own attributes and stories, they are also considered to be aspects of the same ultimate reality, Brahman. This means that while they may have different roles and functions, they are ultimately manifestations of the same divine essence. The Trimurti represents the unity and interconnectedness of all things in Hinduism.

Hinduism, major world religion originating on the Indian subcontinent and comprising several and varied systems of philosophy, belief, and ritual. Although the name Hinduism is relatively new, having been coined by British writers in the first decades of the 19th century, it refers to a rich cumulative tradition of texts and practices, some of which date to the 2nd millennium BCE or possibly earlier. If the Indus valley civilization (3rd–2nd millennium BCE) was the earliest source of these traditions, as some scholars hold, then Hinduism is the oldest living religion on Earth. Its many sacred texts in Sanskrit and vernacular languages served as a vehicle for spreading the religion to other parts of the world, though ritual and the visual and performing arts also played a significant role in its transmission. From about the 4th century CE, Hinduism had a dominant presence in Southeast Asia, one that would last for more than 1,000 years.
