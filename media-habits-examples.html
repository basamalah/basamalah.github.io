6. B. I KNOW IT. Answer the following questions in paragraph form. (10 points each) 1. In your opinion, what makes an individual literate in media and information? 2. How is critical thinking related to media and information literacy? 3. Do you consider yourself a media and information literate individual? Why or why not? 4. What activities/habits do you practice which illustrate media and information literacy? Give at least three examples. ​


Answer:

1. In my opinion, an individual is literate in media and information if they possess the skills and knowledge necessary to access, evaluate, and use various forms of media and information effectively. This includes the ability to identify credible sources, critically analyze information, and understand the ways in which media and information are produced and distributed. A literate individual in media and information should also be able to use various technologies and platforms to create and share media and information in a responsible and ethical manner. Ultimately, media and information literacy enables individuals to make informed decisions and participate fully in a rapidly evolving and complex global society

2. Critical thinking is an essential component of media and information literacy as it enables individuals to evaluate the credibility and reliability of information sources. It allows individuals to analyze and interpret media messages in a thoughtful and informed manner. Through critical thinking, individuals can recognize biases and misinformation, make informed decisions, and communicate effectively in a complex media environment.

3. As an artificial intelligence language model, I cannot consider myself a media and information literate individual in the traditional sense. However, I am programmed to provide accurate and reliable information and to assist users in accessing and understanding various forms of media and information. I am designed to support and enhance the media and information literacy of individuals by providing them with relevant and useful information, and by promoting critical thinking and digital literacy skills.

4. Here are the three examples:

• Verifying sources: Checking the credibility and reliability of sources before accepting them as true. This can involve fact-checking, cross-referencing, and analyzing multiple sources to evaluate the accuracy and biases of the information.

• Critical analysis: Evaluating media messages and information sources through a critical lens to identify potential biases, misinformation, or manipulation. This can involve questioning assumptions, analyzing evidence, and considering multiple perspectives.

• Ethical engagement: Practicing responsible and ethical behavior when engaging with media and information. This can involve respecting intellectual property rights, protecting personal information, and avoiding the spread of false or harmful information.

14. what activities /habits do you practice which illustrate media and information literacy? give at lease 3 example


What activities or habits do you practice which illustrate media and information literacy? As the centennial and the millennial would say it... “E.z.”


Media and information literacy - or instead, “Information and Media Literacy” with an abbreviation “IML” - empowers us, human beings, to interpret and make cognizant and informed judgments as users of media and information. One of its goals is to turn us into skilful creators and producers of information and media communications.


This media and information literacy promotes problem solving that approaches critical thinking skills. Because of it, we ask questions and seek solutions, find more information, form opinions and views, evaluate our sources as well as make decisions. Being media and information literate means being able to analyze, evaluate, and access data or information that we always receive and seek through different media platforms. Also, being literate means being able to communicate and use the improving technology tools effectively. Thus, it means we can and must think critically and express ourselves confidently.



Here are the three (3) examples of activities that practice media and information literacy:


1. Think Twice - Before I post anything on my social media accounts (Instagram, Facebook, Twitter, Tumblr, etc.), I make sure I thought about it. Thinking about what to post is self-awareness. It is like checking yourself up if your brain is updating like your iOS and android version or just stagnant and empty. Is my statement offensive, homophobic, racist, sexist, misogynist, or something my grandma won’t like to read on her FB timeline? Are these the right things to say? Am I "informed" enough?  Lots of questions in my head before hitting any button 'coz I must think twice before I click!  (Plus, proofread after posting. Watch out for the typos, too.)


2. When in Doubt, Google It! – Browser is one of the best computer-age inventions! I can search the correct spelling of a word and even how to pronounce it, I can download a book that I need for my studies (not the pirated ones, of course), I can buy and sell stuff, and I can even learn how to cook Korean cuisines just because of I can look them all up on browsers!


3. Social Issues > Selfies (read this as: social issues are greater than selfies) – I use media, information literacy, and technology to keep myself up to date on what is happening around the world especially in my own country. Some people say that internet keeps people away from reality… well, these “some people” are perhaps doing and using the technology wrong. My Twitter is my newspaper, Facebook or Messenger is the easiest way to reach people, Instagram is the art galleries / museums, Tumblr and other websites are my books, magazines, and zines. Of course, no to fake news, too! I must fact-check first before disseminating or sharing news and articles. Again, I use the cyberspace or internet as a window to educate myself, reach and help people, as well as cherish and appreciate life. I do not just post selfies… I am also trying my best to be humane, compassionate and not to be selfish.


Those are my examples of habits which illustrate media and information literacy. I hope you won’t just copy this answer but reflect, too, if you’re using the media and technology right. So the next time someone asks you about what activities do you practice which illustrate media and information literacy, you won’t hesitate to answer.
