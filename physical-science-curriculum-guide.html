6. how would you use the curriculum guide for science.​


The way to use the curriculum for science must be adapted to the needs of learning science.

In general, science aims to be able to reveal facts in support of knowledge. In essence, the scope of science includes everything that can be done by the five senses, namely sight, hearing, touch, and taste. Therefore, using the curriculum for science can be done with a scientific approach, namely through the stages of observing, asking, trying, reasoning, and communicating. First, observation activities in using the curriculum for science are by presenting problems that are relevant to everyday life, for example, presenting the problem of environmental pollution. The purpose of observing is to be able to provide a stimulus to students. Second, the questioning activity. Students are required to be able to ask questions to be more active because in the application of the curriculum for science, it cannot be separated from questioning activities as an effort to find out more in-depth science problem solving. Third, try activities. In the science curriculum, self-practice is the most important thing, because science will be closely related to research activities. Fourth, that is reasoning. When students are able to carry out their own experiments, they can think scientifically. Through this reasoning activity, which will become the capital for students in analyzing science problems. The last, after being able to reason, using the curriculum for science is done by providing opportunities for students to communicate the results of their experiments or findings. Through this communication, science can continue to be developed from generation to generation.

7. What is the need of Science Curriculum in Early Childhood Curriculum ?​


Answer:

There is a growing understanding and recognition of the power of children’s early thinking and learning as well as a belief that science may be a particularly important domain in early childhood, serving not only to build a basis for future scientific understanding but also to build important skills and attitudes for learning. This paper addresses the question of what the nature of science teaching and learning in the early childhood classroom should be. It proposes four basic ideas: (1) doing science is a natural and critical part of children’s early learning; (2) children’s curiosity about the natural world is a powerful catalyst for their work and play; (3) with the appropriate guidance, this natural curiosity and need to make sense of the world become the foundation for beginning to use skills of inquiry to explore basic phenomena and materials of the world surrounding children; and (4) this early science exploration can be a rich context in which children can use and develop other important skills, including working with one another, basic large- and small-motor control, language, and early mathematical understanding. The paper describes a structure for learning through inquiry and criteria for the selection of appropriate content for young children. It concludes with a discussion of implications for the classroom, focusing on child-centered curriculum, the role of materials, the use of time and space, the key role of discussion and representation, and the teacher’s role.

23. Research on the legality of physical education in the curriculum...​


Physical education (PE) is generally considered to be a mandatory part of the curriculum in most public schools in the United States. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) require that students with disabilities be provided with appropriate physical education. However, some states have different requirements for PE. In some states, homeschooled students are not required to participate in physical education. Additionally, there are some private schools that do not include PE in their curriculum. In general, physical education is considered an important part of a well-rounded education, and its inclusion in the curriculum is supported by a wide range of organizations, including the National Association for Sport and Physical Education (NASPE) and the American Heart Association (AHA).
