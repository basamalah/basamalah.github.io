10. A. Stellar Nucleosynthesis

The nucleosynthesis of chemical elements during supernova explosions is known as supernova nucleosynthesis.

When helium, carbon, oxygen, and silicon are burned sequentially in sufficiently massive stars, nucleosynthesis—the fusing of lighter elements into heavier ones—occurs. In this process, the remains of one nuclear fuel are compressed and used as fuel for the next burning stage. The term "burning" here refers to nuclear fusion rather than a chemical process.

The production of chemical elements within stars through nuclear fusion reactions is known as stellar nucleosynthesis. Since the initial generation of hydrogen, helium, and lithium during the Big Bang, stellar nucleosynthesis has taken place. It produces precise estimations of the measured abundances of the elements as a predictive theory. It explains why some elements and their isotopes are substantially more numerous than others as well as why observed abundances of elements fluctuate throughout time.

Over the course of their lifetimes, stars change due to variations in their chemical makeup (the abundance of their component elements), first by burning hydrogen (main sequence stars), then helium (horizontal branch stars), and ultimately burning higher elements.

Explanation:

The metallic character of an element is determined by its ability to lose electrons and form positive ions. Metals tend to have low electronegativity and ionization energy, as well as high electron mobility and metallic bonding. In general, metallic character increases as you move down and to the left of the periodic table.

Among the given options, Tin (Sn) is located to the left of Antimony (Sb), Bismuth (Bi), and Lead (Pb) in the periodic table, and it is in the 14th group. Tin has a lower electronegativity and ionization energy than the other elements in the options. Therefore, it has a higher metallic character than Antimony, Bismuth, and Lead.
