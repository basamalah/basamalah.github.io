Additionally, preserving and promoting mother tongue languages can help to maintain cultural heritage and identity. Therefore, addressing the challenges in implementing mother tongue-multilingual education can lead to improved education and better life outcomes for children and communities.

Additionally, using the mother tongue as the primary language of instruction can help to preserve linguistic and cultural diversity. Furthermore, it can help to promote social inclusion and reduce the marginalization of certain linguistic groups. It also has been observed that when students are taught in their mother tongue, it creates a more positive and welcoming learning environment which in turn leads to better engagement and participation from students.

Language policies are articulated in most multilingual postcolonial countries: they establish or affirm

the national language(s), often including any ex-colonial language(s) embedded in the country’s

institutions, and selected regional or local languages that are spoken as regional lingua francas

governments tend to foreground the national languages ahead of regional and local languages,
