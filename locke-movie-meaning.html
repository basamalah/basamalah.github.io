Answer:

Bleeding Steel is a work of fiction and john locke philosophy is a branch of real-world philosophy. However, some possible connections that could be drawn between the two include the idea of the social contract and the importance of individual rights and freedoms. In Bleeding Steel, the character Jackie Chan portrays a man who is fighting for his life and the lives of others in a dystopian future world. This could be seen as a metaphor for the individual fighting against an oppressive government or society. In locke's philosophy, the individual has a natural right to life, liberty, and property. According to John Locke, psychological continuity determines one's identity. He believed that the foundation of one's identity, or "self," was consciousness, namely recollection, rather than the substance of either the soul or the body.

In his latest dramatic science fiction film  Origins, writer/director Mike Cahill dives into issues of love, relationships, science, and the afterlife with eerily stunning effects.

Michael Pitt plays Ian Gray, a Ph.D. candidate in molecular biology who focuses especially on the eye. The human eye has been used as proof of intelligent design ever since Darwin's time because science cannot account for an object with the technical complexity of the human eye.

Ian has been conducting a study on the evolution of the eyeball via many animals in an effort to demonstrate that the eye can be explained scientifically.

In Origins, Cahill analyzes what it means to be alive and, ultimately, what the affirmation or rejection of an afterlife would entail for our species as a whole. It is only natural for us to wonder why we are here. All of this is revealed through the enthralling romance between Ian and Sofi, which strikes the perfect mix between intimacy and passion.

Cahill also pushes the plot in unexpected directions that only serve to deepen the issues and characters.  Origins is an excellent introduction into the sci-fi genre and a frightening, poignant work that delivers thought-provoking concepts with an emotional gut punch.

According to Locke, the self's conscious awareness of its existence as a thinking, reasoning, and reflecting identity is what makes up the self.
