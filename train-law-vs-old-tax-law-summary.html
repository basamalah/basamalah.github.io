2. differentiate the old tax vs the train law


Answer:

The old tax law and the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN) law are two different tax systems in the Philippines. Here are some key differences between the two:

Tax rates: Under the old tax system, there were only three tax brackets with a maximum tax rate of 32%. With the TRAIN law, there are now seven tax brackets with lower tax rates, with the highest rate at 35%.

Exemptions and deductions: The TRAIN law increased the tax-exempt amount for individuals to Php 250,000 and removed the personal exemption. It also reduced the allowable itemized deductions for employees, while expanding the list of allowable deductions for self-employed individuals.

Value-added tax (VAT): The old tax law had a fixed VAT rate of 12%, while the TRAIN law exempts some goods and services from VAT and retains the 12% rate for other items.

Excise tax: The TRAIN law imposes higher excise taxes on certain products such as tobacco and sugary drinks.

Estate and donor's tax: The TRAIN law increased the threshold for estate tax and donor's tax to Php 5 million.

Overall, the TRAIN law aims to simplify the tax system, lower the tax burden for low-income earners, and increase revenue for government programs.

3. differentiate the old tax vs the train law


Answer:

The "old tax" and the "TRAIN Law" refer to two different tax systems in the Philippines.

The old tax system refers to the National Internal Revenue Code (NIRC) of 1997, which was the prevailing tax law before the enactment of the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN) Law in 2017.

Here are some key differences between the old tax and the TRAIN Law:

Income tax rates: The TRAIN Law adjusted the tax brackets and lowered the tax rates for individual taxpayers. For example, individuals earning up to PHP 250,000 annually are now exempt from paying income tax, compared to the previous exemption limit of PHP 50,000.

Excise taxes: The TRAIN Law increased the excise taxes on various goods and services, such as petroleum products, sugary beverages, and cars. This was intended to generate more revenue for the government and discourage the consumption of certain products.

Estate tax: The TRAIN Law increased the threshold for estate tax from PHP 2 million to PHP 5 million, which means that smaller estates are no longer subject to this tax.

Value-added tax (VAT): The VAT system remained largely the same under the TRAIN Law, but certain exemptions were removed, such as the exemption for the sale of low-cost housing and the VAT-free status of cooperatives.

Overall, the TRAIN Law was designed to make the tax system more progressive by reducing the tax burden on low- to middle-income earners and increasing the taxes on luxury goods and services. However, it also drew criticism for potentially increasing the cost of living for some sectors of the population.

Tax reform is the process of changing the way taxes are collected or managed by the government and is usually undertaken to improve tax administration or to provide economic or social benefits.[1] Tax reform can include reducing the level of taxation of all people by the government, making the tax system more progressive or less progressive, or simplifying the tax system and making the system more understandable or more accountable.

Numerous organizations have been set up to reform tax systems worldwide, often with the intent to reform income taxes or value added taxes into something considered more economically liberal. Other reforms propose tax systems that attempt to deal with externalities. Such reforms are sometimes proposed to be revenue-neutral, for example in revenue neutrality of the FairTax, meaning they ought not result in more tax or less being collected.[2] Georgism claims that various forms of land tax can both deal with externalities and improve productivity.
