However, just 10 days before that, the Supreme Court of the Philippines issued a status quo ante (or restraining) order against the RH Law for 120 days,2 during which period it would review the petitions challenging the new law itself; oral arguments before the Supreme Court had been set to begin on June 18, 2013, or six months since the enactment by Congress.

One of the conflicts that occurred in the history of the Philippines is the Moro conflict.

The Moro conflict is an ongoing rebellion on the island of Mindanao, Philippines.

In 1969, political tensions and open fighting developed between the Philippine Government and Moro Muslim rebel groups. The Moro rebellion led to the Jabidah massacre, which killed 60 Filipino Muslim commanders over a planned operation to reclaim the eastern Malaysian state of Sabah.

In response, University of the Philippines professor Nur Misuari founded the Moro National Liberation Front (FPNM), an armed rebel group aiming to establish an independent Mindanao. In the following years, FPNM split into several different groups which included the Moro Islamic Liberation Front, which wanted to establish an Islamic state in the Philippines.

The Moro rebellion is rooted in the long history of rebellion by the Bangsamoro people against foreign rulers, beginning with the American annexation of the Philippines in 1899. Since then, the Moro rebellion has shifted against the Philippine government.

There are several advantages to learning about the controversies in Philippine history. Some of these advantages include:

1. Critical thinking skills: Studying controversies in Philippine history requires critical thinking skills to examine and analyze different perspectives, interpretations, and evidence. It encourages students to question and evaluate historical events, sources, and narratives, helping them develop their critical thinking skills, which are valuable in various aspects of life.

2. Historical awareness: Understanding controversies in Philippine history provides a more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of the country's past. It allows students to appreciate the complexity and diversity of historical events, characters, and contexts, going beyond simplistic or one-sided views of history. It promotes historical awareness and a deeper appreciation of the country's heritage and identity.

3. Empathy and perspective-taking: Learning about controversies in Philippine history exposes students to different viewpoints, ideologies, and interests. It helps them develop empathy and perspective-taking skills by understanding the motivations, beliefs, and experiences of different historical actors. This can foster tolerance, open-mindedness, and an appreciation for diversity in society.

4. Civic engagement: Studying controversies in Philippine history can inspire civic engagement and active citizenship. It encourages students to be critical and informed members of society who are aware of their rights and responsibilities. It can motivate them to participate in social, cultural, and political issues, and to make informed decisions as engaged citizens.

5. Historical lessons for the present: Controversies in Philippine history often have contemporary relevance. By examining historical controversies, students can draw connections to current issues and challenges in society, such as social inequality, human rights, governance, and national identity. It provides an opportunity to learn from history and apply those lessons to the present, fostering critical thinking and active engagement in current affairs.
