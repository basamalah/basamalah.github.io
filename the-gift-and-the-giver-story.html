Answer:

1. The statement "Love the giver, not the gift" suggests that the act of giving, rather than the physical gift itself, is the most important aspect of gift-giving. This means that the intention and effort behind the gift, rather than the gift itself, should be the focus of attention and appreciation. By focusing on the giver and their intentions, we can show gratitude and appreciation for the thought and effort that went into the gift.

2. The statement "Be thankful for everything" suggests that it is important to have an attitude of gratitude and to appreciate everything that we have, no matter how small or insignificant it may seem. This means being grateful for the people, experiences, and opportunities that we have in our lives, and recognizing the value and importance of these things. By being thankful for everything, we can cultivate a positive attitude and a sense of contentment and appreciation for the good things in our lives.

Answer:

Grace (gratia, Charis), in general, is a supernatural gift of God to intellectual creatures (men, angels) for their eternal salvation, whether the latter be furthered and attained through salutary acts or a state of holiness. Eternal salvation itself consists in heavenly bliss resulting from the intuitive knowledge of the Triune God, who to the one not endowed with grace "inhabiteth light inaccessible" (1 Timothy 6:16). Christian grace is a fundamental idea of the Christian religion, the pillar on which, by a special ordination of God, the majestic edifice of Christianity rests in its entirety. Among the three fundamental ideas — sin, redemption, and grace — grace plays the part of the means, indispensable and Divinely ordained, to effect the redemption from sin through Christ and to lead men to their eternal destiny in heaven.

Before the Council of Trent, the Schoolmen seldom used the term gratia actualis, preferring auxilium speciale, motio divina, and similar designations; nor did they formally distinguish actual grace from sanctifying grace. But, in consequence of modern controversies regarding grace, it has become usual and necessary in theology to draw a sharper distinction between the transient help to act (actual grace) and the permanent state of grace (sanctifying grace).
