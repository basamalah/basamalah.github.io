The Cry of Balintawak and the Cry of Pugadlawin are two significant events in Philippine history that marked the beginning of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Here are some comparisons and contrasts between the two:

- Both events were sparked by the desire of Filipinos to fight for their independence from Spanish colonial rule.

- Both events were led by Filipino revolutionaries who were inspired by the ideals of the Propaganda Movement and the injustices of Spanish colonial rule.

- Both events involved the raising of a Philippine flag as a symbol of Filipino nationalism and resistance against Spanish colonialism.

Differences:

- The Cry of Balintawak happened on August 26, 1896, while the Cry of Pugadlawin happened on August 23, 1896, three days earlier.

- The Cry of Balintawak was led by a woman, Gregoria de Jesus, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was led by Andres Bonifacio, the founder of the Katipunan.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a peaceful protest, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a violent uprising that marked the start of the armed struggle against Spanish colonial rule.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a localized event that took place in Caloocan, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a more widespread event that involved several towns in the province of Manila.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a response to the arrest of Filipino nationalist leaders, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a premeditated revolt that aimed to overthrow Spanish colonial rule.

Overall, both the Cry of Balintawak and the Cry of Pugadlawin were significant events that played a crucial role in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonialism. While they had some similarities, they also had some notable differences in terms of their timing, leadership, nature, and goals.

The Cry of Balintawak, also known as the Cry of Pugad Lawin, was a significant event in Philippine history that marked the beginning of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonization. It occurred on August 23, 1896, when a group of Filipino revolutionaries, led by Andres Bonifacio, tore their cedulas or tax certificates and raised the Philippine flag in Balintawak, a town in present-day Caloocan City.

The Cry of Balintawak is important because it symbolized the Filipinos' desire for freedom and independence from Spanish colonial rule. It sparked a wave of revolution and resistance across the country, with Filipinos from different regions joining the cause and fighting for their rights and sovereignty. It also led to the formation of the Katipunan, a secret revolutionary society that aimed to overthrow Spanish colonial rule and establish an independent Philippine republic.

The Filipinos who participated in the Cry of Balintawak desired to end the centuries-long oppression and exploitation under Spanish colonialism. They wanted to reclaim their rights, culture, and identity, and establish a government that would represent their interests and aspirations. They were willing to sacrifice their lives and engage in armed struggle to achieve their goals and gain freedom from foreign domination.

The Cry of Balintawak teaches us the importance of unity, courage, and perseverance in the face of adversity. It reminds us that freedom and independence are not easily won and require sacrifice and struggle. It also highlights the role of ordinary people in shaping history and fighting for their rights and dignity. As we commemorate the Cry of Balintawak, we honor the bravery and patriotism of our ancestors and recommit ourselves to the pursuit of justice, democracy, and national sovereignty.

 In another interview published in the newspaper Bagong Buhay on August 26, 1957, Masangkay changed his narrative stating that the revolution began on August 23, 1896, similar o the assertion of Dr. Pio Valenzuela. The Cry for a Nationwide Revolution

 Nineteenth-century journalists used the phrase “el grite de rebellion’’ or the “the Cry of Rebellion” to describe the momentous events sweeping the Spanish colonies: in Mexico it was the “Cry of Dolores” (September 16, 1810). Brazil the “Cry of Ypiraga” (September &, 1822), and in Cuba the “Cry of Matanza” (February 24, 1895).

 In August 1896, northeast of Manila, Filipinas similarly declared their rebellion against the Spanish historian, who institutionalized the phrased for the Philippines in his 1897 book, La Insurreccion en Filipinas. All these “Cries” were milestones in the several colonial-to-nationalist histories of the world.

In focus: Balintawak

Raging controversy

 If the expression is taken literally –the Cry as the shouting of nationalistic slogans in mass assemblies –then there were scores of such Cries. Some writers refer to a Cry of Montalbanon April 1895, in the Pamitinan Caves where a group of Katipunan members wrote on the cave walls, “Viva la indepencia Filipina! Long before the Katipunan decided to launch a nationwide revolution.

 The historian Teodoro Agoncillo chose to emphasize Bonifacio’s tearing of the cedula (fax receipt) before a crowd of Katipuneros who then broke out in cheers. However, Guardia Manuel Sityar never mentioned in his memories (1896- 1898) the tearing or inspection of the cedula, but did note the pacto de sangre (blood pact) mark on every single Filipino he met in August 1896 on his reconnaissance missions around Balintawak.

Raging controversy

 Some writers consider the first military engagement with the enemy as the defining moment of the Cry. To commemorate this martial event upon his return from exile in Hong Kong, Emilio Aguinaldo commissioned a “Himno de Balintawak” to herald renewed fighting after the failed peace of the pact of Biyak na Bato.
