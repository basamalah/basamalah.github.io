In the Philippines, PHIVOLCS classify volcanoes into three categories – active, inactive, and potentially active. An active volcano should have a historical eruption with accompanying records or witnesses. Alternatively, the geologic deposits should be dated at least 10,000 years old or younger, well within the bounds of the Holocene epoch. Potentially active volcanoes, on the other hand, do not have any historical eruptions or any geologic deposits that is Holocene in age, but solfataric activity is present in the volcano. Inactive volcanoes lack any historical eruptions or geological deposits that are Holocene in age, and they also lack any solfataric activity. 5. what makes mount smith and binintiang malaki differ from the rest of the volcanoes? ​


Smith Volcano and Binintiang Malaki

Smith Volcano and Binintiang Malaki are different from other volcanoes because they are a part of a larger volcanic edifice, and they are actually cones made from pyroclastic materials. We call them pyroclastic cones or cinder cones.

What are cinder cones?

Cinder cones are a type of volcanic cones. They are also known as pyroclastic cones and scoria cones. It forms after a violent volcanic eruption, usually after the lava solidifies in the air and accumulates near the vent. Usually, the height of cinder cones varies from a few meters to hundreds of meters high.

Location of Smith Volcano

Smith Volcano is a cinder cone located near Babuyan Claro volcano, in the island of Babuyan in extreme northern Luzon. This volcano last erupted in 1924, and it is one of the most active volcanoes in the Babuyan Group of Islands.

Location of Binintiang Malaki

Binintiang Malaki is a cinder cone that formed in the northwestern edge of Taal Volcano Island. It is only 263 meters high, but it is known for violent eruptions. The last time an eruption took place in Binintiang Malaki was in 1715. Today, most of Taal Volcano’s activities are centered around the Main Crater.

12. number of deaths when the Bulusan, kanlaon, smith volcano last erupt​


Bulusan, Kanlaon, and Smith Eruptions

So far, the latest eruptions of Bulusan (2017), Kanlaon (2017), and Smith (1924) did not result to any fatalities. The last time someone died from the eruption of these volcanoes was in 1996, when three hikers were killed while climbing Kanlaon volcano. It suddenly had a phreatic eruption which killed the hikers.

What are phreatic eruptions?

Phreatic eruptions are a type of volcanic eruption that is steam-driven. It is dangerous because it can happen anytime. In 1996, three hikers were killed after Kanlaon erupted, spewing extremely hot volcanic materials which immediately led to fatalities.

What makes a volcano deadly?

The extrusion of hot materials from a volcanic vent can burn and kill someone. In addition, related hazards to volcanic eruptions such as ash fall, debris avalanche, pyroclastic density currents, and lahars can also contribute to the number of deaths.

- The geologic setting of Taal, and the variability of eruption sites and magnitudes, generates a diverse range of volcanic hazards, such as base surges, lava flows, ballistic fallout, ash and scoria fallout, toxic gases, acidic flashes from crater lake, lake tsunamis and seiches, lakeshore flooding, earthquakes, ground fissuring and subsidence, landslides and sectoral collapse, turbulent ashflows, and lahars.
