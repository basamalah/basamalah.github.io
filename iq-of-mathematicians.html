9. Deviation IQ scores, sometimes called Wechsler IQ scores, are scores with a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of 15. What percentage of the general population have IQ's larger than 130?​


To answer this question, we need to use the properties of the normal distribution. We know that the mean is 100 and the standard deviation is 15.

We want to find the percentage of the population with IQ scores greater than 130. We can use a standard normal distribution table or a calculator to find the corresponding z-score, which is:

z = (130 - 100) / 15 = 2

This means that an IQ score of 130 corresponds to a z-score of 2. We can then find the percentage of the population with a z-score greater than 2 using a standard normal distribution table or a calculator.

Looking up the value in the standard normal distribution table, we find that the percentage of the population with a z-score greater than 2 is approximately 2.28%. Therefore, we can conclude that approximately 2.28% of the general population have IQ scores larger than 130.

Step-by-step explanation:

A. To find the proportion of people in the general population with IQs between 90 to 110, we need to standardize the IQ scores using the formula:

z = (x - μ) / σ

where:

x = the IQ score

μ = the mean IQ score (100)

σ = the standard deviation (16)

For x = 90:

z = (90 - 100) / 16 = -0.625

For x = 110:

z = (110 - 100) / 16 = 0.625

Using a standard normal distribution table or calculator, we can find the proportion of people with IQs between -0.625 and 0.625:

P(-0.625 < z < 0.625) = 0.4129 - 0.2334 = 0.1795

Therefore, about 17.95% of the general population have IQs between 90 to 110.

B. To qualify for MENSA membership, one needs to have an IQ in the top 2%. We can find the IQ score that corresponds to the top 2% by using the inverse normal distribution (z-score) table.

The z-score that corresponds to the top 2% is approximately 2.05. Therefore, we can solve for x in the following equation:

2.05 = (x - 100) / 16

x - 100 = 2.05 * 16

x = 132.8

So, the lowest IQ score in the SBIS that would qualify for MENSA membership is approximately 133.

About Deductive reasoning

Deductive reasoning is a logical conclusion based on existing premises.

Premises here are assumptions, thoughts, and the basis for conclusions that are considered true. The following is an example of deductively drawing conclusions from premises.

According to deductive reasoning, if the premises provided are true, then the conclusion must be true or valid. So, if the conclusion turns out to be incorrect or invalid, it means that there is something wrong with the premises.

History

The history of deductive reasoning can be traced back to ancient times, when Aristotle worked a lot.

This Greek philosopher is noted for documenting many deductive reasoning in the 4th century BC.

Aristotle is known as the Father of Deductive Reasoning, because he was the first to develop the formal system of reasoning.

He observed that in fact deductive validity can be seen from the structure we have discussed earlier. Here is the most classic example that Aristotle made:

Premise 1: All men are mortal. (All humans can die)

(All humans can die) Premise 2: Socrates is a man. (Socrates is human)

. (Socrates is human) Therefore, Socrates is mortal. (So, Socrates could die)

For the record, mortal has the meaning of mortal, not immortal, and can die
