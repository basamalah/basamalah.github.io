1. acquisition learning hypothesis?​


Answer:

The acquisition–learning hypothesis claims that there is a strict separation between acquisition and learning; Krashen saw acquisition as a purely subconscious process and learning as a conscious process, and claimed that improvement in language ability was only dependent upon acquisition and never on learning.


2. The input hypothesis the most well known of krashen's hypothesis states that for language acquisition (not learning) to advance, there must be comprehensive input to acquire more of the language.​


Answer:

Absolutely, the Input Hypothesis proposed by Stephen Krashen suggests that language acquisition occurs when the learner is exposed to comprehensible input that is slightly beyond their current level of competence, without any explicit focus on language form or structure. This means that in order to improve their language skills, learners need to be provided with input that is understandable, interesting, and relevant to their lives. By being exposed to such input, learners are able to unconsciously absorb the language and gradually internalize its rules and structures, leading to the development of fluency and accuracy over time.

21. What do acquisition and learning lead to?


Explanation:

Acquisition and learning are two related but distinct processes in language acquisition.

Acquisition refers to the subconscious process by which we internalize the rules and patterns of a language through exposure to input. It is a natural, intuitive process that is similar to the way we learn our first language as children. In acquisition, we develop a feel for the language and its structures, without necessarily being able to explain or articulate them explicitly.

Learning, on the other hand, refers to the conscious process of gaining knowledge about a language through study and instruction. It involves a more deliberate effort to understand and apply the rules and patterns of the language, and it typically involves explicit instruction, such as grammar rules and vocabulary lists.

Both acquisition and learning are important for language development and proficiency. Acquisition helps us to develop an intuitive sense of the language and to use it effectively in real-world situations, while learning helps us to better understand the underlying rules and structures of the language and to use it more accurately and effectively in formal contexts such as writing and public speaking.

Together, acquisition and learning lead to the development of language proficiency and fluency, allowing us to communicate effectively and confidently in a variety of social, professional, and personal contexts

30. What does the term learning mean in contrast to acquisition?


In contrast to acquisition, "learning" in second language refers to the process of consciously studying and memorizing language rules, grammar, vocabulary, and other aspects of language, often in a classroom setting.

Unlike acquisition, which occurs through natural exposure and use of the language in real-life situations, learning is a more conscious and deliberate process of acquiring knowledge about a language.

For example, learning a language may involve studying grammar rules, practicing verb conjugations, memorizing vocabulary lists, and taking language tests. While learning can be a valuable tool for acquiring a language, it is often considered less effective than acquisition, as it can be more difficult to apply learned rules and vocabulary to real-life situations and contexts.
