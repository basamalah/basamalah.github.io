10. Anecdotal report about child with disability scribd


SPED 5326.253Robin Bryan

Anecdotal Records

Anecdotal Record: A method of formative assessment in which an unbiasedobserver records information about a student’s behavior after it takes place.These records are used to analyze student progress, and inform instructionaldecisions for differentiation.

Anecdotal Records & Students withDisabilities

Teachers of all students –especially those with disabilities—should useanecdotal records as a method of formative assessment, because theseobservations provide data that helps the teacher organize groups, planinstruction that meets the needs of the individual learner, and give directed,useful feedback to students.After analyzing anecdotal records—unbiased, real time reports of what astudent did either behaviorally or academically—the teacher can identifyindividual needs, which can be used to plan ways to differentiate thatstudent’s instruction rather than providing them with untargeted, genericlessons.Teachers can use anecdotal records to measure student progress in meetingobjectives outlined in an IEP. As a reminder, before observations begin, theteacher notes the objective somewhere on the recording tool. As the teacherobserves the student engaging in an activity or behavior related to the IEPobjective, the observation is recorded.Anecdotal records also provide teachers with a source of data that can bepresented at IEP meetings, discussed at parent-teacher conferences, andadded to student portfolios.

12. showing support to a classmate with physical disability​


Answer:

A child who has a disability or physical limitation is still a person. They enjoy activities and engaging with others their age. They want to be loved and accepted by their peers. They may simply require special accommodation to do so. As your child grows, teach them the importance of giving all students a chance to participate in activities. Encourage them to invite their classmates who have special needs to join in playground games and extracurricular activities when appropriate. If they aren’t sure what’s okay or their friend requires special accommodation, encourage your child to ask a teacher how to best include and assist their friend. It’s better to ask for an explanation than make incorrect assumptions and leave people out

Here are some other attitudes when meeting and interacting with people with disabilities:

Ask before giving help

People with disabilities have varying degrees of independence. Never assume someone with a disability has a low level. If someone looks like they are struggling, ask before helping them. They want to accept help, or they want everyone to be left alone.

People with disabilities are actually more resilient and strong in living independently. Even though we have good intentions, not all people with disabilities like to be pitied. Friends must ask before helping so as not to offend them.

Preserve words and actions

When interacting with people with disabilities, be mindful of the words and actions of friends. Speak and display good manners and don't make fun of them.

Understand their situation

There are people with disabilities who have mental and physical limitations. We have to understand that and provide support if needed.

Treat fairly

People with disabilities are humans too. Friends must treat them fairly and not discriminate against others. So, don't stare at people with disabilities for too long because it will offend them.

Be tolerant

Friends must have a tolerant attitude towards everyone, including people with disabilities. Avoid asking questions about their disability if they don't tell you first. In addition, you must respect the lives of persons with disabilities by not belittling them.

It is better to show mutual respect and not to mention shortcomings. You can only ask when he feels comfortable. Everyone has privacy. Always ask permission to discuss disability issues before asking.
