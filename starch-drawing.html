14. 2. What are the similarities and differences of purified starch and modified starch?​


Answer:

Starch is a long-chain polysaccharide, typically with hundreds or thousands of individual glucose units in each starch molecule. It doesn't taste sweet, since it is too big to easily bind to our sweet taste receptors. The body breaks it down into glucose before absorbing it. Starch comes in a couple of different forms depending on the amount of branching in the starch chain.

Starch is present in large quantities in many plants, especially in parts used for energy storage (like roots, seeds, etc). Corn starch is starch extracted from field corn.

Maltodextrin is a short-chain polysaccharide, typically with under twenty individual glucose units in each maltodextrin molecule. It is small enough to find to our taste receptors, so it can taste sweet -- the shorter the maltodextrin, the sweeter. It looks just like a really, really short starch molecule to the body. In fact, it's highly likely that starch gets broken into short chains like maltodextrin as part of the digestion process, so you could think of it as partially-digested (or artificially digested) starch. It can be made from just about any starch, although it's likely to be made from corn starch.

Maltodextrin is at least 3 glucose-units long. If it were just two, it would be a well-known sugar called "maltose", originally extracted from malted grain (as used in beer/whisky production).

16. how will you classify natural starch from modified starch?​


Answer:

Modified starch is used as a thickening agent. Modified starch, also called starch derivatives, are prepared by physically, enzymatically, or chemically treating native starch to change its properties.

Natural starch is produced commercially from potatoes, corn, wheat, rice and tapioca. Its two main components are polymers of α-glucose, amylopectin and amylose, shown in Fig. 4.1 . The thickening effect of starch is mainly due to the long and branched amylopectin chains.

17. What is the substitute of 1/2 tbsp cornstarch,potato starch rice starch or arrowroot starch?​


Answer:

You should substitute potato starch for cornstarch in a 1:1 ratio. This means if your recipe needs 1 tablespoon of cornstarch, swap that out for 1 tablespoon of potato starch. It's also worth noting that many cooks recommend adding root or tuber starches like potato or arrowroot later in the cooking process.
