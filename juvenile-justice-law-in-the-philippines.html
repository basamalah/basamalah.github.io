14. What is the main purpose of the juvenile justice system?​


Answer/Explanation:

Youth under the age of 18 who are accused of committing a delinquent or criminal act are typically processed through a juvenile justice system1. While similar to that of the adult criminal justice system in many ways—processes include arrest, detainment, petitions, hearings, adjudications, dispositions, placement, probation, and reentry—the juvenile justice process operates according to the premise that youth are fundamentally different from adults, both in terms of level of responsibility and potential for rehabilitation. The primary goals of the juvenile justice system, in addition to maintaining public safety, are skill development, habilitation, rehabilitation, addressing treatment needs, and successful reintegration of youth into the community

One of the key goals of the juvenile justice system is to rehabilitate young offenders and help them become productive members of society. Unlike the adult justice system, which often focuses on punishment and retribution, the juvenile justice system seeks to address the root causes of juvenile delinquency and provide support to help young people overcome their challenges.

The juvenile justice system is based on the principle of parens patriae, which means that the state has a responsibility to act as a parent or guardian for young individuals who are in need of protection or support. This principle recognizes that young people are not yet fully developed and may not have the same level of judgment and decision-making skills as adults.

One of the challenges of the juvenile justice system is determining the appropriate balance between rehabilitation and punishment. While rehabilitation is important, it is also important to hold young offenders accountable for their actions and ensure that they understand the consequences of their behavior.

Another challenge of the juvenile justice system is addressing disparities and biases that may exist in the system. Research has shown that young people from certain racial and ethnic groups, as well as those from low-income backgrounds, are more likely to be involved in the juvenile justice system.
