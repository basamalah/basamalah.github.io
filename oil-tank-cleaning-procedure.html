1. What is Standard Procedures for Tank Cleaning, Purging and Gas free Operation for Oil tankers?


Tank cleaning is the process of removing hydrocarbon vapors, liquids, or residues from cargo tanks onboard a tanker. Tank cleaning may be required for one or more of the following reasons:

To carry clean ballast.To gas-free tanks for internal inspections, repairs, or before entering the dry dock.To remove sediments from tank top plating. This may be required if the vessel is engaged in the repetitive carriage of fuel oil or similar sediment settling cargoes. Although washing may not be necessary for between consecutive voyages, assuming the cargoes are compatible, many Ship Owners have found it prudent to water wash a small group of tanks on a rotation basis between voyages, thus preventing any large accumulation of sediments.

10. Flor used a pail to transfer clean water from a tap to her fish tank while cleaning the fish tank. Initially, she transferred water equivalent to 1/3 of the pail's capacity. When she had completed cleaning the fish tank, she had transferred a total volume of water equivalent to 6 times the pail's capacity. If the difference between the amount of water transferred initially and when she had completed cleaning the fish tank was 765 ml, what was the actual capacity of the pail?


Answer:

Step-by-step explanation:

Let's call the capacity of the pail "x".

According to the problem, initially, she transferred 1/3 of x liters of water to the fish tank. So the amount of water transferred was (1/3)x liters.

When she completed cleaning the fish tank, she transferred a total volume of water equivalent to 6 times the pail's capacity. So the amount of water transferred was 6x liters.

The difference between the amount of water transferred initially and when she had completed cleaning the fish tank was 765 ml or 0.765 liters. This can be expressed as:

6x - (1/3)x = 0.765

Simplifying the left side:

(18/3)x - (1/3)x = 0.765

Multiplying both sides by 3:

17x = 2.295

Dividing both sides by 17:

x = 0.135

Therefore, the actual capacity of the pail was 0.135 liters or 135 ml

24. A rectangular tank contains 3 litres of water and 3 litres of oil. The height of the water level in the tank is 20 cm. Find the height of the oil level. (Consider only the height of the oil layer, not from the bottom of the container)


To solve this problem, we need to use the fact that the total volume of the liquid in the tank is 6 liters, and the height of the water level is 20 cm.

Let's assume that the height of the oil level is h cm. Then, the height of the liquid layer in the tank is (20 + h) cm.

We can set up an equation using the volume of each liquid:

The volume of water = (height of water layer) x (area of tank base)

3 liters = 0.2 m x (area of tank base)

The volume of oil = (height of oil layer) x (area of tank base)

3 liters = h m x (area of tank base)

Since the area of the tank base is the same for both liquids, we can set the two equations equal to each other:

0.2 m x (area of tank base) = h m x (area of tank base)

The area of the tank base cancels out, leaving us with the following:

0.2 m = h

Therefore, the height of the oil level in the tank is 0.2 meters or 20 cm.
