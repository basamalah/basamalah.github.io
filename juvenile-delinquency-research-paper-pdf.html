6. what is meant by juvenile delinquency?​


Answer:

Juvenile delinquency, also known as "juvenile offending", is the act of participating in unlawful behavior as a minor or individual younger than the statutory age of majority.[1] For example, in the United States of America a juvenile delinquent is a person who is typically below 18 (17 in the states of Georgia, New York, Michigan, Missouri, North Carolina, New Hampshire, Texas, and Wisconsin) years of age and commits an act that otherwise would have been charged as a crime if they were an adult.[2] Juvenile crimes can range from status offenses (such as underage smoking/drinking), to property crimes and violent crimes.[3]

Some scholars have found an increase in arrests for youth and have concluded that this may reflect more aggressive criminal justice and zero-tolerance policies rather than changes in youth behavior.Youth violence rates in the United States have dropped to approximately 12% of peak rates in 1993 according to official US government statistics, suggesting that most juvenile offending is non-violent.Many delinquent acts can be attributed to the environmental factors such as family behavior or peer influence.One contributing factor that has gained attention in recent years is the school to prison pipeline. The focus on punitive punishment has been seen to correlate with juvenile delinquency rates.Some have suggested shifting from zero tolerance policies to restorative justice approaches.

Juvenile detention centers, courts and electronic monitoring are common structures of the juvenile legal system.Juvenile courts are in place to address offenses for minors as civil rather than criminal cases in most instances. The frequency of use and structure of these courts in the United States varies by state.Depending on the type and severity of the offense committed, it is possible for people under 18 to be charged and treated as adults.

17. summarize our subject course juvenile delinquency and crime prevention by using terminologies,with a minimum 50terms please include, juvenile delinquency RA 9344 intervention,diversion and CICL ​


Answer:

Juvenile delinquency is a term used to describe criminal acts committed by minors under the age of 18. The Republic Act 9344, also known as the Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act, is a Philippine law that outlines the intervention and rehabilitation of children in conflict with the law (CICL).

Intervention refers to the measures taken to address the root causes of juvenile delinquency, which may include family, school, or community-based interventions. Diversion programs aim to prevent juveniles from entering the formal justice system by providing alternative programs such as counseling, community service, or education.

CICL are children who are alleged as, accused of, or recognized as having committed an offense under Philippine law before the age of 18. The law recognizes that CICL are still in the developmental stage and therefore require special care and protection.

Crime prevention refers to measures taken to prevent crime from occurring. It includes situational crime prevention, which involves changing the physical environment to make it less conducive to criminal activity, and social crime prevention, which aims to address the root causes of crime through social and economic interventions.

Other relevant terms include restorative justice, which seeks to repair harm caused by the offense by involving the victim, offender, and community in the justice process. Recidivism refers to the tendency of offenders to re-offend after being released from prison or completing a sentence. Risk factors refer to characteristics or conditions that increase the likelihood of juvenile delinquency, such as poverty, drug abuse, or lack of parental supervision. Protective factors refer to characteristics or conditions that protect against juvenile delinquency, such as positive family relationships, strong social support, and access to education.


18. What should be done to juvenile delinquents?​


Answer:

The most effective way to prevent juvenile delinquency has indisputably been to assist children and their families early on. Numerous state programs attempt early intervention, and federal funding for community initiatives has allowed independent groups to tackle the problem in new ways. The most effective programs for juvenile delinquency prevention share the following key components:

Education

Model programs have assisted families and children by providing them with information. Some programs inform parents on how to raise healthy children; some teach children about the effects of drugs, gangs, sex, and weapons; and others aim to express to youth the innate worth they and all others have. All of these programs provide youths with the awareness that their actions have consequences. This is particularly important in an era where youth are barraged with sexual and violent images. Educational programs have the underlying intent of encouraging hope and opening up opportunities for young people.

Recreations

One of the immediate benefits of recreational activities is that they fill unsupervised after-school hours. The Department of Education has reported that youths are most likely to commit crimes between 2 p.m. and 8 p.m., with crime rates peaking at 3 p.m. Recreation programs allow youths to connect with other adults and children in the community. Such positive friendships may assist children in later years. Youth programs are designed to fit the personalities and skills of different children and may include sports, dancing, music, rock climbing, drama, karate, bowling, art, and other activities.

30. 3. What are manifestations of juvenile delinquency?​


Juvenile delinquency can manifest in a variety of ways, some of which include:

- Violent behavior, such as fighting or bullying

- Property crimes, such as vandalism or theft

- Drug and alcohol use or abuse

- Gang involvement

- Truancy or dropping out of school

- Running away from home

- Sexual misconduct or assault

- Social withdrawal or isolation

- Defying authority figures, such as parents or teachers

- Repeatedly engaging in risky or harmful behavior.

It is important to note that these behaviors do not necessarily indicate that a young person is delinquent, but they can be warning signs that may require intervention and support.
