9. DRR TOPICS #1-Concept of Disaster #2-Concept of Disaster Risk #3- The Effect of Disaster #4- Disaster from Different Perspective​


1. Concept of Disaster: A disaster is an event that causes widespread damage, destruction, or loss of life. It may be a natural event, such as a hurricane, earthquake, or wildfire, or a man-made event, such as a terrorist attack, industrial accident, or nuclear meltdown. Disasters can have a significant impact on communities, economies, and individuals, and they often require a coordinated response from government agencies, non-governmental organizations, and other stakeholders.

2. Concept of Disaster Risk: Disaster risk refers to the potential for loss, damage, or destruction resulting from a disaster. It is influenced by a range of factors, including physical hazards (such as earthquakes or floods), social vulnerabilities (such as poverty or inadequate infrastructure), and environmental degradation (such as deforestation or climate change). Understanding and managing disaster risk is an important aspect of disaster preparedness and response.

3. The Effect of Disaster: Disasters can have a range of effects, depending on the nature and severity of the event. These effects can include:

Loss of life, injury, and illness

Damage to buildings, infrastructure, and property

Disruption of essential services, such as water and electricity

Displacement of people from their homes and communities

Economic impacts, such as loss of income and increased costs for response and recovery

Psychological and social impacts, such as trauma, stress, and social disruption

The effects of a disaster can be long-lasting and may require ongoing support and assistance for affected individuals and communities.

4. Disaster from Different Perspective: Disasters can be viewed from different perspectives, including:

Environmental perspective: This perspective focuses on the natural hazards that can lead to disasters, such as earthquakes, floods, and hurricanes. It emphasizes the need to understand and manage these hazards to reduce the risk of disasters.

Social perspective: This perspective emphasizes the role of social factors in shaping disaster risk and vulnerability. It highlights the need to address issues such as poverty, inequality, and marginalization in Disaster and Risk

Economic perspective: This perspective focuses on the economic impacts of disasters, such as loss of productivity, damage to infrastructure, and increased costs for response and recovery. It emphasizes the need for economic planning and preparedness to mitigate these impacts.

Technological perspective: This perspective emphasizes the role of technology in disaster management, including early warning systems, communication networks, and emergency response tools. It highlights the need for ongoing innovation and development in disaster technology.

Overall, understanding disasters from multiple perspectives can help to develop more comprehensive and effective strategies for disaster risk management and response.

22. when isnt a natural disaster a disaster ​


Answer:

A natural disaster is defined by the UN as: “the consequences of events triggered by natural hazards that overwhelm local response capacity and seriously affect the social and economic development of a region.” In other words, if an earthquake takes place on an uninhabited island and no one is affected, it is not a natural disaster. In order to be a disaster, people must be affected. Similarly, if flooding takes place in an area where there is adequate preparation, it probably isn’t a natural disaster. If a similar level of flooding, however, takes place in an area where there isn’t preparation and crops are ruined and people are forced to abandon their homes, it then can be a natural disaster. Similarly, heavy rainfalls occur in many parts of the world. Normally, they are not natural disasters, but when the rainfall is heavier than usual and when precautions have not been taken, a natural disaster can result. The rainfall itself is not the disaster, but rather the consequences of the rainfall...
