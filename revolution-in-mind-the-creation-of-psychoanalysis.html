1. Give a difficult concepts of the revolution in mind in the creation of psychoanalysis​


Answer:

Deep into this lucid history of the rise of psychoanalysis, George Makari cites a tribute an acolyte sent Freud for his 60th birthday in 1916. “Beware ... when the great God lets loose a thinker on this planet,” he wrote. “Then all things are at risk. It is as when a conflagration has broken out in a great city, and no man knows what is safe, or where it will end.” Makari doesn’t cite Freud’s somewhat morose response: “Only a funeral oration at the Central Cemetery is normally as beautiful and affectionate.”

Faced with Freud’s tendency to play the heretic to the psychoanalytic orthodoxy he yet defined, followers and detractors alike may have wished their understanding of him carried the certainty of a eulogy.

In “Revolution in Mind,” Makari argues that we’ve been blinded to the cultural reach of psychoanalysis by the magnitude of Freud’s stature and the magnetic pull or repulsion of his personality and theories. In Makari’s view, much contemporary discussion about the relevance of psychoanalysis is based on a false choice: “Freud as everlasting genius, or Freud as relic and fraud.” To Makari, the director of Cornell University’s Institute for the History of Psychiatry, this dichotomy is artificial. Instead, he argues, we should look to the rich, polyphonous context that gave birth to and was influenced by the analytic enterprise: “the culture of Kant; the assumptions of Geisteswissenschaft and a European classical education,” along with “evolutionary biology, positivism and Newtonian physics.”

8. 4.Who is Sigmond Freud and explain it's theory. 4.1.What are the level of minds under psychoanalysis? Explain each briefly. ​


Answer:

Sigmund Freud was an Austrian neurologist and founder of the psychoanalytic school of psychology. He is well known for his theories on the unconscious mind, the role of childhood experiences in shaping personality, and the use of talk therapy to treat mental health disorders.

Freud's theory of psychoanalysis proposes that human behavior is largely influenced by unconscious thoughts, feelings, and memories that are buried deep in the mind. According to Freud, the mind is divided into three levels:

The conscious mind: This is the part of the mind that is currently active and aware of one's surroundings and immediate experiences.

The preconscious mind: This level contains information that is not currently in the conscious mind but can be easily brought to consciousness with a little effort, such as memories or knowledge.

The unconscious mind: This is the largest part of the mind and contains repressed memories, emotions, and desires that are beyond awareness. Freud believed that these unconscious contents could influence conscious behavior in unexpected ways.

Freud's theory proposes that by uncovering and understanding the unconscious influences on behavior, people can gain insight into their own motivations and conflicts, leading to greater self-awareness and healing.

21. What is Psychoanalysis?​


Answer:

a system of psychological theory and therapy which aims to treat mental disorders by investigating the interaction of conscious and unconscious elements in the mind and bringing repressed fears and conflicts into the conscious mind by techniques such as dream interpretation and free association.

Psychoanalysis is defined as a set of psychological theories and therapeutic methods which have their origin in the work and theories of Sigmund Freud. The primary assumption of psychoanalysis is the belief that all people possess unconscious thoughts, feelings, desires, and memories. a method of analyzing psychic phenomena and treating emotional disorders that involves treatment sessions during which the patient is encouraged to talk freely about personal experiences and especially about early childhood and dreams.
