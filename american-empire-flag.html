The history of the Philippines from 1898 to 1946 describes the period of the American colonialization of the Philippines. It began with the outbreak of the Spanish–American War in April 1898, when the Philippines was still a colony of the Spanish East Indies, and concluded when the United States formally recognized the independence of the Republic of the Philippines on July 4, 1946.

With the signing of the Treaty of Paris on December 10, 1898, Spain ceded the Philippines to the United States, thereby beginning the era of American colonialization.[1] The interim U.S. military government of the Philippine Islands experienced a period of great political turbulence, characterised by the Philippine–American War.

Beginning in 1901, the military government was replaced by a civilian government—the Insular Government of the Philippine Islands—with William Howard Taft serving as its first Governor-General. Also, a series of insurgent governments that lacked significant international and diplomatic recognition existed between 1898 and 1904.[a]

Following the passage of the Philippine Independence Act in 1934, a Philippine presidential election was held in 1935. Manuel L. Quezon was elected and inaugurated second President of the Philippines on November 15, 1935. The Insular Government was dissolved and the Commonwealth of the Philippines, intended to be a transitional government in preparation for the country's full achievement of independence in 1946, was brought into existence.[2]

After the World War II Japanese invasion in 1941 and subsequent occupation of the Philippines, the United States and Philippine Commonwealth military recaptured the Philippines in 1945. The United States formally recognised the independence of the Republic of the Philippines on July 4, 1946, according to the terms of the Philippine Independence Act.[2]

21. Why does Clemens keep talking about the american flag and american ideals?


Answer:

The American flag is a symbol of freedom, liberty and human rights. It is a symbol of our home and all that we believe in. A symbol men rally behind, whether on our shores or foreign shores. Some hold the flag as something sacred, others have a moderate reverence for it.

But what about those who demonstrate their beliefs and views by desecrating the American flag?

Over the past month, there have been a few cases regarding the desecration of the American flag, one of which in Pennsylvania. These cases always bring up the question of: Should citizens be allowed to burn a flag that many people revere and hold sacred, or should such actions be punished?

Many people may desecrate a flag in order to protest against government policy, to demonstrate a clear hatred toward America, or perhaps merely to practice their first amendment rights. Often we see videos of people overseas in the streets chanting and burning American flags as a sign of grave disrespect. For most, seeing something that symbolizes who we are going up in flames is a very unsettling feeling.

Over the years, there have been numerous attempts to ban flag burning, one attempt in 2006 almost passing the Senate floor.

However, the Supreme Court of the United States has ruled that it is unconstitutional for the government to outlaw the desecration of a flag on first amendment grounds of “symbolic speech.”

Here at the Dakota County Star, we understand the grief many people endure when they see their flag go up in flames, especially when done by a fellow citizen living in all the comforts and freedoms the very flag they are burning represents.

However, we stand by the fact that citizens should be able to express themselves freely as long as no one is being harmed. Though disrespectful, desecrating a flag does no immediate harm, and should be a protected form of free speech. One can despise the person’s character and choice of demonstration, protest against it and educate people on what the flag means, but banning the act legally takes away one more freedom from the land the flag represents – the land of freedom and opportunity.

As Voltaire said, “I do not agree with what you have to say, but I’ll defend to the death your right to say it.”

30. Why did the Americans forbid the waving of the Filipino flag?

1898 – 1901

On May 28, 1898, days after the return of General Emilio Aguinaldo from exile in Hong Kong, Filipino troops were once again engaged in a battle against Spanish forces in Alapan, Cavite. It was in this skirmish that the Philippine flag was first unfurled as the revolutionary standard. Sewn in Hong Kong by Filipino expatriates and brought to the country by Aguinaldo, the flag was a tri-color featuring red and blue with a white triangle framing three yellow stars and an anthropomorphic eight-rayed sun.
