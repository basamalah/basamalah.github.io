Margaret Ogola's novel The River and the Source explores various themes that are significant to Kenyan culture and society. Through her stylistic approach, she reveals the complexities of family, identity, gender roles, social injustice, and spirituality. Here are five themes explored in the novel:

Family: Ogola examines the importance of family in Kenyan culture, particularly the role of women in preserving the family's legacy. She portrays Akoko, the matriarch of the family, as a wise and respected figure who passes down her knowledge and traditions to her daughters and granddaughters.Identity: The novel explores the themes of identity and belonging, especially for women who face societal pressures to conform to traditional gender roles. Through the character of Nyabera, Ogola portrays the struggles of a woman who must navigate her identity as a mother, wife, and career woman in a patriarchal society.Gender Roles: Ogola challenges traditional gender roles and highlights the importance of education and empowerment for women. Ogola portrays the struggles of women who must fight for their rights and equality in a society that favors men.Spirituality: The novel explores the themes of spirituality and faith, particularly the role of Christianity in Kenyan society. Ogola portrays the character of Akoko, who embodies the values of forgiveness, love, and compassion, and serves as a moral compass for her family and community.

Through her stylistic approach, Ogola portrays the complexities of Kenyan culture and society, highlighting the importance of family, identity, gender roles, social injustice, and spirituality.
