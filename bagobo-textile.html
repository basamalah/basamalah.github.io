How does IKAT processing contribute to the making of B'laan textile? 4. Describe the group or the Bagobo people? 5. Enumerate the arts and crafts of Bagobo? (Give 4)​


1) The B'laan people, also known as the Bilaan, inhabit the southern parts of the Philippines, particularly in the provinces of Sarangani, South Cotabato, Sultan Kudarat, and Davao del Sur.

2) The B'laan people are known for their artistic skills, and they create various crafts using natural materials such as rattan, bamboo, and abaca. Some of their notable arts and crafts include:

Beadwork, which is used to adorn their clothing and accessoriesMat weaving, which is used for sleeping, sitting, and carrying goodsWoodcarving, which is used for creating various items, such as musical instruments and household toolsBrass casting, which is used for making bells, gongs, and other musical instruments

3) IKAT processing is a technique used by the B'laan people in making their traditional textile. This involves tying and dyeing the threads before weaving, resulting in a unique and intricate pattern. The IKAT process is done by binding certain areas of the threads with cords, then dipping them into a natural dye solution. After dyeing, the cords are removed, and the threads are ready for weaving.

4) The Bagobo people, also known as the Tagabawa, are an indigenous group that inhabits the Davao regions of the Philippines. They are known for their unique culture, including their distinctive clothing, which is adorned with intricate beadwork and embroidery.

5) Like the B'laan, the Bagobo people also have a rich tradition of arts and crafts. Some of their notable creations include:

Brassware, which includes various items such as betel nut boxes, bells, and other decorative piecesBeadwork, which is used to adorn their clothing, jewelry, and other accessoriesWoodcarving, which is used for creating musical instruments, household tools, and other decorative itemsBasketry, which is used for carrying goods, storage, and decoration.

The Bagobo constitute one of the largest groups among the indigenous peoples of southern Mindanao. They are composed of three (3) sub-groups, namely the TAGABAWA, the CLATA or GUIANGAN and the UBO. Apo or Apo Sandawa to the tribal people.

The Yakan are an indigenous Muslim tribe native to the tropical island of Basilian. Located in the Sulu Archipelago in the most southern region of the Philippines, Yakan people are recognised for their remarkable technicolour geometric weaves and the distinctive face decorations used in their traditional

The Blaan people, alternatively spelled as "B'laan," are one of the indigenous peoples of Southern Mindanao in the Philippines. Their name could have derived from "bla" meaning "opponent" and the suffix "an" meaning "people." Other terms used to refer to this group are Blaan, Bira-an, Baraan, Vilanes, and Bilanes.

The T'boli (Tagabili to lowlanders) are an animist ethnic group inhabiting highland areas in southwestern Mindanao, centering on Lake Sebu (TauSebu is another of the people's names). Their immediate neighbors are the Manobo and Bilaan, other animist upland peoples (with whom they are often in conflict).

The Maranao people (Maranao: ['mәranaw]; Filipino: Maranaw), also spelled Meranao, Maranaw, and Mëranaw, is the term used by the Philippine government to refer to the southern indigenous people who are the "people of the lake", a predominantly-Muslim Lanao province region of the Philippine island of Mindanao.
