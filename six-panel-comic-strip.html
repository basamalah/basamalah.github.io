8. 6-8 panel comic strip about how conflicts within the social group can cause societal problems​


Answer:

Panel 1:

[Scene: A diverse social group consisting of people from different backgrounds, races, and cultures gathered around a table, engaging in a discussion.]

Character 1: "We need to address the issues within our social group and work together to find solutions."

Panel 2:

[Close-up of Character 2, looking frustrated with crossed arms.]

Character 2: "I don't see the point. We're all different, and we can't agree on anything."

Panel 3:

[Character 3, a mediator, steps in, trying to calm the situation.]

Character 3: "Let's try to understand each other's perspectives and find common ground."

Panel 4:

[Characters 4 and 5, representing two opposing sides, are engaged in a heated argument.]

Character 4: "We can't ignore the inequalities and injustices faced by some members of our group!"

Character 5: "Those issues don't concern me. It's not my problem!"

Panel 5:

[Characters 6 and 7, looking concerned, are listening to the argument.]

Character 6: "This conflict is causing tension within our group."

Character 7: "If we don't find a way to resolve it, it could lead to bigger societal problems."

Panel 6:

[Scene shifts to show the social group divided, with individuals isolated in their own corners, not engaging with each other.]

Narration (in a caption box): "The conflicts within the social group led to division and lack of cooperation."

Panel 7:

[Scene shows the negative consequences of the unresolved conflict, such as protests, discrimination, and inequality.]

Narration (in a caption box): "Societal problems arose due to the conflicts that remained unresolved within the social group."

Panel 8:

[The social group comes together, engaging in a constructive dialogue, finding common ground, and working towards solutions.]

Narration (in a caption box): "But when the social group finally addressed their conflicts and worked together, positive change was possible."

[End of comic strip]

RESEARCH METHODS

First Panel : Quantitative Methods

Definition

Simply put, quantitative data provides the numbers that validate the general points of your study while qualitative data provides the details and context to understand all the implications.

Quantitative research is a data collection technique that allows the researcher to analyze behaviors, opinions, or even expectations in quantity. The goal is often to derive statistically measurable conclusions, unlike a qualitative study .

In a research work, the quantitative study makes it possible to prove or demonstrate facts by quantifying a phenomenon. This study technique uses a questionnaire or panel survey to collect data for analysis.

The results, expressed in figures, take the form of statistical data that can be represented in graphs or tables.To conduct a quantitative study, the investigator must accurately select a representative sample of the population under study. This representativeness allows the results to be relevant.

To conduct a quantitative study, the investigator has two tools:

The survey  : to ask a question.The questionnaire  : to ask several questions.The researcher must choose which tool will be the most suitable to provide him with relevant information on his research topic. For a survey or a questionnaire , the questions are closed and take the form:Yes/no answers.Multiple choice (for MCQs).

  4. The poll

The survey allows you to obtain a precise answer on a given question. This question is asked of a representative sample of individuals, that is to say that the persons questioned must represent the population studied in theory. (THIS IS THE METHOD USED IN FIRST PANEL)

Second Panel: Qualitative Method

Definition

Qualitative studies are in-depth studies of a psychological nature, seeking to explain behaviors, motivations or to guide creative research. They relate to small samples (individual interviews or group meetings).Unlike quantitative research, qualitative research is a method for analyzing and understanding phenomena, group behaviors, facts or subjects.Observation is a technique widely used in qualitative studies and allows an analysis of reality: it allows you to describe behaviors, places, situations and emotions that you witness as an observer.

Several techniques exist:

Incognito observation  : observing without being seen or revealing your research process.Uncovered observation  : observing while having informed the people observed of the research process.Armed observation  : observing and possessing an instrument (or a person) which proposes the questions to the subjects and records the answers.
