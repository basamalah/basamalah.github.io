What Does the Brain Do?

The brain controls what you think and feel, how you learn and remember, and the way you move and talk. But it also controls things you're less aware of — like the beating of your heart and the digestion of your food.

Think of the brain as a central computer that controls all the body's functions. The rest of the nervous system is like a network that relays messages back and forth from the brain to different parts of the body. It does this via the spinal cord, which runs from the brain down through the back. It contains threadlike nerves that branch out to every organ and body part.

Luckily, this neurological relay race happens in an instant.

The Human Nervous System  

Human nervous system is known to be the body's control system. It is compose of brain, spinal cord and nerves. It was called to be the control system of our body because it sends, receives and processes the nerve impulse all over the body which are received through our senses. Addition to that, the nervous system is capable to manage, command, and direct that regulates our behavior.

The nervous system is delicate and complex. The control center of it is the brain. Brain is made up of cerebrum, cerebellum, thalamus, hypothalamus and the brain stem. Our brain is responsible of sending messages that commands our body on how it will move and response. Aside from this, the spinal cord. It looks like a long pipe shape emerging from the medulla oblongata going through the vertebral column. It is responsible for transmitting motor and sensory signals between the brain and nerves. It also has separate circuit for the many reflexes.

On top of this is the basic unit of the nervous system, the neuron or nerve cell. About billions of neurons enables the distinct parts of a human body to interact on both ways through the brain and the spinal cord. It is coated with myelin to regulate the insulation of the neurons and therefore allows the nerves to communicate rapidly.

If a person's nervous system is working properly, it will be easy for him to finish his task, understand his feelings and cope with his circumstances, because the his nerve cells are working properly, enough for his spinal cord send signals to the brain, where the brain will react and properly control his behavior.

The two principal divisions of the nervous system are the 1) Brain and the 2) spinal cord of the nervous system. The nervous system in humans is made of 3) Axon, Dendrites, Nerve fibers, and all the neurons that serve as communication channels between the various organs of the body.  ​

Explanation of each answer

The brain - is a complex organ that controls thought, memory, emotion, touch, motor skills, vision, breathing, temperature, hunger, and every process that regulates our body.

The spinal cord -  is a long, tube-like band of tissue. It connects your brain to your lower back. Your spinal cord carries nerve signals from your brain to your body and vice versa. These nerve signals help you feel sensations and move your body. Any damage to your spinal cord can affect your movement or function.

What are the 3 major functions of the spinal cord?

Motor Functions - directs your body's voluntary muscle movements.

Sensory Functions – monitors sensation of touch, pressure, temperature, and pain.

Autonomic Functions – regulates digestion, urination, body temperature, heart rate, and dilation/contraction of blood vessels (blood pressure).

Axons are very thin nerve fibers that carry nerve impulses away from a neuron (nerve cell) to another neuron. A neuron is responsible for receiving sensory input, sending motor commands to your muscles, and transforming and relaying the electrical signals throughout these processes.

Dendrites are tree-like extensions at the beginning of a neuron that help increase the surface area of the cell body. These tiny protrusions receive information from other neurons and transmit electrical stimulation to the soma. Dendrites are also covered with synapses.

nerve fiber, a portion of a nerve cell (neuron) that carries nerve impulses away from the cell body. A neuron typically has one axon that connects it with other neurons or with muscle or gland cells. Some axons may be quite long, reaching, for example, from the spinal cord down to a toe.
