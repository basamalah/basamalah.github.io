All sides of these tiles are of the same length; and all their angles are multiples of 36° (Δ/5 radians), giving five and tenfold symmetries. The tiles are painted with strapwork lines (girih), usually more prominent than the tile borders. Geometric patterns are one of three non-figurative forms of decoration in Islamic art, including calligraphy and vegetal patterns. Both alone or used in combination with non-figurative ornamentation or figurative representation, geometric patterns are popularly identified with Islamic art, primarily due to their aniconic quality. These geometric patterns not only adorn the surfaces of monumental Islamic architecture, but also serve as a significant decorative feature in a wide range of objects of all types. Although geometric ornamentation may have reached a pinnacle in the Islamic world, in late antiquity the sources for both forms and complex designs existed among the Greeks, Romans, and Sasanians in Iran. Islamic artists appropriated core elements of the classical style, then complicated and expanded on them in order to invent a modern type of decoration that highlighted the importance of unity and order. The significant academic achievements of Islamic mathematicians, astronomers and scientists have been key to the development of this unique new style. Consisting of, or formed from, basic forms such as the circle and the square, geometric patterns were fused, duplicated, interlaced and organized in intricate combinations, thus being one of the most distinctive features of Islamic art. However, these complex patterns tend to be a refusal to adhere exclusively to the laws of geometry. As a matter of fact, geometric ornamentation in Islamic art indicates a surprising amount of freedom; in its repetition and complexity, it provides the possibility of infinite growth and can also allow the inclusion of other forms of ornamentation. In terms of abstractness, repeating shapes, and symmetry, geometric patterns have much in common with the so-called arabesque form found in many vegetal patterns. Calligraphic ornamentation is often seen in combination with geometric patterns.

17. Who came up with Dual-Nature of light?A.Christian HuygensB.Isaac NewtonC.James Clark MaxwellD.Max Planck​


Answer:

B.Isaac Newton

Explanation:

Long story. Way back in the late 17-early 18 century, Newton proposed the particle nature of light, while Huyghens proposed the wave theory. Both pictures successfully explained the then known properties of light: rectilinear propagation, reflection, refraction and dispersion. A decisive blow in favour of the wave theory was the discovery of interference (Young’s double slit experiment) and diffraction (Fraunhofer, Fresnel, …) Another discovery in support of the wave theory was the discovery of polarization. Thus, at the end of the 19th century, all the evidence was in favour of the wave theory. The blow came with the discovery of the photoelectric effect, which showed that light sometimes behaved as a particle and Planck’s explanation for the black body spectrum, which again assumed a particulate nature of light. The only way of reconciling the two was to say that light was a quantum object that sometimes behaved like a classical particle and sometimes like a classical wave.
