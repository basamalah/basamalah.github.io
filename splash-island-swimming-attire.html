3. What is the importance of proper swimming attire in a swimming pool​


Street clothes (especially those made of cotton) can transport air and waterborne contaminants to the pool.

Absorbent materials such as cotton can break down in the water. These fibers can clog pool filters and create the need for expensive repairs. Lycra and Nylon are the best non-absorbent materials for swimwear.

Any colored or dyed materials (unless specifically made for swimming) can bleed into the pool, affecting the water chemistry.

11. what is splash erosion ​


Splash erosion or rain drop impact represents the first stage in the erosion process. Splash erosion results from the bombardment of the soil surface by rain drops. Rain drops behave as little bombs when falling on exposed or bare soil, displacing soil particles and destroying soil structure.

EXAMPLE:

Splash ErosionSplash ErosionWhen raindrops hit the soil, they cause individual soil particles on the ground to disperse and splash back down. This makes it difficult for rain to permeate the ground, so the water builds up on the surface and leads to the formation of moving water, in the form of runoff.

27. 16. In the Intertidal zone, how do organisms in the high tide zone receive moisture needed to survive? a. Other animals come to give it to them b. Rain c. Wave splash d. Organisms swim to get moisture​


How do organisms in the high tide zone receive moisture needed to survive

d. Organisms swim to get moisture

Because the high tide zone is only underwater at high tide, it is hotter and drier. Organisms in the intertidal zone create their own communities as they move up and down the zone's elevation gradient. Some species reside higher up the beach, closer to the high tide line, while others live lower down, closer to the low tide line. Anything that lives in the intertidal zone must be able to tolerate severe waves as well as fluctuations in moisture, temperature, and salt. Sea stars, snails, seaweed, algae, and crabs live in the intertidal zones of rocky shorelines. Barnacles, mussels, and kelps may live in this environment by clinging to the rocks. Barnacles and mussels may also store seawater in their closed shells to save themselves from drying out during low tide. Intertidal zones rich in sediments are populated by various clam and sand species.
