12. EPP teching impact of behaviorism​


Behaviorism can impact EPP teaching especially in the learning process of young children by shaping positive behavior in the classroom. It can also teach students that every behavior has consequences.

Shaping positive behavior

One of the principles in the theory of behaviorism is concerned with the formation of habits. The assumption that experience is the most influential factor in shaping behavior implies how plastic humans are. By creating a relevant environment, it is simple to shape into anything. Behaviorism theory is very suitable for acquiring abilities that require practice and habituation that contain elements such as speed, spontaneity, flexibility, reflexes, and endurance. One of the laws in this theory is the law of practice. The more often a behavior is practiced or used, the stronger the association. This is related to the theory of trial and error, namely the existence of activity. There are various responses to various situations, there is the elimination of various wrong responses, and there is progress in reactions to achieve goals.

Teach students that every behavior has consequences.

In behaviorism theory, reward and punishment are important in the learning process. In this theory, the teacher gives a reward or high value so that the child will be more diligent. On the other hand, the teacher will give punishment if students violate the rules that have been set. Prioritizing the mechanism for the formation of learning outcomes through stimulus response procedures is important in this learning theory so that learning is oriented towards results that can be measured and observed, and if an error occurs, it must be corrected immediately.

This behavioristic method is very suitable for acquiring abilities that require practice and habituation that contain elements such as: speed, spontaneity, flexibility, reflexes, endurance and so on, for example: foreign language conversation, typing, dancing, using a computer, swimming, sports and so on. This theory is also suitable to be applied to train children who still need the dominance of adult roles, like to repeat and have to be used to, like to imitate and are happy with forms of direct rewards such as being given candy or praise.
