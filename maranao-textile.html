1. what is the difference between maranao textile and t'boli textile​


Answer:

Maranao textiles are traditional textiles produced by the Maranao people of the Philippines, who are native to the Lake Lanao region of Mindanao. These textiles are known for their intricate patterns and bright colors, and are often used in traditional Maranao clothing and other decorative items.

T'boli textiles, on the other hand, are traditional textiles produced by the T'boli people of the Philippines, who are native to the province of South Cotabato in Mindanao. These textiles are also known for their intricate patterns and bright colors, and are often used in traditional T'boli clothing and other decorative items.

One key difference between Maranao and T'boli textiles is the specific patterns and designs used. Maranao textiles are characterized by geometric patterns and motifs, while T'boli textiles are known for their depiction of natural subjects, such as flowers and animals. In addition, Maranao textiles are typically produced using the ikat dyeing technique, while T'boli textiles are often created using the weft ikat technique

Identify the similarities and differences of the textile of Ilocos and the textile of Cordillera Province, also the textile of Cagayan Valley from the textile of Mountain Province

The types of textiles produced from different regions depend on their place of origin and the availability of natural materials in the area. In addition, there are weaving traditions that are passed down from generation to generation in each tribe.

Explanation

Products that are finely crafted by hand such as textile products, each piece of fabric has a function, symbolism, beliefs, and culture of those who make it. This adds to the aesthetics of the product and elevates it to the level of a work of art. There are many textile manufacturers spread across the Philippines. Ilocos and Cordillera in the northern part of Luzon are the centers of weaving villages. In addition, the islands of Mindoro and Bicol also have weavers.

Textiles of Ilocos and Cordillera provinces

In terms of materials and functions, Ilocos and Cordillera textiles are both made using natural materials and are used for traditional clothing. Ilocos textiles are made from cotton and Cordillera textiles are made from abaca fiber. The designs made by Cordillera weavers are different from the designs made by Ilocos weavers. Ilocos textile designs have intricate geometric designs.

Cagayan Valley and Mountain Province textiles

Cagayan valley textiles use slatted textiles, while mountain province textiles use zigzags. The colors of the Cagayan valley textiles are dark that degrade with light and the designs are unusual, especially the stripes. The colors used in Mountain Province textiles use contrasting colors with dark colors such as black. The weaving style used in the Mountain Province uses traditional backstrap weaving and loom weaving. Not only traditional clothing, backstrap weavers also make traditional cloth and clothing for ceremonies. Loom weavers make hats, purses and other souvenirs.

Thus, the variety of textiles from different places is based on the patterns, designs, colors, and materials used by them.

The Bagobo constitute one of the largest groups among the indigenous peoples of southern Mindanao. They are composed of three (3) sub-groups, namely the TAGABAWA, the CLATA or GUIANGAN and the UBO. Apo or Apo Sandawa to the tribal people.

The Yakan are an indigenous Muslim tribe native to the tropical island of Basilian. Located in the Sulu Archipelago in the most southern region of the Philippines, Yakan people are recognised for their remarkable technicolour geometric weaves and the distinctive face decorations used in their traditional

The Blaan people, alternatively spelled as "B'laan," are one of the indigenous peoples of Southern Mindanao in the Philippines. Their name could have derived from "bla" meaning "opponent" and the suffix "an" meaning "people." Other terms used to refer to this group are Blaan, Bira-an, Baraan, Vilanes, and Bilanes.

The T'boli (Tagabili to lowlanders) are an animist ethnic group inhabiting highland areas in southwestern Mindanao, centering on Lake Sebu (TauSebu is another of the people's names). Their immediate neighbors are the Manobo and Bilaan, other animist upland peoples (with whom they are often in conflict).

The Maranao people (Maranao: ['mәranaw]; Filipino: Maranaw), also spelled Meranao, Maranaw, and Mëranaw, is the term used by the Philippine government to refer to the southern indigenous people who are the "people of the lake", a predominantly-Muslim Lanao province region of the Philippine island of Mindanao.
