11. 3. What is the Latin translation of "Life only comes from life"?a. Omne ex vivo vivumb. Vivo ex vivum omne c. Vivum omne ex vivo d. Omne vivum ex vivo​


Omne vivum ex vivo is the Latin translation of "Life only comes from life".

History of the Latin Language: Origins of the Language

Latin comes from Italic. Italic is commonly spoken in what is now Italy. Latin was never the only language spoken in this region. In fact, the entire region is home to many different peoples and languages.

Let's go back to the 6th century BC. The Italic people lived in the central and southern regions of Italy. At that time, this region was known as Latium. This place is the origin of the Vatican. Italic, like all languages spoken in Europe, is an Indo-European language.

Including long-lost languages as well as languages such as Italian, French, Portuguese, Spanish and Romanian. It should be noted that although the regional language we now know as Italian is italic, the speakers of the Italic language did not originate in Italy.

According to historians, they migrated from the Balkans around 1500 BC. Some scholars think that the group that lived in Italy before them were the Ligurians who are considered Italian-Celts.

Before Rome was founded in 753 BC, scholars believe that the Italic language was heavily influenced by the Greeks and Estrucans. Once Rome was founded, the spread of Latin was high on the agenda throughout the region.

Latin inherited 6 of its 8 grammars from Indo-European languages. With the:

Genitive: used for possession Vocational: used to refer to or respond to people Accusative: used for the object of the verb Dative: used for active nouns Ablative: used with passive nouns Nominative: used for the subject of the verb
