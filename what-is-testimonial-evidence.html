22. ABOUT EVIDENCE$9.44 Testimony of witness assessed in its entirety.​


$9.44 Testimony of witness assessed in its entirety.

In ascertaining the true meaning of the testimony given by a witness, everything stated by him on his examination-in-chief as well as on cross must be considered. Facts imperfectly stated in answer to one question may be supplied by his answer to another;and when from one statement considered by itself an inference may be deduced, that inference may be strengthened or repelled by facts disclosed in another. There should be no selection of isolated parts of the testimony; its general bearing must be taken together." Generally, a witness' testimony is considered in its entirety. The exception to the rule is that the trial court may give credence only to its part which it may reasonably believe to be credible and reject the other part which it may be unbelievable. However, the trial court is not bound to disclose which part of the testimony it deems credible.⁴⁹²

Explanation:

The evidences being discussed are testimonial evidence, anecdotal evidence, statistical evidence, and analogical evidence.

In judging the validity of these evidences, one must consider the following:

Testimonial evidence: Is the person providing the testimony an expert in the field? Are there any biases or conflicts of interest that may affect their testimony? Is their testimony consistent with other evidence presented?

Anecdotal evidence: Is the anecdote representative of a larger trend or is it an isolated incident? Is the anecdote based on first-hand experience or hearsay?

Statistical evidence: Are the statistics from a reputable source? Are the statistics accurately presented and interpreted? Are the statistics relevant to the argument being made?

Analogical evidence: Is the analogy valid and relevant to the argument being made? Are there any key differences between the two situations being compared that make the analogy inappropriate?

The validity of evidence depends on a variety of factors, including the quality and reliability of the source, the relevance of the evidence to the argument being made, and the consistency of the evidence with other sources of information. Testimonial evidence can be valid if it comes from an expert in the field and is consistent with other evidence presented. Anecdotal evidence can be valid if it represents a larger trend and is based on first-hand experience. Statistical evidence can be valid if it is from a reputable source and accurately presented and interpreted. Analogical evidence can be valid if it is a relevant and accurate comparison.
