AIM

The goal of rebellion is resistance while a revolt seeks a revolution. As power shifts relative to the external adversary, or power shifts within a mixed coalition, or positions harden or soften on either side, an insurrection may seesaw between the two forms.

PURPOSES

Rebellion, uprising, or insurrection is a refusal of obedience or order. ... If rebellion generally seeks to evade and/or gain concessions from an oppressive power, a revolt seeks to overthrow and destroy that power, as well as its accompanying laws.

CAUSE

rebellion may be the result of personal, political, military, social, or religious grievances, which culminate in a large-scale movement to remove the object of aggression. Unlike a revolt, which seeks to form a revolution, the main feature of a rebellion is active or passive resistance.

How rebels can add value. Rebel leaders might cause initial fear and discomfort, but they create a sense of excitement and a vision that people can get behind.

16. What are the different meaning of cry of rebellion


Answer:

The term "Cry" is translated from the Spanish el grito de rebelion (cry of rebellion) or el grito for short. Thus the Grito de Balintawak is comparable to Mexico's Grito de Dolores (1810). However, el grito de rebelion strictly refers to a decision or call to revolt. It does not necessarily connote shouting, unlike the Filipino sigaw.[

Answer:

The Cry of Balintawak (Filipino: Sigaw ng Balíntawak, Spanish: Grito de Balíntawak), was the beginning of the Philippine Revolution against the Spanish Empire.[1]

At the close of August 1896, members of the Katipunan secret society (Katipuneros) led by Andrés Bonifacio rose up in revolt somewhere in an area referred to as Caloocan,[2] wider than the jurisdiction of present-day Caloocan City which may have overlapped into present-day Quezon City.[3]

Originally the term cry referred to the first clash between the Katipuneros and the Civil Guards (Guardia Civil). The cry could also refer to the tearing up of community tax certificates (cédulas personales) in defiance of their allegiance to Spain. The inscriptions of "Viva la Independencia Filipina" can also be referred as term for the cry. This was literally accompanied by patriotic shouts.[4]

Because of competing accounts and ambiguity of the place where this event took place, the exact date and place of the Cry is in contention.[3][4] From 1908 until 1963, the official stance was that the cry occurred on August 26 in Balintawak. In 1963 the Philippine government declared a shift to August 23 in Pugad Lawin, Quezon City.

22. Complete the table below about the opium wars and rebellions in China. OPIUM WARS CAUSE EFFECTS First Opium War Second Opium War REBELLIONS Taiping Rebellion Boxer Rebellion 4. Exclamatory:


Answer:

I'd be happy to help you complete the table. Here's what I can add:

OPIUM WARS CAUSE EFFECTS

First Opium War Trade imbalance and British smuggling of opium into China. China cedes Hong Kong to Britain, forced to open treaty ports and grant extraterritoriality to British citizens.

Second Opium War Renewed tensions between China and Britain, French intervention. China forced to legalize opium trade, open additional treaty ports, and pay indemnity to foreign powers.

REBELLIONS

Taiping Rebellion Frustration with government corruption, poverty, and social inequality. Led by Hong Xiuquan, the rebellion sought to establish a Christian-based utopia. Resulted in massive loss of life, particularly in the Yangtze River valley, and weakened the Qing dynasty.

Boxer Rebellion Anti-foreign and anti-Christian sentiment, fueled by frustration with foreign influence and drought. Led by the "Boxers," a group of martial artists, the rebellion sought to expel foreign powers from China. Resulted in foreign intervention and further weakening of the Qing dynasty.

Answer:

First Opium War | Cause: Imposition of illegal opium trade by the British | Effect: Opium imports to China rose dramatically

Second Opium War | Cause: Chinese refusal to allow more opium into the country | Effect: Chinese defeat and the imposition of various punitive treaties

Taiping Rebellion | Cause: Disgruntlement over Manchu rule and the resulting frustration with foreign interference | Effect: Loss of life and death of the rebellion's leader

Boxer Rebellion | Cause: Growing frustration over imperialistic foreign activities | Effect: End of Chinese sovereignty and the eventual Empire of Japan's occupation of Manchuria.
