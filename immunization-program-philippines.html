7. Prevention and control of National Immunization program​


The prevention and control of the National Immunization Program involves a comprehensive approach to ensure that the population receives the required vaccines to prevent and control the spread of infectious diseases. The program is designed to provide immunization to all individuals, especially children, against vaccine-preventable diseases such as measles, polio, tuberculosis, diphtheria, and tetanus.

The program includes several key components such as vaccine supply management, vaccine delivery, and distribution, vaccine safety monitoring, and community outreach and education. It is also important to maintain accurate and up-to-date records of vaccine administration to ensure that each individual receives the appropriate vaccine at the recommended time.

Community outreach and education play a vital role in promoting the program and ensuring its success. Through community engagement, healthcare providers can educate the public on the importance of immunization, address any concerns, and encourage individuals to receive the required vaccines.

To ensure the effectiveness of the program, it is also essential to monitor the vaccine's safety and efficacy continually. This includes monitoring the incidence of vaccine-preventable diseases, vaccine coverage rates, and adverse events following immunization.

Overall, the prevention and control of the National Immunization Program is critical in promoting public health and preventing the spread of infectious diseases. It requires collaboration between healthcare providers, government agencies, and the community to ensure that all individuals receive the recommended vaccines and maintain optimal health.

17. positive impact of the program of global alliance for vaccines and immunization ​


Answer:

Abstract

Background: The Global Alliance for Vaccines and Immunisation (GAVI) was created in 1999 to enable even the poorest countries to provide vaccines to all children. We aimed to assess the effect of GAVI on combined diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis vaccine (DTP3) coverage.

Methods: We examined the relation between DTP3 coverage for GAVI recipient countries from 1995 to 2004 and immunisation services support (ISS) and non-ISS expenditure per surviving child, controlling for income per head and local political governance variables. We analysed DTP3 coverage reported by governments and estimated by WHO/UNICEF. We also investigated the effect of GAVI on country reporting behaviour.

Results: In countries with DTP3 coverage of 65% or less at baseline, ISS spending per surviving child had a significant positive effect on DTP3 coverage (p=0.0005). This effect was not present in countries with DTP3 coverage of 65-80% or 80% or more at baseline. If ISS expenditure only is assessed, the estimated cost per additional child immunised in countries with baseline coverage of 65% or less is US$14 and if ISS and non-ISS expenditures are included the cost per child is almost $20.

Interpretation: The success of ISS funding in countries with baseline DTP3 coverage of 65% or less provides evidence that a public-private partnership can work to reverse a negative trend in global health and that performance-related disbursement can work in some settings. Because ISS funding seems to have no effect in countries with baseline coverage greater than 65%, GAVI should consider redistributing its resources to countries with the lowest coverage.
