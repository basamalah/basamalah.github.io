14. factors that motivate perpetrators of cyberbullying​


Explanation:

For kids and teens

Know that it’s not your fault. What people call “bullying” is sometimes an argument between two people. But if someone is repeatedly cruel to you, that’s bullying and you mustn’t blame yourself. No one deserves to be treated cruelly.

Don’t respond or retaliate. Sometimes a reaction is exactly what aggressors are looking for because they think it gives them power over you, and you don’t want to empower a bully. As for retaliating, getting back at a bully turns you into one – and can turn one mean act into a chain reaction. If you can, remove yourself from the situation. If you can’t, sometimes humor disarms or distracts a person from bullying.

Save the evidence. The only good news about bullying online or on phones is that it can usually be captured, saved, and shown to someone who can help. You can save that evidence in case things escalate.

Tell the person to stop. This is completely up to you – don’t do it if you don’t feel totally comfortable doing it, because you need to make your position completely clear that you will not stand for this treatment any more. You may need to practice beforehand with someone you trust, like a parent or good friend.

Reach out for help – especially if the behavior’s really getting to you. You deserve backup. See if there’s someone who can listen, help you process what’s going on and work through it – a friend, relative or maybe an adult you trust.

Use available tech tools. Most social media apps and services allow you to block the person. Whether the harassment’s in an app, texting, comments or tagged photos, do yourself a favor and block the person. You can also report the problem to the service. That probably won’t end it, but you don’t need the harassment in your face, and you’ll be less tempted to respond. If you’re getting threats of physical harm, you should call your local police (with a parent or guardian’s help) and consider reporting it to school authorities.

18. factors that motivate peretrations of cyber bullying​


Answer:

External Motivations

The level one code external motivations, was defined as the reasons for cyberbullying provoked by the characteristic of the cybervictim or by something specific to the situation. Three level two codes (no consequences, non-confrontational, target was different) were categorized as external motivations.

Internal Motivations

The level one theme, internal motivations, described high school students’ motivations for bullying that were perceived to be influenced by the cyberbully’s emotional state. There were ten level two codes (redirect feelings, revenge, make themselves feel better, boredom, instigation, protection, jealousy, seeking approval, trying out a new persona, anonymity/disinhibition effect) categorized as internal motivations

Explanation:

Intrinsic factors are internal motivations that drive a person's actions, such as a passion for a subject or a desire to help others. In the context of a student nurse, intrinsic factors might include a desire to make a positive impact on patients' lives and a love of the challenges and rewards of nursing.

Extrinsic factors, on the other hand, are external factors that can influence a person's choices and actions, such as the need for a future income or parental pressure. In the context of a student nurse, extrinsic factors might include the need to earn a good salary to support oneself or one's family, or the desire to climb the career ladder and advance in the nursing profession.

Both intrinsic and extrinsic factors can play a role in a student nurse's choice of career. Understanding these factors can help researchers, educators, and others understand how students develop their career plans and how those plans might affect their motivation to learn and improve.

In addition to intrinsic and extrinsic factors, there are also many other determinants that can influence a student nurse's academic performance. These determinants can be grouped into four broad categories: student-related factors, school-related factors, home-related factors, and teacher-related factors.

Student-related factors include things like a student's aptitude, interests, and attitudes. School-related factors might include the quality of the nursing education program, the availability of resources and support, and the school's reputation. Home-related factors can include things like family support, access to educational resources, and the home environment. Teacher-related factors might include the quality of instruction, the availability of support and guidance, and the teacher's teaching style.

Overall, the academic achievements of student nurses can be assessed by their ability to understand and apply the knowledge and skills they learn in nursing school. This might involve the ability to comprehend text, lessons, and practical skills, as well as the ability to apply that knowledge in real-world situations.
