21. Tong by sionil Jose-characters-time-place-conflict-key events-climax-resolution-point of view​.


Tong by Sionil Jose

- Characters:

Alice Tan, the main character and a Chinese-Filipino, represents the Ongpin Chinese, the lower class. Although her uncle owns an electrical store, he is on the same social level as hers. The other Chinese character in the novel, the old, wealthy Chinese man to whom Alice plans to marry, symbolizes the upper class. Conrado Lopez, the second main character, is a Filipino who has developed class consciousness after falling in love with Alice Tan.

- Time

This may explain that Alice's life is affected by patriarchy in their ideology, but this fails to account for the oppression of Conrado as a person of a different race. But, unfortunately, this still happens in the Philippines. And this story is the allegory to represent this culture.

- Place

Ongpin, Manila, Philippines

- Conflict

The primary source of conflict in the relationship between the two main characters is Alice's uncle's desire to amass wealth and join a higher social level. In this narrative, the freedom of the two main characters to choose who to love is affirmed regardless of their class, color, or culture. Location is used to classify the social divisions of Chinese Filipinos in the narrative.

Conrado Lopez, the second main character, is a Filipino who has developed class consciousness after falling in love with Alice Tan. This demonstrates that Filipinos and Chinese Filipinos are portrayed differently in the story. In contrast to the former, the latter lacks class consciousness.

- Key Events

There is a bias or stereotype against them, accentuating how Chinese Filipinos perceive them as a race. For example, according to Chinese Filipinos, Filipinos are goldbricks and nerds. This is because they do not know the business. Therefore, Filipinos images negatively.

- Climax

Two races confront one another based on class, race, and philosophy. These are inferred through the author's use of foreshadowing in the narrative.

- Resolution

Tong is the Chinese word for "pambayad utang," and the Chinese woman in the short story was the tong. Their forbidden love tale is further affected by the fact that she is a tong; they cannot be together because their destinies do not coincide. Tong is a narrative that teaches us that it is impossible to foretell the specifics of our love experiences in the realm of love.

Every tale has a unique conclusion, and no matter how much you fight for it, if it's not for you, it's not for you. No matter what you do, you cannot get what you want. This narrative intends to demonstrate two distinct cultures. As the saying goes, love is the most dangerous thing in the world, and the purpose of this narrative is to teach us to be cautious in romantic relationships. It is a narrative for everyone of all ages, not only for couples. We cannot argue that this type of love tale does not exist in the present because there are still forbidden love stories, not only because the lovers are Chinese but also due to their diverse cultures, beliefs, preferences, and customs.

- Point of view

This story's intersectionality illustrates the author's social construction of Chinese-Filipino culture in the Philippines. It demonstrates how Chinese Filipinos and vice versa perceive Filipinos. Sionil Jose's literature, which typically focuses on Philippine history and colonialism, defends social justice and the morality of the Filipino people. This short story on the clash between Filipino and Chinese cultures reflects his feelings against Chinese Filipinos. Even today, Sionil Jose's attitude and perception towards Chinese Filipinos are reflected in his commentaries about the story.

27. what is your reflection of the story "the God Stealer" by Francisco sionil jose?


     The God Stealer is a Philippine literature written by Francisco Sionil Jose. It is characterized by Philip Latak a.k.a. “Ip-pig” - a native Ifugao who seeked for a greener pasture in the big city; Sam Christie – Philip’s boss who’s from Boston; Philip’s grandfather – the village doctor; Reverend Doone – a San Franciscan clergyman; and Sadek Latak – Philip’s brother who disliked him.

Philip abandoned his native land seeking for greener pastures. As a result, his relatives disowned and despised him. He found a job at an “agency” in the big city leading him to meet pathways with Sam Christie, his American boss. After working for several years in the city, news came that his grandfather was ill and this made him return to his hometown. Sam Christie came together with him too and he also got the chance to mingle with the Igorots.

Sam Christie, who has some fascination on traveling and keeping souvenirs, got very interested in having a sacred relic from the Ifugao – a wooden-carved god.  In order to pay gratitude to the man responsible for his pay raise, Philip stole the idol from his grandfather and gave it to Sam. The day after the god was stolen, Philip’s grandfather tragically died in his arms. This scenario urged Philip to stay in the mountains instead of going back to the city with Sam.

If I was on Philip’s shoes, I would also understand his course of action of moving to the city to survive. It was a practical decision to make considering the rough way of living in the mountains. He was young and he had dreams for himself. However, I do not approve of him stealing the wooden god from his grandfather. That was such an unforgivable decision to make and he did deserve to face the anticipated consequence. He had it coming.  

It is appropriate to pay for gratitude to the people who are able to help us. Yet, it isn’t fair to go beyond the lines in order to do that. I think it would have been better if Philip just offered to carve a replica of the god and sent it to Sam when it was done. Although it would take time to finish it, at least he wouldn’t have hurt anyone and he wouldn’t have regretted his action.
