Kimono is a traditional Japanese dress. The literal meaning of kimono is clothing or something to wear (ki means use, and mono means goods). Today, the kimono is shaped like the letter "T", resembling a coat with long sleeves and a collar. The length of the kimono is made up to the ankles. Women wear a kimono in the form of a dress, while men wear a kimono in the form of a suit. The right collar should be under the left collar. A cloth belt called an obi is wrapped around the stomach/waist, and tied at the back. Footwear when wearing a kimono is zōri or geta.

Kimono is now more often worn by women on special occasions. Unmarried women wear a type of kimono called a furisode.  Furisode's hallmark is the wide sleeves that almost touch the floor. Girls who even turn 20 wear furisode to attend shiki permission. Men wear the kimono at weddings, tea ceremonies and other formal events. When performing outside the sumo arena, professional wrestlers are required to wear a kimono. Children wear kimonos when attending Shichi-Go-San celebrations. In addition, kimonos are worn by workers in the service and tourism industries, female waiters at traditional restaurants (ryōtei) and employees at traditional inns (ryokan).

Answer:

Explanation:

The patadyong (pronounced pa-tad-jóng, also called patadyung, patadjong, habol, or habul), is an indigenous Philippine rectangular or tube-like wraparound skirt worn by both men and women of the Visayas islands and the Sulu Archipelago, similar to the Malong, or Sarong. It was also historically worn in parts of Luzon like Pampanga and Sorsogon.[1][2][3][4]

In the precolonial era, it was usually worn with a barú or bayú, a simple collar-less shirt or jacket with close-fitting long sleeves.[2][5] During the Spanish period, this evolved into the kimona, a variant of the baro't saya worn by Christianized Visayan lowlanders consisting of a loose translucent blouse, an undershirt, and a patadyong or a patadyong-patterned skirt.[6][7]

The patadyong is identical to the malong used in mainland Mindanao, but is longer than the tapis of Luzon; it is also identical to the sarong of neighboring Indonesia, Brunei, and Malaysia, for which only the designating name changes (patadyong in Visayan languages vs. sarong in Malay language).[1] Its name means "straight [in shape]" in Visayan languages, from the root word tadlong, "[to go] straight"; its alternative name "habol" or "habul" means "woven [textile]", though it usually means "blanket" in modern Visayan.
