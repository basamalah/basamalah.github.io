3. 1. A pressure vessel that has a volume of 10 m³ is used to store high pressure air for operating a supersonic wind tunnel. If the air pressure and temperature inside the vessel are 20 atm and 300 K, respectively. What is the mass of air stored in the vessel (in kilograms)?


Answer:

The mass of air stored in the vessel is 1.206 * 10^4 kg.

Step-by-step explanation:

The mass of air stored in the vessel can be calculated using the ideal gas law:

PV = nRT

Where:

P = pressure (in atm)

V = volume (in m³)

n = number of moles of gas

R = universal gas constant

T = temperature (in Kelvin)

Therefore, the mass of air stored in the vessel can be calculated as follows:

m = nM

Where:

m = mass of air (in kg)

n = number of moles of gas

M = molar mass of air

Using the ideal gas law, we can calculate the number of moles of gas as follows:

n = PV/RT

Therefore, the mass of air stored in the vessel can be calculated as:

m = PV/RT * M

Plugging in the values given in the problem, we get:

m = (20 * 10^3) / (8.314 * 300) * 28.964

m = 1.206 * 10^4 kg


4. How many moles of gas would be present in a gas contained in a 1000.0-ml vessel at 25.00C at a pressure of 2.50 atm?


Answer:

0.102 moles of gas

Explanation:

Ideal Gas Law

The ideal gas law gives the relationship among the pressure, temperature, volume, and the number of moles of a gas. The ideal gas law formula is:

PV = nRT

where P - pressure, T - temperature, n - number of moles, V - volume, and

R - gas constant which is equivalent to 8.314 Pa [tex]m^{3}[/tex] / mol K in SI unit.

In this problem, the given are the volume of the gas of 1000 mL, the temperature of 25 Celcius, and the pressure of 2.50 atm. We need to know the moles of the given gas.

Step 1: Rearrange the ideal gas law in terms of the unknown variable n.

PV = nRT

Equation 1: n = [tex]\frac{PV}{RT}[/tex]

Step 2: Notice that the given values' units are not in the SI unit. Convert the given values in SI units to avoid unit deviation in the solution.

V = 1000 mL x [tex]\frac{1 Liter}{1000 mL}[/tex] x [tex]\frac{1 m^{2} }{1000 Liters}[/tex] = 1 x [tex]10^{-3}[/tex] [tex]m^{3}[/tex]

P = 2.50 atm x [tex]\frac{101325 Pa}{1 atm}[/tex] = 253312.5 Pa

T = 25 celcius + 273.15 = 298.15 K

Step 3: Substitute the resulting values in equation 1 and solve for the number of moles. Thus,

n = [tex]\frac{PV}{RT}[/tex]

n= [tex]\frac{253312.5 Pa x 1 x 10^{-3} m^{3} }{8.314 Pa m^{3}/ mol K x 298.15 K }[/tex]

n = 0.102 moles

Therefore, there are 0.102 moles of gas.

5. A vessel contains 5.0 ft3 of water at 40°F and atmospheric pressure. If the water is heated to 80°F what will be the percentage change in its volume


Answer:

Percentage change in volume will be -0.0741 %.

Explanation:

First we have to compute for the final volume using the Charles's Law equation which is [tex]\frac{V1}{T1}[/tex] = [tex]\frac{V2}{T2}[/tex].

Let V1 = initial volume and V2 = final volume

T1 = initial temperature then T2 = final temperature. We are interested in the final volume for us to be able to compute for the percentage change.

By rearranging the Charles's Law we will arrive to the final working equation which is V2 = [tex]\frac{(V1)(T2)}{(T1)}[/tex].

We must first convert the temperatures in Kelvin for us to arrive to a positive and correct answer. To do that, we subtract 32, multiply by [tex]\frac{5}{9}[/tex], and then 273.15, to both temperatures.

Substituting the values, V2 = [tex]\frac{(5 ft^3)(277.59 K)}{299.82 K} }[/tex] = 4.629 ft³

To compute for the percentage change in volume we will use the equation

% change = [tex]\frac{(V2-V1)}{V1}[/tex]

Substituting, % change = [tex]\frac{(4.6292 ft^3 - 5 ft^3)}{5 ft^3}[/tex] × 100% = -0.0741 %

The negative sign in the percentage change indicates that there has been a decrease in the volume, meaning the final volume is smaller than the initial volume.

23. A sample of 3.00 g of SO2(g) originally in a 5.00-L vessel at 21 °C is transferred to a 10.0-L vessel at 26 °C. A sample of 2.35 g of N2(g) originally in a 2.50-L vessel at 20 °C is transferred to this same 10.0-L vessel. (a) What is the partial pressure of SO2(g) in the larger container? (b) What is the partial pressure of N2(g) in this vessel? (c) What is the total pressure in the vessel?


Answer:

1) The partial pressure of SO₂ gas in the larger container = 0.115 atm.

2) The partial pressure of N₂ gas in the larger container = 0.206 atm.

3) The total pressure in the vessel = 0.321 atm.

Explanation:

To calculate the partial pressure of each gas, we can use the general law of ideal gas: PV = nRT.

where, P is the partial pressure of the gas in atm,

V is the volume of the vessel in L,

n is the no. of moles of the gas,

R is the general gas constant (R = 0.082 L.atm/mol.K),

T is the temperature of the gas in K.

1) What is the partial pressure of SO₂ gas in the larger container?

∵ P = nRT/V.

n = mass/molar mass = (3.0 g)/(64.066 g/mol) = 0.047 mol.

R = 0.082 L.atm/mol.K.

T = 26 °C + 273.15 = 299.15 K.

V = 10.0 L. (The volume of the new container)

∴ P = nRT/V = (0.047 mol)(0.082 L.atm/mol.K)(299.15 K)/(10.0 L) = 0.115 atm.

2) What is the partial pressure of N₂ gas in the larger container?

∵ P = nRT/V.

n = mass/molar mass = (2.35 g)/(28.0 g/mol) = 0.084 mol.

R = 0.082 L.atm/mol.K.

T = 26 °C + 273.15 = 299.15 K.

V = 10.0 L. (The volume of the new container)

∴ P = nRT/V = (0.084 mol)(0.082 L.atm/mol.K)(299.15 K)/(10.0 L) = 0.206 atm.

3) What is the total pressure in the vessel?

According to Dalton's law the total pressure exerted is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases.

∵ The total pressure in the vessel = the partial pressure of SO₂ + the partial pressure of N₂.

∴ The total pressure in the vessel = 0.115 + 0.206 = 0.321 atm


24. At a constant temperature a 564.2 cm of gas is contained in a vessel under a pressure of 600 mmHg. What would be the new volume of the gas if the pressure is changed to 850 mmHg.​

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_1 & = \text{600 mmHg} \\ V_1 & = \text{564.2 cm}^3 \\ P_2 & = \text{850 mmHg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

[tex]\begin{aligned} V_2 & = \frac{P_1V_1}{P_2} \\ & = \frac{(\text{600 mmHg})(\text{564.2 cm}^3)}{\text{850 mmHg}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{398.3 cm}^3} \end{aligned}[/tex]
