2. What component of culture involves its representations of reality?


A symbol is one of the five components of culture. It is anything that is used to represent something else. Members of a particular culture attach meanings to objects, images, gestures, or anything that is part of their reality, and the symbols of these realities can either be physical or non-physical.

Five Components of Culture Symbols Language Beliefs Norms Values

Symbols

Every culture has important symbols that it treasures. For example, Christians all over the world cherish the Cross as a symbol of the sacrifice of Jesus Christ. To many, a symbol is a form of nonverbal communication. To those who know what it means, the symbol does not need to be explained.

Language

Every culture has its own language, which is both written and spoken. Interestingly, even cultures that share the same language have differences in certain nuances.  

Beliefs

This component addresses the spiritual side of a culture. For example, a country may have largely Christian beliefs, but a smaller cultural group may have Muslim beliefs instead.

Norms

These are the rules, traditions, and mores that members of a culture observe as a group. They help keep order in a group.

Values

These reflect the standard for good and bad in a particular culture. They inform people on what is considered acceptable behavior in society.  

Examples of Symbols in Culture

Symbols can represent any component of culture, including values, norms, beliefs, and language. Here are some examples:

A gold ring as a symbol that someone is married The dove as a sign of peace The raven as symbol of death and bad omen Roses as a symbol of romantic love A police officer’s badge and uniform as symbols of authority  The swastika as the symbol of the Nazi party during World War II Black cat as a symbol of bad luck

25. DO you think the painting weeping woman is an accurate representation of reality


A painting is an art form, an expression of art, a visually creative expression of something. Sometimes inspired by reality, sometimes from pure imagination. Picasso's painting of the Weeping Woman was believed to be a continuation of his epic painting Guernica. But the Weeping Woman was no longer directly referring to the effects of the Spanish Civil War, but of the face of universal suffering. The model was said to be Dora Maar. Dora Mar was a professional photographer when Picasso met her and was believed to be Picasso's mistress for some years. For Picasso, who has painted Dora for number of guises, Dora was a weeping woman, and sees women as weeping machines.

The depiction of the suffering of the Weeping Woman, for me, was a creative representation of reality. These representations may not be absolutes, they can be somehow subjective and relative, but nevertheless, inspired by an experience or a form of reality.
