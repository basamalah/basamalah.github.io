Androcles and the Lion is one of George Bernard Shaw's shortest, simplest and most peculiar works. Described as a "fable play," it mixes childlike parable with Shavian dialectic. A.D. Players' staging captures a good deal of the play's peculiar charm — increasingly as the play progresses, and especially in its encounters of the man and beast.

Shaw built the play around the ancient tale of a wanderer who encounters a lion in the forest, extracts a thorn from the suffering creature's paw, and thus makes a friend for life. The two meet again later, when Androcles is tossed to the lions in the Colosseum, with results that astonish the Emperor, his soldiers and the mob.

As Shaw has fleshed out the human hero, Androcles is a gentle Greek tailor saddled with a nagging wife, Megaera. When first encountered, they have fled their home because of Androcles' Christianity and devotion to animals. After he and the lion meet and bond, they waltz off together, leaving Megaera in the wild.
