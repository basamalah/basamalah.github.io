Violence or the threat of violence against worker​

Workplace violence is violence or the threat of violence against workers. It can occur at or outside the workplace and can range from threats and verbal abuse to physical assaults and homicide, one of the leading causes of job-related deaths.

Workplace Violence Is Broken Down into 4 Categories

Type I—Criminal Intent. In this kind of violent incident, the perpetrator has no legitimate relationship to the business or its employees. Type I violence is usually incidental to another crime such as robbery, shoplifting, or trespassing. Acts of terrorism also fall into this category.

Type II—Customer/Client. When the violent person has a legitimate relationship with the business—for example, a customer, client, patient, student, or inmate—and becomes violent while being served by the business.

Type III—Worker on Worker. The perpetrator of Type III violence is an employee or past employee of the business who attacks or threatens other employee(s) or past employee(s) in the workplace.

Type IV—Personal Relationship. The perpetrator in these cases usually does not have a relationship with the business but has a relationship with the intended victim. This category includes victims of domestic violence who are assaulted or threatened while at work.

18. Speak out against structural violence​


Structural violence refers to a form of violence wherein social structures or social institutions harm people by preventing them from meeting their basic needs. Although less visible, it is by far the most lethal form of violence, through causing excess deaths—deaths that would not occur in more equal societies. Not only is it the deadliest violence, greater in scope and in implication than any other type of violence, it grows exponentially as unequal power differentials are used to create more unequal structures. Yet, because these limitations are embedded within social structures, people tend to overlook them as nothing more than the ordinary difficulties of life. Examples of structural violence include health, economic, gender, and racial disparities. Derivative forms include cultural, political, symbolic, and everyday violence. Structural violence is also the most potent stimulant of behavioral violence in the form of homicides, suicides, mass murders, and war. It is therefore one of the most critical areas of violence studies to examine in our time. Conceptualizing structural violence can help guide peace research through the consideration of conditions that might add positively to peace, rather than merely aid the cause of peace in the negative way of reducing violence and war.

Violence against women is a serious and widespread problem that affects women of all ages, races, and socioeconomic backgrounds. It can take many forms, including physical, sexual, and emotional abuse, and can have devastating consequences for the women who experience it, as well as their families and communities.

One of the most common forms of violence against women is domestic violence, which occurs when a partner or family member uses physical, sexual, or emotional abuse to control and dominate another person. This can include physical assault, sexual assault, psychological abuse, and other forms of coercion and control. Domestic violence can have long-lasting effects on the health and well-being of the women who experience it, and it is often difficult for them to escape these situations due to fear, shame, or economic dependence on their abusers.

Another form of violence against women is sexual assault, which is any unwanted sexual contact that occurs without the victim's consent. This can include rape, sexual harassment, and other forms of sexual violence. Sexual assault can have severe physical and emotional consequences for the victims, and can also lead to long-term psychological problems such as post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

The impacts of violence against women are far-reaching and can affect entire communities. It can lead to decreased productivity and economic growth, as well as increased healthcare costs and social services expenditures. It can also have a negative impact on the mental and physical health of the women who experience it, as well as their children and other family members.

It is important for individuals and communities to take a stand against violence against women, and to work towards creating a safer and more equal society. This can include supporting organizations and initiatives that work to prevent violence against women, advocating for stronger laws and policies to protect women, and speaking out against violence and discrimination against women. By taking action and working together, we can create a world where women are safe and respected, and can live their lives free from fear and violence.
