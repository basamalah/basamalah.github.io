From a philosophical perspective, the self is seen as a complex and subjective concept that is composed of different parts. Philosophers such as Descartes, Hume and Kant have argued that there is a fundamental essence of the self that is both unique and immutable. This essence is often seen as being composed of the mind and body, and is thought to be the basis of our sense of identity. Philosophers have also discussed the relationship between the self and the external world, arguing that our sense of self is shaped by our experiences and interactions with others. The self is also seen as being composed of our beliefs, values and desires, which can be seen as the source of our autonomy and free will. Finally, philosophical theories of the self address the concept of self-knowledge, arguing that we can only truly understand ourselves through introspection and reflection.

25. Discuss the different representations and conceptualizations of the self from various disciplinal perspectives.​


Different disciplines have varying ways of studying and conceptualizing the concept of the self. In psychology, the self is often seen as a person's sense of their own identity and the unique characteristics that make them who they are. This can include things like their physical, social, and psychological traits, as well as their values, beliefs, and goals. Sociologists, on the other hand, tend to focus on how the self is shaped by social interactions and the cultural context in which an individual lives. In philosophy, the self is often associated with ideas about personal identity and the nature of consciousness. Some philosophical theories of the self focus on the role of consciousness in defining the self, while others examine how the self is embedded in social and cultural systems. Overall, there are many different ways of understanding the self, and these different perspectives can provide valuable insights into this complex concept.
