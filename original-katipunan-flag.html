Mathematics' study of random events is known as probability, and there are four primary types of probability: axiomatic, classical, empirical, and subjective. Since probability is the same as a possibility, you could say that it is the likelihood that a specific event will occur.

These are the four practical laws of probability:Either it occurs or it doesn't. The probability of an event occurring when added to the likelihood that it won't occur is always 1.

P(A occurs) + P(A does not occur) = 1

Exclusivity. If A and B cannot occur simultaneously (in which case A and B are said to be mutually exclusive), then

P(either A or B occurs) = P(A occurs) + P(B occurs) (B happens)

Independence. If B is equally likely to occur regardless of whether or not A occurs (in which case we say that A and B are independent), then

P(either A or B occurs) = P(A occurs) * P (B happens)

Sub-Events. If B must occur anytime A occurs, then B must have a probability at least equal to that of A.

P(A occurs) <= P (B happens)
