Eliminate the arbitrary constant in the following equation

1. Cx^3 + y^3 = 6x − 42. 4x sin 2y = 3x ^2 + C

~Answers number 1:✏️

Multiply 1 and 3 to get 3.

[tex]3Cx^{A}+\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(y)\times 3=6x-4 [/tex]

Subtract start fraction, d, divided by, d, x, end fraction, left parenthesis, y, right parenthesis, times, 3 from both sides.

[tex]3Cx^{A}=6x-4-\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(y)\times 3 [/tex]

Multiply minus, 1 and 3 to get minus, 3.

[tex]3Cx^{A}=6x-4-3\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(y) [/tex]

The equation is in standard form.

[tex]3x^{A}C=6x-4 [/tex]

Divide both sides by 3, x, start superscript, A, end superscript.

[tex]\frac{3x^{A}C}{3x^{A}}=\frac{6x-4}{3x^{A}} [/tex]

Dividing by 3, x, start superscript, A, end superscript undoes the multiplication by 3, x, start superscript, A, end superscript.

[tex]C=\frac{6x-4}{3x^{A}} [/tex]

Divide 6, x, minus, 4 by 3, x, start superscript, A, end superscript.

[tex]C=\frac{2\left(3x-2\right)}{3x^{A}} [/tex]

~Solve for A:✏️

[tex]\left\{\begin{matrix}A=\log_{x}\left(-\frac{2\left(2-3x\right)}{3C}\right)\text{, }&\left(x<\frac{2}{3}\text{ and }C<0\text{ and }x>0\right)\text{ or }\left(x>\frac{2}{3}\text{ and }C>0\text{ and }x\neq 1\right)\\A\in \mathrm{R}\text{, }&\left(x=-1\text{ and }Denominator(A)\text{bmod}2=1\text{ and }Numerator(A)\text{bmod}2=1\text{ and }C=\frac{10}{3}\right)\text{ or }\left(C=\frac{2}{3}\text{ and }x=1\right)\text{ or }\left(x=\frac{2}{3}\text{ and }C=0\right)\end{matrix}\right.[/tex]

[tex]4\sin(2y)x=3x_{2}+c [/tex]

19. y=c1x^2 + c2e^2x , I need to eliminate those Arbitrary constants


Answer:

The equation is 3y' - 4xy' + 4xy - 2y - y" - xy" = 0

Step-by-step explanation:

To eliminate the arbitrary constants, derive the equation 2 times.

Deriving the equation

[tex]y=C_{1} x^2 +C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]          equation 1

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x +2C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]       equation 2

[tex]y"=2C_{1} +4C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]        equation 3

Solving the equation

1. Use equation 1, then substitute to equation 2

[tex]y=C_{1} x^2 +C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]

[tex]C_{2} e^{2x}=y-C_{1} x^2[/tex]

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x +2C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x +2(y-C_{1}x^2)[/tex]

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x +2y-2C_{1}x^2[/tex]

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x(1-x) +2y[/tex]          equation 4

2. Use equation 2, then substitute to equation 3

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x +2C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]

[tex]2C_{2} e^{2x}=y'-2C_{1} x[/tex]

[tex]y"=2C_{1} +4C_{2} e^{2x}[/tex]

[tex]y"=2C_{1} +2(y'-2C_{1} x)[/tex]

[tex]y"=2C_{1} +2y'-4C_{1} x[/tex]

[tex]y"=2C_{1}(1-2x) +2y'[/tex]          equation 5

3. Solve for [tex]C_{1}[/tex] using equation 5, then substitute to equation 4

[tex]y"=2C_{1}(1-2x) +2y'[/tex]

[tex]y"-2y'=2C_{1}(1-2x)[/tex]             divide both side by 2(1-2x)

[tex]C_{1} =\frac{y"-2y'}{2(1-2x)}[/tex]                            substitute to equation 4

[tex]y'=2C_{1} x(1-x) +2y[/tex]

[tex]y'=2\frac{y"-2y'}{2(1-2x)} x(1-x) +2y[/tex]

[tex]y'-2y=\frac{y"-2y'}{1-2x} x(1-x)[/tex]                            cross-multiply

[tex](y'-2y)(1-2x)=(y"-2y')(1-x)[/tex]        

Get the product of both sides of the equation

[tex]y'-2xy'-2y+4xy=y"-xy"-2y'+2xy'[/tex]

Simplify by combining like terms

3y' - 4xy' + 4xy - 2y - y" - xy" = 0

Therefore the final answer is 3y' - 4xy' + 4xy - 2y - y" - xy" = 0
