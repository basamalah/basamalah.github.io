1. LAMBINGAN BRIDGE ESSAY?​(kailangan kona po ngayon)


Answer:

Lambingan Bridge, located in the heart of Manila, is a historic landmark that holds a special place in the hearts of Filipinos. It is not just a structure that connects two places, but it is a symbol of the Filipino people's resilience and their ability to come together in times of need.

The bridge was built in the early 1900s and was named after the Tagalog word "lambing," which means "affection" or "endearment." It was a fitting name for a bridge that connected two of Manila's oldest and most historic districts, Quiapo and Santa Cruz.

Over the years, the bridge has witnessed many significant events in the country's history, including the Philippine Revolution, the Japanese occupation, and the People Power Revolution. It has also been a witness to the daily struggles of the Filipino people, as they make their way to work or school, or visit loved ones.

But perhaps the most remarkable thing about Lambingan Bridge is how it has become a symbol of hope and unity in times of calamity. In 2013, when Typhoon Yolanda devastated the Visayas region, Lambingan Bridge became a focal point for relief efforts. Donations poured in from all over the country, and volunteers came together to pack and distribute relief goods.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, the bridge once again became a symbol of unity. As the government imposed lockdown measures, many Filipinos found themselves stranded in Manila, unable to return to their hometowns. Lambingan Bridge became a gathering place for these stranded individuals, who found comfort in each other's company and shared experiences.

In many ways, Lambingan Bridge represents the spirit of the Filipino people. It is a reminder of the country's rich history and cultural heritage, as well as its ability to overcome adversity and come together in times of need. As such, it is not just a bridge, but a symbol of hope and unity, a testament to the strength and resilience of the Filipino people.

The cariñosa (Spanish pronunciation: [kaɾiˈɲosa], meaning loving or affectionate) is a Philippine dance of colonial-era origin from the Maria Clara suite of Philippine folk dances, where the fan or handkerchief plays an instrumental role as it places the couple in a romance scenario.

The cariñosa originated in Panay Island and was introduced by the Spaniards during their colonization of the Philippines. It is related to some of the Spanish dances like the bolero and the Mexican dance jarabe tapatio or the Mexican hat dance.
