1. how do coping capacity affect vulnerability​


Answer:

The capacity for coping with a natural hazard is generally inversely related to vulnerability – the higher the coping capacity, the lower the vulnerability of a system, region, community, or individual. In some cases, however, even strong coping capacities do not necessarily reduce vulnerability.

13. Strengthen and when necessary develop coordinated regional approaches, and create or upgrade regional policies, operational mechanisms, plans and communication systems to prepare for and ensure rapid and effective disaster response in situations that exceed national coping capacities. paki explain po (DRR)​


Answer:

This statement refers to the importance of developing coordinated regional approaches to disaster response. It recognizes that some disasters may be too large for a single country to handle on its own, and that a regional response may be necessary to effectively address the situation.

To strengthen disaster response, it is important to develop regional policies and operational mechanisms that can facilitate cooperation and coordination between different countries and organizations. This may involve establishing communication systems that allow for the timely exchange of information and resources, as well as developing joint plans and protocols for responding to disasters.

By working together on disaster response, regional partners can pool their resources and expertise to ensure a more rapid and effective response. This can include sharing equipment, personnel, and supplies, as well as coordinating search and rescue operations, medical care, and humanitarian assistance.

21. 29. In your own words, explain the formula for disaster. Cite an example of disasterand explain how we can reduce the impact of disaster using the factors in theformula.HAZARD + VULNERABILITY CAPACITY TO COPE= Disaster​


Answer:

Historically, dealing with disasters focused on emergency response, but towards the end of the 20th century it was increasingly recognised that disasters are not natural (even if the associated hazard is) and that it is only by reducing and managing conditions of hazard, exposure and vulnerability that we can prevent losses and alleviate the impacts of disasters. Since we cannot reduce the severity of natural hazards, the main opportunity for reducing risk lies in reducing vulnerability and exposure. Reducing these two components of risk requires identifying and reducing the underlying drivers of risk, which are particularly related to poor economic and urban development choices and practice, degradation of the environment, poverty and inequality and climate change, which create and exacerbate conditions of hazard, exposure and vulnerability. Addressing these underlying risk drivers will reduce disaster risk, lessen the impacts of climate change and, consequently, maintain the sustainability of development (UNISDR, 2015a).
