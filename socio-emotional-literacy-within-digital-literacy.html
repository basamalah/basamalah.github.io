11. · What is Media Literacy, Information Literacy and Digital Literacy pa help po​


Answer:

Media literacy encompasses the practices that allow people to access, critically evaluate, and create or manipulate media.

Information literacy is the ability to find, evaluate, organize, use, and communicate information in all its various formats, most notably in situations requiring decision making, problem solving, or the acquisition of knowledge.

Digital literacy refers to an individual's ability to find, evaluate, and clearly communicate information through typing and other media on various digital platforms. It is evaluated by an individual's grammar, composition, typing skills and ability to produce text, images, audio and designs using technology.

Answer:

Start with “information.” It can be unreliable, questionable, incomplete, outdated, unsubstantiated, distorted, irrelevant, and so on. It can also be reliable, fully researched, sufficient, substantial, “solid,” and so on.

Information comes to us through media, face-to-face, and our direct own experience. It’s always “filtered” through at least our own experiences and knowledge. If it comes through “media” — including digital media — it’s also been filtered by some agent who chose to share it.

“Literacy” means being aware of the filtering agents involved. They may have tried simply to ensure whatever degree of reliability is possible, but they may also have tried to bias your understanding. It’s up to you to gather the information necessary to be the final filter of that incoming info — to determine the quality of the information.

Digital literacy is part of media literacy.

Digital literacy is not just the ability to read information in digital media, but also the ability to search, identify, evaluate, and use the information obtained.

Digital literacy is, without a doubt, an ability that you may subconsciously use every day while surfing the internet and using technology.

26. use the venn diagram to compare and contrast media literacy, information literacy and digital literacy​


Three similarities and differences comparing and contrasting media literacy, information literacy, and technology literacy are discussed below in detail.

The three styles of education all aim to adapt students to the realities of modern life, which are present in all professional domains, which is where the parallels between them arise. The variances may be seen in the unique goals and interests that each of them has.

→ You may find the Venn diagram you're referring to below.

In the context of your three specific literacy courses, we are thinking:

Media Literacy – Aims to acclimate people to the many media forms utilized in modern culture by letting them understand how they work and what they can do. Knowledge about media, how it works, the approach it presents, and the knowledge that not everything is always objective or perfect. Many of these may contain misunderstandings and prejudices.

Technology literacy – Its goal is to provide users with the skills necessary to operate technical tools proficiently, comprehend their significance in modern society, and follow best practices for doing so. Refers to your understanding of technology, your proficiency with it, your ability to use it to your daily activities or job, etc. As in my case, I can typically guess the operation of the majority of gadgets without a handbook. My father can accomplish almost anything with a PowerPoint presentation, while my mother is adept with Excel spreadsheets.

Information Literacy – Its goal is to acclimate the user to the available informational tools, enabling them to identify reliable sources of information and comprehend how various forms of information work. Combine this with learning literacy knowing that it is the awareness/understanding of the information provided in any configuration, be it learning, video, audio, etc.
