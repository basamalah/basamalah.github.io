21. Why did Rizal's annotated the book of Antonio de Morga when he was in london?


Answer:

By publishing his annotated version of de Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas (Events of the Philippine Islands, originally published in 1609), Rizal's intent was not only to provide the Filipino people their early history, a pre-Spanish history, but to present to them their own authentic culture and identity.Sep 1, 1999

Answer:

The discovery of Morga’s Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas proved to be serendipitous to Rizal. He had been rethinking the idea of the Filipino not as a people who were equal to their Spanish colonizers but a people with a unique civilization that was destroyed by colonization. Rizal found the answer to his idea in Morga’s book and it was this work that he decided to reprint with his annotations.

26. When did rizal go to london and go back to philippines


RIZAL  WENT TO LONDON, ENGLAND IN MAY 1888 THEN WENT BACK TO THE PHILIPPINES IN JUNE 1892

Rizal's Travel and Stay in LondonIn May 1888, Rizal landed in England. Inside the British Museum, where he spent his days reading all the texts on Philippine history he could discover, Rizal spent his time copying Antonio de Morga's vast study of the Philippines, Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas, which he subsequently annotated for publishing "as a present to the Filipinos." His family, who were being persecuted by Spanish religious landlords, the Filipino nationalists in Spain, and his Austrian friend Ferdinand Blumentritt, with whom he intended to establish an organization of Philippine intellectuals, were among the many persons with whom he had communication.BRITISH MUSEUMRizal began writing on the illustrious past of the Filipinos by the middle of August 1888. On August 16, 1888, he signed up to be a reader at the British Museum. The Museum's books and manuscripts could only be studied with permission, which may be requested in writing. Those that were admitted were given tickets, which they had to show while entering the Reading Room.Rizal's acceptance into the British Museum has traditionally been attributed to Dr. Rost. This should not come as a surprise given Rost's impact and the friendship the two men shared.
