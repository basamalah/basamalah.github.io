5. If the amino acis sequence of the two organisms are similar, would their DNA be also similar?


If the amino acis sequence of the two organisms are similar, would their DNA be also similar?

Yes, If the amino acid sequences of two species are identical, the DNA sequences of the organisms would be similar as well. According to biology's core dogma, DNA holds the precursor to RNA, which then codes for proteins.

Amino acid Their DNA sequences can be identical, as shown by their sequences. The genetic material for animals is found in the DNA, which also contains information for amino acids. Since certain amino acids mimic DNA. Some amino acids are compared because they contain Leucine, stop codons, Phenylephrine, and genetic material. And if two organisms have the same amino acid chain, they would still be distinct. Each amino acid can be coded by three DNA bases (A/T/C/G), which is known as a codon. These codons each code for a different amino acid, but even the same amino acid may have a different DNA base sequence. Phenylalanine, for example, may have a codon of UUU or UUC. Protein synthesis is divided into two stages: transcription and translation. The two strands of DNA unwind during transcription. One of the strands acts as a basis for the formation of an mRNA strand. A codon is a group of three nucleotides on an mRNA. These codons will be crucial in the second stage of protein synthesis known as translation.  After being formed during transcription, mRNA migrates to the cytoplasm via a nuclear pore. During encoding, mRNA, ribosomes, rRNA, tRNA, and amino acids collaborate to create a protein strand. The mRNA and ribosomes bind until they enter the cytoplasm. Ribosomes function as scaffolds, matching the codons on mRNAs to the anticodons on tRNAs. Anticodons are groups of three nucleotides on a tRNA's base that are complementary to mRNA codons. Additional tRNA anticodons are paired with complementary mRNA codons as the ribosome passes down the mRNA during translation. Amino acids are found on top of tRNA. The amino acids that make up a protein are therefore grouped in the proper order. A peptide bond is formed when two amino acids are adjacent to one another. The polypeptide chain begins to expand until it reaches a stop codon.
