4. Discuss the significance of plant classification in pharmacy.


Plants are living organisms capable of making their own food. They also help us live by releasing oxygen (the gas we breathe needed for the body’s energy) and absorb carbon dioxide (the gas people and other manmade resources release that are needed for food production in plants). Plants play a vital part in human lives. The study of plants, botany, is an important step in improving it. Health has been one of the issues a human encounters. Ever since, plants are used in treating illnesses. A science combining the study of plants and medicine to create and reproduce natural drugs is called pharmaceutical botany. A person studying this field is called a pharmaceutical botanist. Plants possess different features and contain different chemicals that can be used in treatments and they classified according to those. Taxonomy is the scientific way of classifying organisms (including plants) basing its origins, physical characteristics, up to the molecular level. When plants are classified in those ways, it would be easy for the pharmacists and pharmaceutical botanists to look for alternatives that is usually found in the area and that can be used in treating illnesses since they possess similar characteristics and natural chemicals with the commonly used plants. Also, it is useful in determining and separating the medicinal plants that can be used in therapy and palatable medicine to those that are not. It will also help them in discovering plants that can be used as alternatives and conduct studies on it for confirmation and trademark so the usage will not have any issues. Hence, those are reasons why taxonomy is necessary in pharmacy.

Explanation:

A home health aide accompanies an older woman, Mrs. Appleby, to the pharmacy. The aide buys a lot of things that are clearly for herself and not for the client. When it comes time to pay, the aide asks Mrs. Appleby for her credit card. The aide becomes impatient and wrenches the card out of Mrs. Appleby’s hand. Mrs. Appleby says, “Ow, you hurt me.”

The aide asks the pharmacist which of the medications is the most expensive. The pharmacist answers: “This one” and picks up a bottle. The young woman says, “OK, we’ll leave that one behind.”

The pharmacist says: “This is her high blood pressure medication. It is very important that she takes this one every day.” The young woman says: “We still have some left from last month.” She spends $10 on client meds, but more than $50 on personal items for herself.

Mrs. Appleby says, “I want to go home” but the aide ignores her. She puts money down on the counter and leaves without the medication. Mrs. Appleby moves slowly and the aide roughly grabs her by the elbow to tow her along, saying “Off to the mall now.”

Discussion points:

Abuse by undermedication, indicated by the aide’s failure to obtain the needed blood pressure meds, might qualify as both physical abuse and neglect.

The aide’s rough treatment is suspicious for physical abuse.
