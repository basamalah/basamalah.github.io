The person in the group who can be least trusted.

After a hectic day at work, it was almost time for dinner when the cook finally left. He was obviously really hungry. He went to the closest restaurant where he knew for sure that he would have the tastiest food.

He settled onto a chair at a table and carefully studied the menu. It didn't take Alex long to place the order because he had already been there. The restaurant was highly recognized for its talented chef, who created dishes of the highest caliber.

A fantastic crew that could appease palates all around the world totally supported him. The chef started working his magic while Alex waited anxiously. It goes without saying that he did a fantastic job creating a masterpiece! It was time to serve the food.

The moment Alex was about to lose all remaining patience, the kitchen doors opened. A man in rags emerged from them, clutching a battered tray in his left hand that contained the characteristic dish. He approached Alex in the messiest way possible while carrying a dirty piece of cloth in his right hand. The dish and a set of utensils were then placed in front of Alex.

I don't think it matters what happened to Alex's hunger! Although the aforementioned passage is fictional, it is unquestionably true when it comes to business cases that are used to plan the best technological solutions.

The idea for a fresh change would have been fantastically developed by a functioning business unit. They may have had a variety of good ideas for why they wanted to make the change and how it would benefit their business operations. They shared Alex's desire for change.They quickly brought this need to the attention of a project team, who then contacts their IT counterpart for assistance. The "chefs" at IT are now in charge of working their special magic. The team comes up with an excellent solution for the business challenge, whether it be an internal application or a vendor product. After many rounds of deliberation, a viable estimate is produced.

The "dish," or solution, is prepared for serving. Here comes the group of individuals who consider themselves to be Business Case experts. They exist solely to encourage end users to develop an argument for why the change is necessary.

Present this after that with all relevant data such that It is possible to obtain authorization for prompt implementation. The "guy in rags" is revealed at this point. The Business Case won't make sense if it isn't supported by enough data and facts.

If a painstakingly crafted Business Case is not put in front of decision makers, a vital business change supported by a cutting-edge solution will all go down the drain.

Many times, this weakest connection can completely change someone's life. Depending on how strong the business case is, initiatives might be authorized or refused. It is possible to reinvent careers.

Professional pathways could be compelled to deviate. It all comes down to how much heart and soul someone is ready to invest in the process of turning a business case, which is a sales pitch, into a confirmed order.
