4. difference b/w distillation and fractional distillation


The difference between simple distillation and fractional distillations is:

Simple Distillation / Distillation
- used to separate liquids with boiling point gaps of atleast 50 degrees
- uses a simple apparatus with 2 flasks and a condenser
- process do not have to be repeated
- can be used to separate a solvent from a solute
- it is used to purify a sea water

Fractional Distillation
- used to separate liquids with closer boiling points
- uses a complex apparatus with a fractionating column
- process has to be repeated several times to get the pure components
- cannot be used to separate a solvent from a solute
- used in crude oil refining

10. Activity 2: Distillation Process Directions: Distillation is the process of separation of two liquids with different boiling points. Where is distillation used?


Answer:

Distillation is used to separate liquids from nonvolatile solids, as in the separation of alcoholic liquors from fermented materials, or in the separation of two or more liquids having different boiling points, as in the separation of gasoline, kerosene, and lubricating oil from crude oil.

Explanation:

Most methods of distillation used by industry and in laboratory research are variations of simple distillation. This basic operation requires the use of a still or retort in which a liquid is heated, a condenser to cool the vapour, and a receiver to collect the distillate. In heating a mixture of substances, the most volatile or the lowest boiling distills first, and the others subsequently or not at all. This simple apparatus is entirely satisfactory for the purification of a liquid containing nonvolatile material and is reasonably adequate for separating liquids of widely divergent boiling points. For laboratory use, the apparatus is commonly made of glass and connected with corks, rubber bungs, or ground-glass joints. For industrial applications, larger equipment of metal or ceramic is employed.
