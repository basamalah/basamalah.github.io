Answer:

(Answered by Knowledge to help and guide the questioners)

The poem “Still I Rise” by Maya Angelou is about resilience and self-empowerment in the face of adversity. Female strength is strongly highlighted in the poem, as the speaker is a female that refuses to be broken or oppressed by “the ties that binds” her. The poem portrays male characters as oppressive, but not in a way that alienates them. Instead, it is a criticism of the systems of oppression that have been in place for so long, that they have become "natural" values many people default to. Moreover, the poem presents the suppression of women in an almost matter-of-fact way, attributing it to a societal problem, with the focus more on the response to this oppression, which is to not give in and rise against it. All in all, the poem clearly aims to say that regardless of the gender balance in society and systems of oppression that may exist, the individual still has the power to choose to rise and shrug off any barriers placed by others.

14. What is Still i rise poem by maya angelou mainly about?​


Explanation:

Broadly speaking, the poem is an assertion of the dignity and resilience of marginalized people in the face of oppression. Because Angelou often wrote about blackness and black womanhood, "Still I Rise" can also be read more specifically as a critique of anti-black racism.

27. What is the imaginary found in the poem Still I Rise by Maya Angelou.


What is the imagery in the poem Still I Rise?  

In "Still I Rise," Maya Angelou uses gold mines and oil wells as symbols of wealth and confidence. She also uses natural imagery, including the sun, the moon, the tides, and the air, to symbolize the inevitability of her continued rise beyond the reach of oppression.

What is the tone and imagery of Still I Rise?

Maya Angelou uses a tone of self-confident assertiveness in her poem “Still I Rise.” Her air of angry confidence is peppered with sarcastic humor. She addresses the poem to her audience of oppressors by beginning with the word, “You.”

DOROTHY PARKER (1893-1967)

The poem, “Penelope”, explains the hardships that Penelope had to go through while her husband was away. Penelope was the wife of the great Odysseus, a skilled Greek soldier and remarkable tactician. Odysseus had to leave Penelope to go fight in the Trojan War. After his victory at war, Odysseus made his voyage at sea to return home to his family and his kingdom of Ithaca. While Odysseus was sailing the vast seas, Penelope also had a myriad of duties to fulfill at home, such as helping her fellow Ithacans with their problems, doing household chores, driving away inhospitable suitors, and being faithful and loyal to her husband, whom she had no idea was alive or dead.

In the first half of the poem, Dorothy Parker (the poet), using the point of view of Penelope, describes all about Odysseus’ journey home on his ship. Exaggerations and personifications were often used in these beginning lines to describe the peacefulness of the voyage; some examples were: “pathway of the sun”, “footsteps of the breeze”, “ride the silver seas”, and “cut the glittering wave”. All these uses of overemphasis of Odysseus’ trip make his journey seem unreal or fictitious. On the other hand, in the second half of the poem, Parker does not use any exaggeration; she sticks it to the cold, hard truth of all the tough responsibilities that Penelope had back at home. Throughout the whole poem, Parker uses a great deal of imagery to allow the reader to visualize each person’s actions. “Penelope” has a set meter and a rhyme scheme. The poem had two verses, each with a total of five lines. Each line has seven syllables and rhymed with another line. All of the lines in each verse follow this rule, except the last lines in both verses. The last two lines of the verses, “He shall cut the glittering wave” and “They will call him brave” are both important examples of the tone. Penelope’s tone is very tired and annoyed about how little recognition she gets for her hard work. There are almost no examples of how brave or intelligent Odysseus is in the poem; all it talks about is him sailing the seas by “cutting the glittering wave”, which doesn't seem that great a feat, but he will still get honor and “called brave” while Penelope barely gets any praises for her different kinds of jobs. Another justification of the tone is the sudden shift from talking about Odysseus, to Penelope, and back to Odysseus in the poem, making it seem that Odysseus is the more important than Penelope.

Explanation:

The poem alludes to the myth of Odysseus and the Odyssey. After the Greek victory in the Trojan War, the Greek soldiers were to return back to their homes and families. A trip home that should have only took about a few months, took 20 years, after Odysseus and his men run into some trouble with the gods and other immortals. These series of adventures were called the Odyssey. During these 20 years without Odysseus, Penelope was left all alone with their child, Telemachus. Not only did she have to run Ithaca alone, she also had to make sure no other man took Odysseus’ place on the throne or in her heart. She had to be loyal and extremely cunning to drive away the suitors who trashed and invaded the palace daily. One of Penelope’s brilliant ideas was to “snip her thread” and pretend to weave a burial shroud to stall the suitors until Odysseus came home. I think that Dorothy Parker chose to use the allusion to Odysseus and Penelope to show that not everyone gets the recognition that they deserve. Penelope and Odysseus loved each other, but not everything was perfect between them. “They will call him brave” means that people remember Odysseus’ amazing accomplishments and his Odyssey but not much about Penelope and what kind of heartbreak and struggles she went through back at home.
