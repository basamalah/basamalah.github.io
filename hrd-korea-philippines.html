6. why HRD PHILOSOPHY Says every person is unique?​


Answer:

Persons are thought to have a special kind of value, often called "dignity," which, according to Kant, makes them both infinitely valuable and irreplaceably valuable. The author aims to identify what makes a person a person in a way that can explain both aspects of dignity. She considers five definitions of "person": (1) an individual substance of a rational nature (Boethius), (2) a self-conscious being (Locke), (3) a being with the capacity to act for ends (Kant), (4) a being with the capacity to act for another (Kant), and (5) an incommunicably unique subject (Wojtyla). She argues that none is capable of grounding both aspects of dignity since they are incompatible kinds of value; it is impossible for the same thing to ground both. Human persons are infinitely valuable in virtue of shareable qualities of their nature, whereas they are irreplaceably valuable because of a nonqualitative feature of their personhood.

7. What are the main functions and activities of the HRD​


Explanation:

Human resource development is the integrated use of training, organization, and career development efforts to improve individual, group, and organizational effectiveness. HRD develops the key competencies that enable individuals in organizations to perform current and future jobs through planned learning activities.

These functions are:

•Human resource planning.

•Recruitment and selection.

•Performance management.

•Learning and development.

•Career planning.

•Function evaluation.

•Rewards.

•Industrial relations.

8. similarities of Philippines and Korea​


Similarities of Philippines and Korea​

Asian and Western cultures have affected both cultures. Korea and the Philippines culture, like most Asian countries, were attacked by Japan during WWII. Interaction with foreign culture, technology, and the innate instinct of self-preservation have all shaped Korean culture, as has Filipino culture. The Philippines and South Korea enjoy a strong relationship since the two countries have always been and continue to be close diplomatic and military allies. In terms of commerce, immigration, and tourism, they are also important economic partners. The political histories of South Korea and the Philippines are startlingly similar. First and foremost, both countries were colonized. Korea was a Japanese colony from 1910 to 1945, while the Philippines was a US colony from 1899 to 1946 and a Spanish colony or culture for over three centuries before that.
