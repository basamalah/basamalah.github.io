Collage is a visual art form, where pieces of other images are grouped together to create a new piece of art. Collage comes from the French word Coller, which means to glue. 13. How would you differentiate collage from decollage​


Collage is a 2-dimensional work of art by cutting and pasting artwork. Whereas decollage is more of a technique of tearing, removing, cutting out parts of the original image

FURTHER EXPLANATION:

COLLAGE:

Collage is a two-dimensional work of art, which uses various combinations of materials, which can be combined to produce works of art. Quoting Art Space, the word collage comes from the French word 'coller' which means 'gluing' or 'sticking'. Children always love activities that are dynamic and stimulate their motor skills a lot, such as the cutting and pasting activities that are included in learning collage making. Launching the book Teaching Methods for Children's Physical Development, collages can help develop children's fine motor skills with their hands and eyes. Because collages involve a variety of small pieces and a variety of colors. The materials used in making Collages can be in the form of natural materials, artificial materials, semi-finished materials, finished materials, or leftover materials.

DECOLLAGE:

Decollage (French: décollage meaning to tear, release; opposite of collage) is a technique of tearing, removing, cutting out parts of the original image. This technique is often associated with groups of artists who are members of the nouveau realism or new realism movement. This art movement creates works of art, one of which is by tearing posters attached to walls.

20. materials and procedure of decollage​


Answer:

Décollage, in art, is the opposite of collage; instead of an image being built up of all or parts of existing images, it is created by cutting, tearing away or otherwise removing, pieces of an original image.[1] The French word "décollage" translates into English literally as "take-off" or "to become unglued" or "to become unstuck". Examples of décollage include etrécissements and cut-up technique. A similar technique is the lacerated poster, a poster in which one has been placed over another or others, and the top poster or posters have been ripped, revealing to a greater or lesser degree the poster or posters underneath.

We talk a lot about it, but its scope and scope are hard to pinpoint, "Digital Art" defines the technological arts, with complex boundaries providing many potential meanings of terminology. The word itself has developed over time, and while computer art, multimedia art, and cyber art were standard in the 1960s-90s, the emergence of the World Wide Web has introduced a layer of connectivity that has led to a change in language. As such, we now use the terms digital art and new media that can be used interchangeably for some nuances.

Art historians also categorize digital art as twofold: object-oriented artwork and process-oriented visuals. In the first example, digital technologies are a means to an end and act as a medium for producing traditional objects, such as paintings, photos, prints and sculptures. In the second example, technology is the end itself, and artists explore the promise of the very essence of this modern medium. This latter category—often correlated with the word "new media"—refers to all computer art that is digitally created, processed and distributed. In other words, while some works rely on digital media to magnify the current medium, others use digital technology as an intrinsic and indissociable part in the creation of the artifact. With these meanings in mind, the following list presents current practices linked to the digital medium.

Computer-generated visual media

Digital visual art consists either of 2D visual information presented on an electronic visual display or of information mathematically converted into 3D information, viewed through perspective projection on an electronic visual display. The simplest is a 2D computer graphic that represents how you would draw a pencil and a piece of paper. In this scenario, though, the image is on the computer screen and the tool you are drawing with could be a tablet type or a mouse. What is created on your screen can appear to be drawn with a pencil, pen or paintbrush. The second type is 3D computer graphics, where the screen is turned into a simulated world, where the objects are arranged to be "photographed" by the computer. Usually, 2D computer graphics use raster graphics as their primary means of source data representation, while 3D computer graphics use vector graphics to construct interactive virtual reality installations. A potential third paradigm is to produce art in 2D or 3D entirely through the execution of algorithms encoded in computer programs.

Mixed Media The digital medium is important. In contrast to traditional development, computing requires elements of a different nature that can be combined and organized in order to create an entire experience for the audience. Artworks can thus combine still and moving images, augmented reality, music, photos, and so on. One medium of the artwork can also be defined, meaning that one digital creation can result in different physical outputs, depending on the common wishes and goals of the commissioners, artists and curators.
