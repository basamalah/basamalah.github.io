10. y= -(x+3)(x+1)² (2x-5) Polynomial function Activity 12 :it's your turn, show me grade 10 page 121


CONTEXT: Taken from Grade 10, Learner's Module, Unit 2, Activity 12: It's Your Turn, Show me

The question asks for the following:
a. leading term
b. end behaviors
c. x-intercepts
d. multiplicity of roots
e. y-intercept, point on the y-axis
f. number of turning points
g. sketch

[tex] y = -(x+3)(x+1)^2(2x-5)[/tex]

a. leading term: we can solve for the leading term by transforming the function to its polynomial form, and taking the term with the highest degree:

[tex] y = -(x+3)(x+1)(x+1)(2x-5)[/tex]
[tex] y = -(x+3)(x^2+2x+ 1)(2x-5)[/tex]
[tex] y = (-x-3)(x^2+2x+1)(2x-5)[/tex]
[tex] y = (-x^3-2x^2-x-3x^2-6x-3)(2x-5)[/tex]
[tex] y = (-x^3-5x^2-7x-3)(2x-5)[/tex]
[tex] y = (-2x^4-10x^3-14x^2-6x+15x^3+25x^2+35x+15)[/tex]
[tex] y = (-2x^4-5x^3+11x^2+29x+15)[/tex]

we can see here that the highest degree term is [tex]-2x^4[/tex]

b. end behavior: we'll go back to this after figuring out c, since that is necessary.

c. x-intercepts/points in the x-axis: these are the points in our x-axis where y will be zero so, when

[tex]0=(-x+3)(x+1)^2(2x-5)[/tex],

we have zeroes at x = -3, -1, and 5/2. Now we go back to b.


b. end behavior: the easiest way to discover this is to construct a sign table for the zeroes:


term                                             x < -3         -3<x<-1       -1<x<5/2       5/2<x
[tex]-(x+3)[/tex]                                           +                 -                    -               -         
[tex](x+1)^2[/tex]                                            +                 +                   +               +       
[tex](2x-5)[/tex]                                            -                  -                   -                +       
[tex]-(x+3)(x+1)^2(2x-5)[/tex]               -                 +                   +               -       

so what does this table tell us? when x is coming from negative infinity, f(x) also goes down to negative infinity without bound. When x is going to positive infinity f(x) goes to negative infinity without bound.

d. multiplicity of roots: these are basically the exponents of the factored term. So, [tex]-(x+3)[/tex] has a multiplicity of 1, [tex](x+1)^2[/tex] has a multiplicity of 2, and [tex](2x-5)[/tex] has a multiplicity of 1.

e. y-intercepts/points in the y-axis: these are y-values where x will be 0. What we can do is simply apply this to the equation:

[tex]y=-(x+3)(x+1)^2(2x-5)[/tex] 

If x will be zero at this y value, then we can simply replace all x's here with zero:

[tex]y=-(0+3)(0+1)^2(2(0)-5) = (-3)(1)(-5) = 15[/tex] 

therefore, we have a y-intercept at y = 15.
