16. What Species hominids


Answer:

Hominid Species

Introduction

The word "hominid" refers to members of the family of humans, Hominidae, which consists of all species on our side of the last common ancestor of humans and living apes. (Some scientists use a broader definition of Hominidae which includes the great apes.) Hominids are included in the superfamily of all apes, the Hominoidea, the members of which are called hominoids. Although the hominid fossil record is far from complete, and the evidence is often fragmentary, there is enough to give a good outline of the evolutionary history of humans.

The time of the split between humans and living apes used to be thought to have occurred 15 to 20 million years ago, or even up to 30 or 40 million years ago. Some apes occurring within that time period, such as Ramapithecus, used to be considered as hominids, and possible ancestors of humans. Later fossil finds indicated that Ramapithecus was more closely related to the orang-utan, and new biochemical evidence indicated that the last common ancestor of hominids and apes occurred between 5 and 10 million years ago, and probably in the lower end of that range (Lewin, 1987). Ramapithecus therefore is no longer considered a hominid.

The field of science which studies the human fossil record is known as paleoanthropology. It is the intersection of the disciplines of paleontology (the study of ancient lifeforms) and anthropology (the study of humans).

Hominid Species

The species here are listed roughly in order of appearance in the fossil record (note that this ordering is not meant to represent an evolutionary sequence), except that the robust australopithecines are kept together. Each name consists of a genus name (e.g. Australopithecus, Homo) which is always capitalized, and a species name (e.g. africanus, erectus) which is always in lower case. Within the text, genus names are often omitted for brevity.

23. 1. Identify and differentiate the characteristics of the following hominids: Homo erectus, Homo sapiens and Homo sapiens sapiens. Explain the changes they have in biological, cultural and socio-political processes. ​


Answer:

Homo erectus, Homo sapiens, and Homo sapiens are three important hominid species that played a significant role in human evolution. Here are the characteristics that differentiate them:

Homo erectus:

Lived approximately 1.8 million to 200,000 years agoHad a larger brain and a more modern body plan than earlier hominidsWalked upright on two legs (bipedalism)Used stone tools and may have been the first hominid to control fireWas widespread, with populations found throughout Africa and AsiaMay have been the first hominid to leave Africa and colonize other parts of the world

Homo sapiens:

Lived approximately 300,000 to 12,000 years agoHad a larger brain than Homo erectus and more advanced cultural and social practicesWalked upright on two legs (bipedalism)Had a more sophisticated tool-making ability, including the production of more complex stone tools and the use of bone and antlerExhibited evidence of language, art, and symbolismFormed social groups and engaged in cooperative activities such as hunting and gatheringWas able to adapt to a wide range of environments, including extreme cold and arid regions

Homo sapiens sapiens (modern humans):

Emerged approximately 300,000 years ago and are the only surviving species of the Homo genusHave a larger brain and more advanced cultural and social practices than earlier Homo sapiensWalk upright on two legs (bipedalism)Have highly sophisticated tool-making abilities, including the ability to make complex tools and use agriculture to produce foodExhibit language, art, symbolism, and abstract thinkingForm complex social structures, including family units, tribes, and nationsHave developed sophisticated cultural practices, including religion, music, and literature

The changes these hominids have undergone in biological, cultural, and socio-political processes are significant. Biological changes include an increase in brain size and the evolution of bipedalism. Cultural changes include the development of language, art, and symbolism, as well as the ability to produce complex tools and adapt to a wide range of environments. Socio-political changes include the formation of social groups, the development of complex social structures, and the emergence of nation-states.

Overall, these hominid species represent significant milestones in human evolution, with each contributing to the development of the modern human species.

Biological development of Hominids

Hominids are bipedal and have huge brains. They have a few skeletal adaptations to walking upright, such as bended vertebrae and measured femurs. Hominids are bipedal and have large brains. They have many skeletal adaptations to walking upright, such as bent vertebrae and pointed femurs. Hominids were omnivores and developed cooking, which helped in the reduction of their teeth and jaws.

At least a dozen human-like beings, known as hominids, have existed on Earth in the 8 million years or so since our earliest ancestors diverged from the apes. And the list continues to grow. The hominid family tree develops new branches as scientists find new fossils. However, fossils are often difficult to classify neatly as one animal or another. Specific hominids, like all other species, were special. And, over millions of years, much of the organisms changed; they evolved; and some diverged and became new species.

The Hominid Family Tree

At first sight, the relationships between species on the hominid family tree seem to be riddled with more questions than answers. The graphic above illustrates, with connecting lines, how some scientists believe 15 different species relate to one another over a 6 million-year time period. A few connections are obvious. For example, scientists agree that the three most recent hominid species (Homo heidelbergensis, Homo neanderthalensis, and modern humans, Homo sapiens) all originated from an earlier species called Homo erectus. Other relationships, though, are murkier.. Indeed, our understanding of human origins is lacking, and the quest for missing puzzle pieces continues. However, dismissing the question marks on the hominid family tree as holes in our understanding ignores the reality of evolution. Hominid animals evolved over hundreds of thousands of years, adapting to evolving environmental factors. As a result, since the current fossil record only provides a snapshot into these emerging animals, determining when a species "became" something else is very difficult, if not impossible. However, in order to try to comprehend human origins, scholars have had to study and categorize the fossils that they have. They are able to discover patterns within the organisms and have a deeper understanding of how they came to be in this manner. They have thus provided us with a look of our ancient history, which will certainly become clearer in the coming years.
