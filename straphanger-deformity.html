14. In your own words, describe the mechanisms by which semicrystalline polymers (a) elastically deform and (b) plastically deform, and (c) by which elastomers elastically deform.


Answer: Semicrystalline polymers have a mix of crystalline and amorphous regions in their structure, and they can experience both elastic and plastic deformation. Elastic deformation is reversible and occurs when the polymer stretches in response to a stress, but returns to its original shape when the stress is removed. This is caused by the movement of amorphous regions within the polymer structure. On the other hand, plastic deformation is irreversible and occurs when the polymer permanently deforms in response to a stress. This is caused by the breaking of chemical bonds between the polymer chains, which allows the chains to slide past each other and rearrange themselves. Elastomers, which are a type of polymer with a high level of elasticity, also experience elastic deformation, but this is caused by the movement of long polymer chains within the material. When an elastomer is subjected to a stress, the chains stretch and rearrange themselves, and when the stress is removed, the chains return to their original configuration, causing the material to return to its original shape.

23. what is porosity deformation​


Answer:

The influence of porosity on the deformation and fracture behavior of two alloys, powder-fabricated Ti and Ti-6Al-4V, with differing levels of matrix strain hardening has been examined both experimentally and analytically. A large strain elastoplastic finite element model based on a regular array of equal-sized spherical voids is used to predict bulk porosity effects; the analysis is in good agreement with the experimentally observed rates of void growth but underestimates the degradation of strength with increasing porosity. The effects of porosity on a local scale, especially as regards fracture, are examined by a model of a porous continuum which contains imperfections whose magnitude depends upon the maximum porosity path within the continuum. At critical values of strain these imperfections cause localization of plastic flow. The predicted values for the strains at localization are in good agreement with measured fracture strains. The analysis thus explicitly recognizes that a primary effect of pores on fracture is to localize deformation into narrow regions of high porosity (“imperfections”) which are present even in random distributions of pre-existing pores and which are the sites of macrofracture initiation.
