9. Designed for ultrathin section microtome, the specimen is examined under an electron microscope.


With ultramicrotomy, the specimen is studied with an electron microscope after being cut into ultrathin sections.

The microtome itself was created in 1770 by George Adams, Jr., and was improved upon by Scottish watchmaker Alexander Cummings. A sample was advanced by the hand-cranked machine so that it could be sliced with a metal blade.

The primary application of ultramicrotomy in transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is sample preparation. It enables nanometer-scale resolution visualization and analysis of the internal fine structures of materials. It quickly and thoroughly creates samples in ultrathin slices. The size and homogeneity of the electron-transparent region within the sections, as well as the speed with which the sections are created, are two major benefits of ultramicrotomy.

Biological specimens, commercial materials, such as polymers (rubber and plastics), and ductile, hard, or brittle materials (metals or ceramics) can all be studied using ultramicrotomy. Focused ion beam (FIB) milling, ion etching, tripod polishing, and electrochemical processing are additional methods for preparing thin sections of these samples, although ultramicrotomy has advantages over these methods in terms of speed and purity.
