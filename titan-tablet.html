17. A critique of titanic


Titanic is a classic love story in the form of a cinematic masterpiece. Released in 1997, the film was incredibly popular and spawned a new wave of romantic films. However, while Titanic is entertaining, dramatic, and visually impressive, it is also plagued with inaccuracies, melodrama, and a problematic power dynamic.

The story of Jack and Rose is a powerful narrative, and the acting performances from Leonardo DiCaprio and Kate Winslet make the characters' love believable. Unfortunately, the script is full of inaccuracies, from the way the passengers are portrayed to the actual Titanic’s sinking. Many of the conversations and behaviors between characters are also highly unrealistic and melodramatic.

In addition to factual inaccuracies, the power dynamic between Jack and Rose is cause for critique. While their romance is endearing, Rose is elevated to a position of higher status throughout the film by virtue of her class and gender. This reinforces a problematic notion of patriarchy, where Rose’s financial security depends on her romantic relationship with Jack, and her "promise" to never let go suggests her subordination to him.

Overall, though Titanic is an entertaining and visually impressive film, its inaccuracies and implications of sexism require close critique. While its love story may be an inspiration to many, its various flaws are heavily detracting.
