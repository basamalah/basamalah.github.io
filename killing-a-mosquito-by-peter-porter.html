The poem has 3 stanzas with 6 lines each. The rhyme scheme for the entire poem is ABABAB.

The writer, Peter Porter has a deep knowledge for the social history which explains what his poem is conveying. He was an Australian but was considered as a British poet. His works were characterized as sorrowful, concise, and clever. One of his favorite quotes is “Be subtle, various, ornamental, clever.” Which basically explains the form of the poem. It is written formally, following a rhyme scheme but the context is so uncanny in a smart way as it used some words as designs.

The recurring idea that this poem entails are judgement and death. With the mixture of tones of formality, disparaging, and irony.

There were tons of literary devices that Porter has used. Starting with the obvious, the allusion of one of Francis Bacon quotes, “Men fear death as children fear to go in the dark; and as that natural dear in the children is increased b tales, so is the other.” The rest are all symbolisms.

The poem began killing a “mosquito”, a very normal thing to do until the last line mentioned the ‘court’. Which gives us a hint that the place it was trying to convey is inside a court of judge. The poem goes on with the inequal judgement portrayed as if the judgement is like trying to kill a mosquito which says that we would never care about killing someone who bothers because it would only bring trouble or difficulty to scratch. He ended the quote with Bacon’s quote, that the pen is just as powerful to contain a blood and that it is satisfying to see the wrong judgement be tolerated like killing a mosquito. Just like how we feel after killing a mosquito, you are smiling because you are glad to finally got rid of those who bother regardless it was done correct or wrongly.
