3. What is RURAL HEALTH UNIT in the philippines?


A Rural Health Unit in the Philippine is usually the first line of medical facility available for many Filipinos in the provinces. In Manila and other highly-urbanized cities, the usual place where people seek medical help is thru the clinics or private hospitals. While clinics are also available in provinces, the cost usually deters patients from seeking help from them. The consultation fee is free and basic medicine is also free in rural health units. Ideally, every town should have a rural health unit.  

What happens inside a rural health unit?

Most rural health units have a doctor available for consultation. In some units, there are basic laboratories and x-ray setup but this is not the case in many other health units. In many instances, laboratory requests will be referred to a private diagnostic laboratory. Consultation with the doctor, however is free of charge. One disadvantage to the rural health unit is that there is usually just one doctor available and this is typically a general practitioner. This is good for basic medical problems but could pose problems for when specialized needs arise (such as when there are eye injuries, cancer patients, trauma, etc) that require the assistance of a specialist.  

What are the common reasons for visiting the rural health unit?

The most common is for people seeking care for an illness or an injury, medical check up, neonatal care, vaccination and maternal check up. As such rural health units also have nurses and midwives who are the primary faces of maternal care in the town or barangay. They are also the front-liners when it comes to advocating vaccines for the children and the general population.  

Are people satisfied with the rural health units?

Some surveys indicate that majority of Filipinos prefer receiving treatment from RHUs because of affordability while those seeking excellent services opt for private clinics or hospitals. Patients also have to endure long lines when seeking treatment from an RHU.

22. as the community health midwife, how are you going to prioritize the health concerns of the clients?​


Answer:

Good day.

The first part of a strategy forsolving a problem is identifying the problem. As a community health midwife, this entails that you first have to identify the needs of your community and the target population that you serve. One of the ways you could do this is to conduct a needs assessment in coordination with the local government unit. In the needs assessment survey, you will be able to ask the most pressing questions that you think when answered could better the services that you provide. This may also include questions on what the community thinks is the most urgent concern, their most recent medical encounters, among others. The benefit of this kind of data gathering is that you are able to see a real-time representation of the community's needs. In addition to this, specific concerns of clients that could not be read in numerical data could also be gathered. However, such practice may not be logistically possible since it consumes time and manpower. An alternative to this could be collecting and analyzing already existing data. This could be done by collating data from hospitals, clinics, and other institutions and analyzing the trend in numbers, areas of concern, frequency and demographics. After doing so, you will be able to discover as a midwife which age group are the usual expecting parents, the trend in the usual diseases and complications during gestation to birth, the months when most pregnant clients are expected to give labor, etc. From this, you will be able to specifically screen which concerns are of an urgent matter. For example: After collecting data from the clinic from the past 2 years, you observed that there is an increasing number of teenage mothers. There is also an alarming number of babies born with neural tube defects, a birth defect often caused by folic acid deficiency. There is also a distinct number of caesarian deliveries. This data tells you that the community needs a better folic acid information campaign to encourage pregnant clients to adopt a healthier and sufficient diet and lessen the risk of their babies having neural tube defects by adding more folic acid to their diets. The number of caesarian deliveries may be a result of the increasing teenage pregnancies. To address this you may think that family planning and safe sex education may curb these numbers.  The best approach to community health is through preventive health practices. By exploring the real problems of the community, your role as a health midwife goes beyond delivering babies in the clinic or advising clients on how to have a stress-free pregnancy journey.
