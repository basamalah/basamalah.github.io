17. 3. Message: What themes lie at the heart of Ezekiel’s prophetic message? 34:1-6 and 37:1-14. The prophet of Ezekiel.


CHAPTER 34:1-6

Ezekiel's prophetic message is focused on the theme of proper leadership. In chapter 34, he addresses the issue of poor leadership in Israel, criticizing the leaders for failing to care for and protect the people. He accuses them of being selfish and self-serving and calls on them to take responsibility for the well-being of the nation and to serve as true shepherds to their flock. Ezekiel presents himself as a model of good leadership, calling on the leaders to follow his example and to take their duties seriously.

CHAPTER  37:1-14

In chapter 37, Ezekiel presents the vision of the "valley of dry bones," which symbolizes the potential for Israel to be reborn and restored after a period of spiritual and political stagnation. The bones come together and are given new life, representing the rebirth of the nation and the renewal of its covenant with God. This vision serves as a reminder that even in the darkest times, there is hope for those who turn to God and seek his guidance.

SUMMARIZE

Overall, Ezekiel's message emphasizes the need for robust and responsible leadership and the potential for renewal and restoration for those who turn to God. He calls on the leaders of Israel to take their responsibilities seriously and to serve as true shepherds to the people. He also encourages the people to have faith in God's plan and to trust in his power to restore and renew their nation.

28. How did God describe Israel to Ezekiel?


Answer:

Ezekiel, also spelled Ezechiel, Hebrew Yeḥezqel, (flourished 6th century BC), prophet-priest of ancient Israel and the subject and in part the author of an Old Testament book that bears his name. Ezekiel’s early oracles (from c. 592) in Jerusalem were pronouncements of violence and destruction; his later statements addressed the hopes of the Israelites exiled in Babylon. The faith of Ezekiel in the ultimate establishment of a new covenant between God and the people of Israel has had a profound influence on the postexilic reconstruction and reorganization of Judaism.

Ezekiel’s ministry was conducted in Jerusalem and Babylon in the first three decades of the 6th century BC. For Ezekiel and his people, these years were bitter ones because the remnant of the Israelite domain, the little state of Judah, was eliminated by the rising Babylonian empire under Nebuchadrezzar (reigned 605–562 BC). Jerusalem surrendered in 597 BC. Israelite resistance was nevertheless renewed, and in 587–586, the city was destroyed after a lengthy siege. In both debacles, and indeed again in 582, large numbers from the best elements of the surviving population were forcibly deported to Babylonia.
