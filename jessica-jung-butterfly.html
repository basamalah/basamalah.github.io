19. 1. What is your understanding to the Individuation principle of Carl Jung?​


According to Jung, the process of individuation is a particular process or road that every individual follows to realize his or her true self, in which there is a harmonious union of all facets of the human personality. One of the cornerstones of Carl Gustav Jung's analytical psychology was the idea of the individuation process. According to the author, individuation is the process of differentiating, constituting, and particularizing one's essence so that the subject can come to terms with who he is and be able to form a personality. Self-realization, which is a natural and instinctive progression toward one's own adulthood, is another indicator of it.

Given that it requires the integration of diametrically opposed aspects, it is crucial to keep in mind that the process of individuation, both in the Jungian vision and in others, is very paradoxical. According to Jung, we are dealing with a process in which conflicts between various opponents occur in a person and are connected to conscious opposition and individuality-collectivity.

Stages of the individuation process according to Carl Jung

The first step to reaching the subconscious is to throw yourself aside.
When the conscience first suggests that consciousness is not the sum of existence, this marks the start of the individuation process. It starts to become aware of the existence of unconscious or covert impulses, desires, and mental contents.Meet the shadows
You must start judging without that shadow once you become aware that it exists, which includes our desires and irrational impulses. Even though some people think they don't look well socially, they have a lot of worth. Integrating the personality's own rejected components is the goal. It's about recognizing the shadow as a part of who we are, not about giving in to impulses—in fact, Jung views repression as something that helps consciousness emerge.Encounter with the anima/animus
This procedure entails merging oneself as well as the archetypes associated with one's sex as well as a portion of them that are often associated with the other sex and making a connection with him. That is, while a woman integrates the animus, or masculine archetype (associated with strength and vitality, reason, and wisdom), a person must do the same with the anima, or feminine archetype (which corresponds to qualities like sensitivity, affection, and emotional expressiveness). In order to make both the logos and the eros sexual archetypes mediate and serve as a source of inspiration and innovation, they must be integrated as a whole.Archetypes of light are incorporated
Once this is done, unknown and dark parts of our psyche start to come to light, which greatly increases our knowledge of ourselves and can give us a sense of narcissistic omnipotence that causes us to believe that we are more powerful than we actually are. But reality's impact helps us realize that we don't have superhuman skills that would qualify as "smoking," which brings back our sense of modesty. At this time, sages or magicians who gave the unknown meaning by examining and discovering their own existence arrived, symbolizing wisdom and inventiveness.Coincidentia oppositorum, the culmination of the individuation process.
At this point, all of the components that make up the mind have been fully incorporated into one another (conscious and unconscious, individual and collective, person and shadow, etc.). He is already alone, conscious of the various facets of himself, and able to distinguish and isolate himself from the outside world. The subject is a complete, unique creature that gradually gains increasing autonomy (even capable of forming its own ethical system).

26. How does Carl Jung interpret introversion and extraversion?​


Answer:

Jung (1923) described extroverts as preferring to engage with the outside world of objects, sensory perception, and action. Introverts he described as being more focused on the internal world of reflection, are thoughtful and insightful.

Answer:

"In Psychological Types, Jung described how extraverts engage with external stimuli (Jung, 1921). He believed that extraverts direct their energy outwards - towards other people - and gain energy from such encounters. Introverts, meanwhile, focus their energy inwards, towards more solitary, thoughtful activities."

Quoted from:

Waude, A. (2017, February 2). Extraversion And Introversion. Psychologist World. Retrieved October 20, 2021
