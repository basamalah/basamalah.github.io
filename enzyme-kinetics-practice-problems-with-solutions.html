21. What are the other practical applications of the kinetic molecular theory in real life?​.


Answer:

Modern refrigeration is a practical application of the kinetic molecular thoery. When freon gas is compressed, the walls moving inward (or piston) adds kinetic energy to the molecules which in turn causes the gas to increase in temperature. The warmed gas is allowed to pass through external cooling coils to dissipate the extra heat into the environment, then the gas expands again (walls moving away slow down molecules) to become cooler and absorb heat inside fridge then start the cycle again. In a cruder form, you could for instance cool a can a soda if the compressed gas above the liquid was a larger volume by just opening it, through it might require a thicker material for the can.

29. lysozyme is an enzyme that breaks bacteria cell wall. A solution containing 0.150g of this enzyme in 210 mL of solution has an osmotic pressure of 0.953 torr at 25 degree Celsius. what is the molar mass of Lysozyme? with solution


Answer:

the molar mass of lysozyme is approximately 1.14 × 10^4 g/mol

Explanation:

The osmotic pressure (π) of a solution can be calculated using the following formula:

π = MRT

where M is the molarity of the solution, R is the gas constant (0.08206 L·atm/mol·K), and T is the temperature in Kelvin.

First, we need to convert the volume of the solution from milliliters to liters:

210 mL = 0.210 L

Next, we need to calculate the molarity of the solution. We can use the formula:

M = n/V

where n is the number of moles of solute and V is the volume of the solution in liters.

To find the number of moles of lysozyme, we need to convert the mass of the enzyme to moles using its molar mass (Mm):

n = m/Mm

We can rearrange this formula to solve for the molar mass:

Mm = m/n

Substituting the given values into the formulas, we get:

π = MRT

0.953 torr = M × 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K × (25 + 273.15) K

M = 0.150 g / (1 mol/Avogadro's number) / 0.210 L

Mm = 0.150 g / (1 mol/Avogadro's number) / 0.210 L / (0.953 torr / 760 torr/atm)

Simplifying and converting units, we get:

M = 1.03 × 10^-3 M

Mm = 1.14 × 10^4 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of lysozyme is approximately 1.14 × 10^4 g/mol.

      = 1/2( 1125 kg·m²/s² )
