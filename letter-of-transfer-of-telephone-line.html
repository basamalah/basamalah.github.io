12. what is the probability that at least one telephone line is busy in a day​


Answer:

To calculate the probability that at least one telephone line is busy in a day, we need to know the total number of telephone lines and the probability of a single line being busy.

Assuming that the probability of any single telephone line being busy at any given moment is p, the probability that a single telephone line is not busy is (1-p).

If there are n telephone lines, the probability that none of them are busy at any given moment is (1-p)^n. Therefore, the probability that at least one telephone line is busy at any given moment is:

P(at least one line is busy) = 1 - P(no line is busy)

= 1 - (1-p)^n

To calculate the probability that at least one telephone line is busy in a day, we need to know how many times a call is made to each telephone line in a day. If we assume that each line receives an equal number of calls, we can use the Poisson distribution to model the number of calls to each line in a day.

Let λ be the average number of calls per line per day, then the probability of a line being busy at any given moment is:

p = 1 - e^(-λ)

Substituting this into the formula above, we get:

P(at least one line is busy) = 1 - (1 - e^(-λ))^n

The specific value of the probability depends on the number of telephone lines and the average number of calls per line per day

16. what is the probability that at least three telephone lines busy in a day​


Answer:

To calculate the probability that at least three telephone lines are busy in a day, we need to know two pieces of information: the total number of telephone lines and the probability that any one line is busy at a given time.

Let's assume that there are 10 telephone lines in total, and the probability that any one line is busy at a given time is 0.3 (or 30%). We can use the binomial distribution formula to calculate the probability of at least three lines being busy in a day:

P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - P(X < 3)

where X is the number of telephone lines that are busy in a day.

Using the binomial distribution formula, we can calculate the probability of X < 3 as follows:

P(X < 3) = C(10,0) * (0.3)^0 * (0.7)^10 + C(10,1) * (0.3)^1 * (0.7)^9 + C(10,2) * (0.3)^2 * (0.7)^8

where C(n,r) is the binomial coefficient, which represents the number of ways to choose r items from a set of n items. For example, C(10,2) represents the number of ways to choose 2 telephone lines out of 10.

Evaluating the above equation, we get:

P(X < 3) = 0.028

Therefore, the probability of at least three telephone lines being busy in a day is:

P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - P(X < 3)

P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - 0.028

P(X ≥ 3) = 0.972

So, the probability that at least three telephone lines are busy in a day is 0.972 or 97.2%.
