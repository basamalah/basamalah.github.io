In a democratic system with an independent judiciary, the President of the Republic does not have the authority to influence the decisions of the courts. The principle of the separation of powers dictates that the executive, legislative, and judicial branches of government are separate and independent of each other. Each branch has its own powers and responsibilities, and there are checks and balances in place to prevent any one branch from becoming too powerful.

In particular, the Supreme Court, as the highest court in the land, is expected to be impartial and independent in its decision-making. The Chief Justice, as the head of the Supreme Court, has a duty to uphold the Constitution and the laws of the land, and to ensure that the Court operates independently and without any undue influence from outside sources, including the President.

However, it is important to note that the President does have some influence over the judiciary in the appointment of judges and justices. The President has the power to nominate individuals for appointment to the judiciary, subject to confirmation by the appropriate body. The President's choice of nominees can have an impact on the composition and direction of the judiciary.

Overall, while the President does not have direct authority to influence the decisions of the courts, their actions can indirectly affect the judiciary. It is important for the President to respect the independence of the judiciary and ensure that their actions do not undermine the integrity of the court system. Similarly, the Chief Justice has a responsibility to uphold the independence and impartiality of the Supreme Court, and to ensure that its decisions are based on the law and not on outside influence.

12. .What is the maximum rendition of decision for Supreme Court?


Answer:

Every Court opinion sets precedent for the future. The Supreme Court’s decisions are not always unanimous, however; the published majority opinion, or explanation of the justices’ decision, is the one with which a majority of the nine justices agree. It can represent a vote as narrow as five in favor to four against. A tied vote is rare but can occur at a time of vacancy, absence, or abstention from a case, perhaps where there is a conflict of interest. In the event of a tied vote, the decision of the lower court stands.

Most typically, though, the Court will put forward a majority opinion. If he or she is in the majority, the chief justice decides who will write the opinion. If not, then the most senior justice ruling with the majority chooses the writer. Likewise, the most senior justice in the dissenting group can assign a member of that group to write the dissenting opinion; however, any justice who disagrees with the majority may write a separate dissenting opinion. If a justice agrees with the outcome of the case but not with the majority’s reasoning in it, that justice may write a concurring opinion.

Court decisions are released at different times throughout the Court’s term, but all opinions are announced publicly before the Court adjourns for the summer. Some of the most controversial and hotly debated rulings are released near or on the last day of the term and thus are avidly anticipated.

13. do you think supreme court have made good decisions in the past?​


Explanation:

The Supreme Court doesn't think that an earlier decision is always correct.

But their first assumption will be that it is. And usually, they're right: most Supreme Court decisions are correct. But not all of them.

Sometimes, rulings change because the relevant law itself will have changed in the meantime, so that the precedent set for one law goes away (because the new law supersedes it).

Usually, though, rulings change based on differences in how the justices consider the laws to apply, which lies in how the judges interpret the facts of the matter.

For instance, in Hammer v Dagenhart (1918), the Supreme Court found that a federal law banning child labor (the Owen-Keating Act) violated the Commerce Clause of the US Constitution, which grants the federal government the power to regulate interstate commerce, but does not grant it power over intra-state commerce.[1] As a question of law, that matter is very clear.

The question before the court, then, was factual in nature: Does child labor constitute interstate commerce (regulable) or intra-state commerce (non-regulable)? The court majority ruled that a labor contract in a factory was intra-state commerce, making the law unconstitutional. Even though the product was sold by interstate commerce, the labor itself was intra-state. The Court also pointed out that while exceptions had long been made for federal regulation of immoral goods (drugs, liquor, gambling, porn), cotton was not an immoral good, so no exception could be made.

The way that the system works, the law was still in effect, but it was basically toothless, because it could not be enforced in a court.

The court's dissent argued that because the goods made by the children (in this case, cotton) were sold in other states, that it became interstate commerce, and could be regulated by the federal government. It also pointed out that even if the goods themselves weren't immoral, the production involving children was, so why wouldn't it be granted an exception and be subject to regulation?

The Supreme Court respected that decision for about 25 years. In US v Darby Lumber Co (1941), the Court found that a federal minimum wage law (part of the Fair Labor Standards Act) did not violate the Commerce Clause. This time, the decision was unanimous[2]: The argument that local manufacturing is only intra-state commerce because the goods might not leave the state doesn't fool anyone. If the business ships its product to other states, it is involved in interstate commerce, and the labor used to make the product is part of that involvement, and hence regulable. Moreover, the point of the law in question was to make sure that states couldn't affect interstate commerce by scuttling basic protections and rights for workers to lure business.

In any case, the Darby decision set up the notion that the federal government can regulate labor almost anywhere, and that precedent has held, and was extended to other regulations, like child labor. The decision (and thus the FLSA, still in effect) has held in large part because later judges and justices have not found any reason not to follow it.

The Supreme Court (Filipino: Kataas-taasang Hukuman; colloquially referred to as the Korte Suprema) is the highest court in the Philippines. The Supreme Court was established by the Second Philippine Commission on June 11, 1901 through the enactment of its Act No. 136, an Act which abolished the Real Audiencia de Manila, the predecessor of the Supreme Court.

The powers of the Supreme Court are defined in Article VIII of the 1987 Constitution. These functions may be generally divided into two—judicial functions and administrative functions. The administrative functions of the Court pertain to the supervision and control over the Philippine judiciary and its employees, as well as over members of the Philippine bar. Pursuant to these functions, the Court is empowered to order a change of venue of trial in order to avoid a miscarriage of justice and to appoint all officials and employees of the judiciary. The Court is further authorized to promulgate the rules for admission to the practice of law, for legal assistance to the underprivileged, and the procedural rules to be observed in all courts.
