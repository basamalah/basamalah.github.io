3. analysis of pinocchio​


Answer:

Pinocchio is a classic children's story written by Carlo Collodi in 1883. It tells the story of a wooden puppet who comes to life and must learn to be brave, truthful, and unselfish in order to become a real boy. The story has been adapted into numerous films, plays, and other media over the years.

The main theme of Pinocchio is that it is important for children to learn how to be responsible and honest. This theme is explored through the character of Pinocchio himself as he learns lessons about honesty, responsibility, and selflessness throughout his journey. The story also emphasizes the importance of family relationships as Pinocchio's father Geppetto shows unconditional love for his son despite his mischievous behavior.

The moral lessons taught in Pinocchio are still relevant today as they emphasize values such as honesty, responsibility, selflessness, and family relationships which are all important aspects of growing up. The timelessness of these themes makes this classic tale an enduring favorite among both adults and children alike.

27. peter pan and pinocchio differences​

Peter Pan and Pinocchio are two well-known characters from children's literature, but there are some key differences between them.

One of the main differences is the setting of the stories. Peter Pan is set in the fantasy world of Neverland, while Pinocchio takes place in the real world of Italy.

Another difference is the nature of the characters themselves. Peter Pan is a mischievous boy who never grows up, while Pinocchio is a wooden puppet who becomes a real boy through his own actions and choices.

In terms of the plots of their stories, Peter Pan is focused on adventures and battles with pirates, while Pinocchio is a tale of self-discovery and moral growth.

Additionally, the themes and messages of the stories are also different. Peter Pan is a whimsical story about the power of imagination and the value of friendship, while Pinocchio is a cautionary tale about the importance of being honest and responsible.

Overall, while Peter Pan and Pinocchio are both beloved characters from children's literature, their stories and themes are quite different from one another.

29. critique paper of pinocchio​


Answer:

Like the titular puppet at its center, “Pinocchio” lingers in an existential purgatory. The latest live-action remake of an animated Disney classic occupies an uncomfortable creative middle ground between remaining true to its beloved roots while also aiming to be fresh for modern audiences. Familiar lines share space with snarky one-liners. It’s not just a block of wood, but it’s not a real boy, either.

Robert Zemeckis’ high-tech reinvention is faithful to the Italian children’s novel source material and the 1940 original film, but it also features new songs and cheeky pop-culture references. (Most of them clang and feel forced, but one is admittedly laugh-out-loud funny). So yes, you get “When You Wish Upon a Star” (which powerhouse Cynthia Erivo now sings as the Blue Fairy), but you also get little meta bits about education, parenting, and the perils of fame. The result is a muddled mixture, offering some moments of exuberance and humor without ever being singular or exceptional.

It certainly makes sense that Zemeckis would want to take on this massive project, though, as director and co-writer with Chris Weitz (“About a Boy”). Just as the original “Pinocchio” was groundbreaking in its artistic complexity, Zemeckis has always pushed the possibilities of animation and visual effects, from the dazzling hybrid of “Who Framed Roger Rabbit?” to his pioneering use of motion capture in “The Polar Express.” (And, thankfully, technology has evolved since 2004 to keep human characters from looking so terrifyingly rubbery.)
