19. What form of footnote do you use when you cited a different page number but in the same source material from the first reference? Select one: a. Loc. sit b. Ibid c. Infra d. Supra


Answer:

B. ibid

Explanation:

The answer is ibid., an abbreviation for the Latin phrase ibidem. This phrase means "in the same place" and "in that very place". When you cited a different page number but you are still citing from the same material as the previous one, you can simply use "ibid." and then the appropriate page number, if it is different.

Below is an example of a different page number but in the same source material from the first reference:

T. Marcus and H.M, Burke. The Psychology of Being. Psychological Times, 95, 129-180, 2005, p. 131ibid., p. 152

Below is an example of the same page being cited from the same source material from the first reference:

T. Marcus and H.M, Burke. The Psychology of Being. Psychological Times, 95, 129-180, 2005, p. 131ibid.ibid.

The abbreviation loc. cit. is short for loco citato, which translates as “in the place cited. It can refer to the same work/book/journal and page number as the one previously cited but this style is outdated and not used anymore.

Supra means "above" and is imprecise. The same goes for infra which means below. These abbreviations mean they were or will be discussed in detail somewhere else.

25. It is a footnote applied when the same source material has been discussed or stated after the page. Select one: a. supra b. loc. sit c. ibid d. infra Answer is:C. Ibid


Ibid

             Originally from the word "ibidem", meaning "in the same spot," usually used in the endnote, footnote, bibliography, or school reference to refer to the source cited in the previous note or list item. This is identical to īdem, literally meaning "the same," which is widely used in legal citations.

             To find the source of the ibid., it is necessary to look at the reference before it. It might be another chapter of a book that has just been listed, for instance. The ibid abbreviation. Often refers to the reference or origin immediately preceding this.

               Example of Ibid:

Maria Magdalena Delos Santos, “The Language of Love,” School Write Ups and How-tos 65, no. 8 (2001): 609. Delos Santos, 537. Ibid. Ibid., 123.
