9. what cause do the chemotherapy works for patient controlled of antiemetic?​


Answer:

Chemotherapy induced nausea and vomiting (CINV) is a common and extremely unpleasant side effect for children receiving chemotherapy. CINV can lead to complications of treatment and also cause significant emotional and physical distress, disruptions to activities of daily living and influence the quality of life of the patient. The goal of antiemetic therapy is to prevent vomiting and minimise nausea both during and after the administration of chemotherapy. The severity of nausea and vomiting can, to some degree, be predicted by the chemotherapeutic agents being delivered but there is a degree of variation between patients. Antiemetic treatments should be initiated prior to the first dose of chemotherapy for best control of nausea and vomiting, as it can often become difficult to control nausea once the child is actually vomiting. Non-pharmacological measures are also an important consideration and should be implemented in conjunction with pharmacological regimes to allow for the effective management of CINV.

23. True or False: Chemotherapy and radiation only affect cancerous cells.


Chemotherapy and radiation only affect cancerous cells?

False. Chemotherapy medications act not just on cancer cells, but also on normal cells, which explains many of the adverse effects frequently linked with the drugs, such as hair loss, nausea, and increased susceptibility to infection. Chemotherapy has a disproportionate effect on cells whose growth-control systems have failed, such as cancer cells, therefore normal cells are better able to recover. As a result, normal cells are usually able to tolerate treatments that kill cancer cells. Chemotherapy combinations of two or more drugs are a regular aspect of cancer treatment. Agents that work in complementary ways—that target separate deficiencies in cancer cells—and that, for the most part, do not have overlapping side effects are used in these combinations. Doctors can boost the treatment's potency while keeping adverse effects to a minimum in this way.

what is the rationale behind the giving of allopurinol as adjunct to chemotherapy?

Allopurinol can be given at the start of a course of chemotherapy. When chemotherapy medicines start to kill the cancer cells, uric acid is released from these cells which can crystallise causing damage to the kidneys. Allopurinol stops these crystals from forming.

Allopurinol is used to treat gout, high levels of uric acid in the body caused by certain cancer medications, and kidney stones. Allopurinol is in a class of medications called xanthine oxidase inhibitors. It works by reducing the production of uric acid in the body.

Allopurinol works by reducing the amount of uric acid made by body cells. In gout, this helps prevent uric acid crystals building up in the joints.
