4. tossing a coin is an example of a statistical experiment. what is the probability that a head will appear when a coin is tossed?​


Answer:

The probability of getting heads on the toss of a coin is 0.5.

Step-by-step explanation:

Let H denote the event that head appears.

Let T denote the event that tail appears.

Let C denote the event that the coin is tossed.

Then C=H∪T and the events H and T are mutually exclusive. Moreover it is for certain that event C occurs and this together leads to:

P(H)+P(T)=P(C)=1

If the coin is fair then also:

P(H)=P(T) so that we can conclude that:

P(H)=P(T)= 1/2 or 0.5.

10. "If the coin tossed 7 times, what is the probability that the coin will land heads on the first and last toss?"​


When a fair coin is tossed the probability of getting head is 1/2

Here, total number of times tossing a coin = 7

Yo get probability that the head appears even no. of times, we have 0 or 2 or 4 or 6 as choices.

So, we add all probabilities in order meet the requirement.

Probability of success= 1/2 (p)

Probability of failure = 1/2 (q)

By binomial distribution,

P (X=r) = nCr * p^r * q^(n-r)

P (X=0) = 7C0 * (1/2)^0 * (1/2)^7 = (1/2)^7

P (X=2) =7C2 * (1/2)^2 * (1/2)^5 = 21*(1/2)^7

P (X=4) = 7C4 * (1/2)^4 * (1/2)^3 = 35*(1/2)^7

P (X=6) = 7C6 * (1/2)^6 (1/2)^1 = 7*(1/2)^7

Total probability = (1 + 7 + 21 +35)*(1/2)^7

= (64)*(1/2)^7

25. A die is tossed once. Find the probability of tossing a number greater than 4. with solution thx.​


Given:---We all know that a die has 6 faces numbered by 1-6. So the sample space is 6 denoted as S={1,2,3,4,5,6}. Therefore S=6

--- There are 2 numbers greater than 4,these are 5 and 6. So, the event that is represented by (E) is E={5,6} . Therefore E=2

Formula:Probability = n(E)/n(S) or P=n(E)/n(S) Where E is the number of possible event and S is the sample space.

Solution:P=n(E)/n(s) =2/6 =1/3NOTE: ALL ANSWERS MUST BE IN ITS LOWEST TERM.

Conclusion:Therefore I conclude that the probability of tossing a die and gets the number greater than 4 is 1/3.
