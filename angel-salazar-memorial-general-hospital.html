     Dr. Jose N. Rodriguez Memorial Hospital (DJNRMH), formerly known as Tala Leprosarium, was founded in 1940 to provide care for Hansen's Disease patients throughout the entire Luzon region. When there was a high success rate of treatment of the initial Hansen patients from research and the progress of methods done within the current medical practice, the hospital started treating general medical cases (non-Disease Hansen's patients) in 1970. The hospital then contemplated admitting general cases due to the sharp decline in Hansen patients. Currently, the hospital serves as both the primary training and research facility for leprosy care and management in the Philippines as well as the primary referral hospital for leprosy patients. It also meets the demands of the locals of Tala and the surrounding areas in terms of public health.

    Dr Jose N Rodriguez Memorial Hospital hopes to develop into a Tertiary General Hospital and a Center for Leprosy Patients' Care. The Philippine Congress is now debating House Bill No. 1395, which would convert 200 of the 2,000 beds at DJNRMH to tertiary general healthcare. DJNRMH's 2,000 beds are now being used for custodial care (1,800 beds) and general care for non-leprosy clients while waiting for this bill to be approved (200 beds). Other services offered by the hospital include radiology, occupational therapy, physical therapy, laboratory testing, and medical-social support for the right patients.

23. give the five generations of computer and the corresponding device that they used in circuitry and memory​


Answer:

1940 – 1956: First Generation – Vacuum Tubes

These early computers used vacuum tubes as circuitry and magnetic drums for memory.

1956 – 1963: Second Generation – Transistors

Transistor-driven machines were the first computers to store instructions into their memories – moving from magnetic drum to magnetic core ‘technology’.

1964 – 1971: Third Generation – Integrated Circuits

These were the first computers where users interacted using keyboards and monitors which interfaced with an operating system, a significant leap up from the punch cards and printouts. This enabled these machines to run several applications at once using a central program which functioned to monitor memory.

1972 – 2010: Fourth Generation – Microprocessors

This revolution can be summed in one word: Intel. The chip-maker developed the Intel 4004 chip in 1971, which positioned all computer components (CPU, memory, input/output controls) onto a single chip. The Intel chip housed thousands of integrated circuits.

2010- : Fifth Generation – Artificial Intelligence

AI is a reality made possible by using parallel processing and superconductors. Leaning to the future, computers will be radically transformed again by quantum computation, molecular and nano technology.

The essence of fifth generation will be using these technologies to ultimately create machines which can process and respond to natural language, and have capability to learn and organise themselves.
